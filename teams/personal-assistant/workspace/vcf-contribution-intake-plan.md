## Triage: what to say based on what they have

1. **Paper (schematics, manuals, listings, photos)**
   - First choice: museum wants the originals (archival value). Offer to arrange pickup/shipping.
   - If they want to keep originals: ask for high-res scans (300+ dpi). Even phone photos beat nothing.

2. **Digital (source code, docs, disk images, tapes)**
   - Ask what media it's on. Old media (9-track tape, 8" floppy, QIC) → museum or VCF has readers; do NOT let them try to "clean it up" or reformat.
   - Get a copy as-is. Bit-for-bit images preferred over "I'll send you the files."

3. **Hardware (boards, machines, peripherals, parts)**
   - Highest bar — space, condition, shipping. Loop the museum in BEFORE accepting; don't promise.
   - Get photos + model/part numbers first.

4. **Stories / contacts ("I don't have anything but I remember…")**
   - Still gold. Capture the story (offer a short call, record it). Ask who else they'd tag.

## Standard reply templates

**Quick acknowledgment (use immediately):**
> Thank you so much — this is exactly the kind of thing I was hoping for. Let me confirm the best way to get it to the museum so it's preserved properly, and I'll come right back to you. Really appreciate you reaching out.

**Arranging handoff:**
> The Vintage Computer Federation museum at InfoAge would love to give this a home. Easiest options are [shipping to the museum / I can arrange pickup if you're near central NJ / high-res scans if you'd rather keep the originals]. What works best for you?

**Hardware (manage expectations):**
> This is great. Hardware takes a little coordination on the museum's side (space and handling), so let me check with them before we set anything up. Could you send a couple of photos and any model/part numbers in the meantime?
