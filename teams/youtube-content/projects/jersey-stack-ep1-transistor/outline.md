# Video Outline: The Jersey Stack — Episode 1: The Transistor

## Target
Documentary, 12–15 minutes (aim ~14:45 narration), general tech-curious audience. Series opener.
Tone: **Veritasium meets early Hank Green.** Intellectually serious, personally alive, zero dryness. Third-person narrator (ElevenLabs Josh) with lived-in authority — someone who has been inside these buildings.

## Series Thesis
New Jersey was the real tech incubator for the world. A ~20-mile stretch invented the physical, electrical, and software layers of the computer age — and this episode is the origin beat.

## Episode Logline
December 23, 1947. Three men at Bell Labs in Murray Hill press a paperclip-sized piece of germanium against two gold contacts. A voice amplifies. The twentieth century pivots. Every phone in every pocket, every server in every cloud, every pixel on every Netflix screen descends from that ten-minute demonstration.

## Critical geography reminders (tag every scene)
- **MURRAY HILL** (Berkeley Heights / New Providence, NJ) — birthplace of the transistor, Dec 23 1947. Still active as Nokia Bell Labs.
- **HOLMDEL** (Saarinen glass building, 1962; now Bell Works; transistor water tower out front) — built 15 years AFTER the invention. Memorial site. Lumon HQ in *Severance*.
- **FORT MONMOUTH / CAMP EVANS** (Wall Township, NJ) — Army Signal Corps Labs, early transistor customer; now being reborn as Netflix Studios (Dec 5 2025 close, Phase 1A 2027, Phase 1B 2028).

## Chapter 3: The Problem — "Why Vacuum Tubes Had to Die" (3:15 – 4:25)
### Summary
AT&T's engineering problem: every long-distance call rode copper and needed amplification every few dozen miles. Vacuum tubes did the amplifying — hot, fragile, enormous, short-lived. Scale the network and the math breaks. Kelly's bet: solid-state physics could replace the tube. Semiconductor primer — germanium, electrons, holes — kept short and correct. Shockley's 1945 field-effect amplifier concept: didn't work. Two years of dead ends.
### Key Points
- The vacuum tube: how it amplifies, why it's a dead end at scale
- Transcontinental repeater problem
- Semiconductor primer — germanium, electrons, holes (kept brief)
- Shockley's early field-effect concept (1945) — didn't work
### Scenes: 3
### Visual Approach

## Chapter 5: December 23, 1947 — "The Bench" (6:10 – 7:55)
### Summary
**EMOTIONAL CENTER. DO NOT RUSH.** The afternoon of Dec 23, 1947, at MURRAY HILL. Gold foil on a plastic wedge pressed into a germanium slab — paperclip-scale. Brattain sets it up. Bardeen and Brattain invite colleagues and managers into Lab 1E-455. Microphone → device → loudspeaker. Brattain speaks. The voice comes out amplified, gain of about eighteen. They call it a "transfer resistor" — quickly shortened to transistor. That evening Bardeen drives home and says to his wife Jane, "We discovered something today." Then goes to bed. The 20th century has pivoted.
### Key Points
- Physical setup — germanium slab, plastic wedge, gold foil, paperclip scale
- How it worked — clean, correct, short
- Live audio demo — amplified voice
- Bardeen's understated home quote

## Chapter 6: The Fracture — "Shockley's Hotel Room" (7:55 – 9:05)
### Summary
Shockley wasn't in the room. When he learns what Bardeen and Brattain did without him, he's furious. Tries to muscle his name onto the point-contact patent. Bell lawyers find his own prior writings too close to Lilienfeld's 1925 patent and keep him off. Shockley boards a train to Chicago, checks into a hotel, and over four weeks of Dec 1947 – Jan 1948 invents — alone, out of competitive rage — a better device: the **junction transistor** (Jan 23, 1948). He shuts Bardeen and Brattain out of the follow-on work. Brattain transfers groups. Bardeen leaves Bell Labs for Illinois in 1951 (and wins a second Nobel in 1972 for superconductivity). The trio never really collaborates again. **One honest sentence acknowledging Shockley's later-life descent into eugenics advocacy — do not dwell.**
### Key Points
- Patent fight — Shockley tries to put his name on point-contact invention alone
- Lilienfeld 1925 as legal obstacle
- Chicago hotel room → junction transistor, Jan 23, 1948
- Team breaks: Brattain transfers, Bardeen leaves for UIUC
- One sentence flagging Shockley's later descent (eugenics advocacy)
### Scenes: 3
### Visual Approach
Cooler palette. Shockley 1959 blackboard shot. AI Chicago hotel room — cigarette smoke, banker's lamp, notebook pages. AI macro of junction-transistor schematic in a notebook. Portraits separate on screen at the chapter close.

## Chapter 8: 1956 — "The Nobel and the Decree" (10:30 – 11:25)
### Summary
Two things happen in 1956. First: Shockley, Bardeen, and Brattain share the Nobel Prize in Physics. They stand on a stage together for the first time in years. Second, and bigger: AT&T signs a consent decree ending a seven-year antitrust fight — it's forced to license every Bell patent, including the transistor, to any American company that asks, for nominal fees. 7,820 patents become effectively open. Gordon Moore will later call it "one of the most important developments for the commercial semiconductor industry." The moat is gone. The industry can begin.
### Key Points
- 1956 Nobel — trio reunites briefly on stage
- 1956 AT&T consent decree — patent moat dissolves
- Gordon Moore quote
- Bell Labs workshops that taught the rest of the industry how to build them
### Scenes: 2
### Visual Approach
Three Nobel PD portraits in sequence (no free group shot of the ceremony exists — use the triptych). Stylized legal-document treatment of the consent decree with animated key phrases. Floodgates metaphor AI shot for "the moat dissolved." Music swells — this is the turning point.

## Chapter 9: The Diaspora — "Silicon Valley Came From Jersey" (11:25 – 12:35)
### Summary
**THIS IS THE PAYOFF BEAT. DO NOT RUSH — but do not over-enumerate.** Later in 1956, Shockley leaves Bell Labs and founds Shockley Semiconductor in Mountain View, CA. He recruits eight brilliant young PhDs. He is, by every account, a terrible boss. **September 18, 1957:** all eight walk into his office and resign together. History names them the **Traitorous Eight**. Twelve blocks away, they found **Fairchild Semiconductor**. In 1968, Noyce and Moore leave Fairchild to found **Intel**. Kleiner later co-founds Kleiner Perkins. Others seed AMD, National Semiconductor, dozens more. **Silicon Valley is a diaspora from New Jersey.** Shockley carried the seed west. The seed came from Murray Hill.
### Key Points
- Shockley → Mountain View, 1956 → bad management → Sept 18, 1957 walkout → Fairchild (combined into one scene in Gate 2 revision)
- Fairchild → Intel (1968, Noyce + Moore) → AMD → Kleiner Perkins → the Valley
- Thesis line preserved verbatim: *"Silicon Valley came from Jersey. It just built a better brand."*

## Chapter 10: What It Became — "One to Ninety-Two Billion" (12:35 – 13:15)
### Summary
One transistor on a bench in 1947. Today Apple's M3 Max packs about ninety-two billion transistors onto a chip the size of a postage stamp. Eight orders of magnitude smaller, a trillion times cheaper. Every phone, every server, every car, every pacemaker, every pixel — all of it riding on that ten-minute demo. Moore's Law: a 77-year story of one demonstration compounding.
### Key Points
- 1 → ~92 billion transistors per chip (M3 Max, largest publicly confirmed M-series figure)
- Moore's Law as compounding of one demo
- Ubiquity — everything modern runs on descendants of the 1947 device
### Scenes: 2
### Visual Approach
