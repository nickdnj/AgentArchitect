But the bigger thing that happened to the transistor in nineteen fifty-six was not the Nobel. It was a federal courtroom. To settle a seven-year antitrust case, AT&T signed a consent decree forcing it to license every patent in its vault — more than seven thousand of them, including the transistor — to any American company that asked, for a nominal fee. Intel co-founder Gordon Moore would later call it 'one of the most important developments for the commercial semiconductor industry.' In one signature, the moat dissolved. Anyone could build a transistor now. And a lot of people were about to.
