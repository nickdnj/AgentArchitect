### A1 — Early Audion Telephone Repeaters, Princeton NJ, 1919
**Title:** Early Audion telephone repeaters.jpg
**Year:** 1919
**Source:** Wikimedia Commons / originally published in Bancroft Gherardi and Frank B. Jewett, "Telephone repeaters," *Transactions of the AIEE*, Vol. 38, Part 2, October 1, 1919, plate 52
**Collection:** Wikimedia Commons — uploaded October 19, 2013
**Direct download URL:** https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bc/Early_Audion_telephone_repeaters.jpg
**Resolution:** 968 × 740 px (below 1080p threshold — see note)
**License:** Public domain (first published before January 1, 1931; copyright expired)
**License basis:** US PD-pre1931
**Visual description:** Black-and-white photograph of two relay racks in a repeater station at Princeton, NJ, on the Boston–Washington, DC trunk line. Each rack holds two Type 22 repeaters, each containing two triode amplifiers (one per direction for duplex operation). Approximately 200 such units were installed; ~1,000 were in service across the US Bell System at the time.
**Bell System confidence:** VERY HIGH. Captioned and sourced directly from a 1919 AIEE paper by Bell System engineers Gherardi and Jewett. This is the canonical "telephone repeater rack" image.
**Note on resolution:** At 968×740 px this falls below the 2000 px target. However, because it's from a published 1919 journal scan, no higher-resolution version exists on Wikimedia. The original paper (available on archive.org) may yield a slightly higher-resolution scan. Acceptable as a B-roll cutaway at moderate scale; may show compression at full 1920×1080 if over-enlarged. Recommend pairing with color correction and careful framing.

## Top Picks

### Best Wide Rack Shot: **A1 — Early Audion Telephone Repeaters (1919)**
The only confirmed public domain photograph showing actual Bell System telephone repeater racks with vacuum tube amplifiers that is currently on Wikimedia and freely downloadable. Authenticity is undisputable: captioned by Bell System engineers in a 1919 AIEE paper. **Limitation:** 968×740 px is below the 2000 px target — usable only at moderate scale (max ~50% screen fill, or as a pull-back shot with some soft focus). Resolution acceptable for B-roll cutaway treatment.

**Strong fallback for Wide Rack: A4 — Bell Labs Record, Feb. 1949 (V3 relay rack photo)**
This should be the top priority to manually extract. The Feb. 1949 Bell Labs Record relay-rack photograph ("ten V3 amplifier units on a relay rack") is exactly what the scene calls for — dense, populated, 1940s-era — and the publication is pre-1964 with high likelihood of PD status. Resolution from a full-page plate in this journal typically yields 1800–2500 px. Access the PDF manually at worldradiohistory.com.

---

### Best Tube Close-Up: **B2 — Western Electric VT-1 (Wikimedia, CC0, 2287×4071 px)**
Clean, high-resolution, CC0, museum-quality photograph. The VT-1 is the foundational Western Electric telephone repeater tube. Exceeds the 2000 px target comfortably. Ready to download directly with no rights issues.

**Strong fallback for Tube Close-Up: B4 — Smithsonian 205D or 216A**
The WE 205D is explicitly a telephone amplifier tube used in Bell System carrier equipment. If the Smithsonian NMAH marks the object photograph CC0, it would be the highest-quality and most contextually accurate tube close-up available. Verify CC0 status before downloading.
