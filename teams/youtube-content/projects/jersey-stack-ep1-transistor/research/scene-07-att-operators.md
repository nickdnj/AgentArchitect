# Scene 07 — AT&T Telephone Operator Photograph Candidates
**Series:** The Jersey Stack — Episode 1 "The Transistor"
**Scene timecode:** ~2:30–3:15
**Usage:** ~3-second beat, slow Ken Burns dissolve, establishing AT&T as regulated monopoly funneling profits into Bell Labs research
**Narration context:** "AT&T was a regulated monopoly. Stable profits, predictable profits — and a portion of those profits was, by design, funneled into research with no deadline."

---

## Recommended Top 2 Picks

### PICK 1 — NARA Bell System International Switchboard (Dec 22, 1943)
**Why:** This is the single most on-brand photograph available in public archives. It explicitly documents Bell System operators (AT&T subsidiary) handling overseas calls during WWII — the exact institutional context your narration is describing. Multiple women are visible in a row at a vast cord-panel switchboard. It is from the Women's Bureau record group (RG 86) at NARA, clearly dated Dec 22, 1943, and reproduced freely by Wikimedia Commons at 2,930 × 2,400 px — sufficient for 1080p Ken Burns with ~37% headroom. License is unambiguous public domain (U.S. Government Records, no copyright restrictions).

### PICK 2 — LOC/FSA-OWI — Washington D.C. Telegraph Office Operators (June 1943, Esther Bubley)
**Why:** Shot by Esther Bubley for the Farm Security Administration/Office of War Information in June 1943 — among the most respected documentary photographers of the era. The FSA-OWI collection is categorical public domain (U.S. Government work). Reproduction number LC-DIG-fsa-8d30845. TIFF master available via LOC Duplication Services; the digital scan is downloadable from the LOC item page and should be 3,000+ px. Washington D.C. municipal setting is visually ambiguous — reads as generic "1940s telephone operations" rather than specifically Southern Bell, which is actually better for the AT&T/monopoly narration beat.
