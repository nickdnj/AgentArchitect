## Critical Errors (Must Fix Before Public Release)

### ERROR 1 — Scene 05: Mervin Kelly's title in 1945 is wrong

**Exact narration:**
> "The man who decided what to do with that money was Mervin Kelly, the Director of Research."

**What's wrong:**
Kelly was Director of Research from 1936–**1944**, when he was promoted to **Executive Vice-President**. By 1945 (the year the narration is describing), Kelly held the title of Executive Vice-President, not Director of Research. He was not promoted to President until 1951.

**What's correct:**
"Mervin Kelly, the Executive Vice-President of Research" — or, more accurately for a general audience: "Mervin Kelly, who ran Bell Labs' research program." The simplest fix: call him "the head of research" or omit the title. Saying "Director of Research" is technically wrong for 1945.

**Sources:**
- [ETHW — Mervin J. Kelly](https://ethw.org/Mervin_J._Kelly): confirms 1936 Director, 1944 Exec VP, 1951 President
- [PBS Transistor — Kelly bio](https://www.pbs.org/transistor/album1/addlbios/kelly.html)

## Minor Errors / Sloppiness (Recommend Fixing)

### MINOR 1 — Scene 16: The "we discovered something today" quote belongs to December 16, not December 23

**Exact narration (Scene 16):**
> "Bardeen drove home that evening. He did not make a speech, he did not call a press conference. When his wife Jane asked him how his day had been, he is reported to have said, quote — 'we discovered something today.' Then he went to bed. The twentieth century had just pivoted in a basement in New Jersey, and the man who did it closed his eyes and went to sleep."

**Context:**
Scene 16 follows the December 23 demonstration (Scene 14/15). The narration implies the "we discovered something today" quote happened on the evening of December 23, after the management demonstration.

**What's wrong (nuanced):**
Most authoritative sources place the Bardeen quote on the evening of **December 16, 1947** — when the first successful amplification was privately achieved by Bardeen and Brattain — not December 23. December 23 was the formal demonstration to Bell Labs management. The two dates are distinct events. The quote is real, but it is canonically attributed to December 16, not December 23.

The Computer History Museum and multiple biographers are explicit: "On December 16, 1947, Bardeen… told his wife Jane, who was peeling carrots, 'We discovered something today.'"

**Risk:**
The narration structure leads viewers to believe the quote happened after the December 23 management demo. History enthusiasts will flag this. The quote is real and the spirit is correct, but the placement in the timeline is off by 7 days.

**Recommended fix:**
Move the quote to its correct date context, or soften the attribution: "On the evening he first got it to work — a week before the Christmas demo — Bardeen reportedly told his wife Jane, 'we discovered something today.'" This is actually a stronger story beat because it emphasizes the quiet private moment of discovery.

**Sources:**
- [Computer History Museum — Surface State Job](https://computerhistory.org/blog/the-surface-state-job/)
- [EDN — First successful test Dec. 16, 1947](https://www.edn.com/1st-successful-test-of-the-transistor-december-16-1947/)

### MINOR 2 — Scene 18: The hotel city and duration of Shockley's isolation are imprecise

**Exact narration:**
> "So Shockley got on a train to Chicago, checked into a hotel room, and for four weeks — while his colleagues celebrated Christmas and New Year — he filled notebook after notebook, alone, out of pure competitive rage."

**What's wrong:**
- The **duration** of "four weeks" is an overstatement. Sources indicate Shockley traveled to Chicago for the American Physical Society annual meeting around New Year's Eve, then worked in his hotel room for approximately **"almost a week"** (IEEE Spectrum), or at most **"by the end of the month"** (January), generating ~30 pages of notes. The key conception date is January 23, 1948 — exactly one month after December 23, but not four continuous weeks in a hotel.
- The phrase "notebook after notebook" implies multiple notebooks; the documented record is a single entry in Bell Labs notebook No. 20455, pages 128-132.
- "Christmas and New Year" framing is plausible but implies he left before Christmas — sources say he went to Chicago for the **New Year's Eve** APS meeting, meaning his colleagues had already celebrated Christmas by then.
- The hotel was the **Bismarck Hotel** in Chicago, per one primary source; naming it would add credibility.

**Recommended fix:**
"Shockley got on a train to Chicago, checked into the Bismarck Hotel, and over several days in January — fueled entirely by jealousy — filled pages in his notebook. On January twenty-third, exactly one month after the basement demo, he had it."

**Sources:**
- [CHM — Conception of the Junction Transistor](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/conception-of-the-junction-transistor/)
- [PBS — Shockley Lab Page Jan. 23, 1948](https://www.pbs.org/transistor/science/labpages/labpg9.html)

## Risky Simplifications (Judgment Calls — Flag for Nick)

### RISK 1 — Scene 17: Patent story — Shockley "tried to get his name added to the patent application — alone"

**Exact narration:**
> "He tried to get his name added to the patent application — alone. Bell Labs' lawyers looked at Shockley's own writings, found them uncomfortably close to a nineteen-twenty-five patent by a German physicist named Julius Lilienfeld, and quietly left Shockley's name off the document entirely."

**Assessment:**
The broad story is accurate: Bell Labs patent attorneys did exclude Shockley from the point-contact transistor patent (US 2,524,035) because his proposed field-effect approach resembled Lilienfeld's 1925/1926 prior art, and Shockley did attempt to claim priority. However, the specific framing has two risks:

1. **Lilienfeld's nationality:** Julius Edgar Lilienfeld was born in what is now Ukraine (then part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire), raised partly in Germany, and was a naturalized American citizen when he filed the patent. Calling him "a German physicist" is a simplification — he's more accurately described as an Austrian-American or German-American physicist, and the patent was filed in the **United States**, not Germany. In YouTube comments, someone will push back on "German physicist."

2. **Patent date:** Lilienfeld filed the key US patent in **January 1925** (published 1926, granted 1930 as US 1,745,175). The narration says "a nineteen-twenty-five patent" — the filing date is correct, though technically it was issued in 1930.

**Recommendation:**
Change "a German physicist named Julius Lilienfeld" to "a physicist named Julius Lilienfeld." The nationality detail is risky and not load-bearing to the story.

**Sources:**
- [CHM — Field-Effect Semiconductor Device Concepts Patented](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/field-effect-semiconductor-device-concepts-patented/)
- [Hackaday — Julius Lilienfeld and the First Transistor](https://hackaday.com/2018/12/11/julius-lilienfeld-and-the-first-transistor/)
- [EDN — Lilienfeld patents FET, October 8, 1926](https://www.edn.com/lilienfeld-patents-field-effect-transistor-october-8-1926/)
