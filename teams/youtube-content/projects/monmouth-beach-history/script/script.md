## [SEGMENT 2: The First People - 0:20-1:00]

[VISUAL: Misty dawn over the Shrewsbury River. Slow pan across marshland and waterways.]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "12,000 Years Ago"]

NARRATION:
"Long before any European set foot on this narrow strip of sand between the Atlantic Ocean and the Shrewsbury River, the Lenape people knew this place. Archaeologists have traced their presence here back twelve thousand years. They were seasonal dwellers -- following the rhythms of the coast. In warmer months, they fished the river for shellfish and harvested the bounty of the shore. When winter came, they moved inland."

[VISUAL: Depiction of Lenape coastal settlement with fishing and trading activity]

"In sixteen eighty-eight, a man named Eliakim Wardell changed everything. He purchased four hundred and fifty-four acres from the Lenape -- all the land north of Long Branch, below Sandy Hook, between the Shrewsbury River and the Atlantic Ocean."

[PAUSE]

"The price? Four pounds. Paid in blankets, clothing, and hardware. Roughly eleven cents an acre."

## [SEGMENT 3: Land Pirates - 1:00-1:50]

[VISUAL: Dramatic painting-style image of a ship being battered by waves near a rocky shore, lanterns visible on the beach]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "The Wreckers of Monmouth County"]

NARRATION:
"As settlements grew along the shore, so did the shipwrecks. Between eighteen forty-six and eighteen forty-eight alone, more than one hundred and twenty vessels wrecked along the New Jersey coast. And with each disaster came a damning rumor -- that the locals were not just salvaging the wreckage. They were causing it."

[VISUAL: Dark figure on a beach holding a lantern, waves in background]

"The people of Monmouth County were branded bandits and land pirates. Newspapers accused them of hanging false lights to confuse ships' captains, driving vessels onto sandbars so they could plunder the cargo."

"In seventeen ninety-nine, the New Jersey legislature made it a crime to display false lights to induce a shipwreck. But here is the part that history often forgets."

[PAUSE]

"There is no evidence -- none -- that anyone in Monmouth County ever lured a ship to its destruction. The so-called wreckers were, in fact, wreck-masters -- government-appointed officials who coordinated rescues and protected survivors."

[TRANSITION: Crossfade, 1s]

## [SEGMENT 4: Storm Warriors - 1:50-2:40]

[VISUAL: Life-saving station on the beach, men in uniform preparing rescue equipment]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "The U.S. Life-Saving Service -- Est. 1871"]

NARRATION:
"The real story of this coast is not piracy. It is heroism. After the wreck of the New Era off what is now Asbury Park in eighteen fifty-four -- a disaster that drowned more than two hundred and forty German immigrants in a single nor'easter -- the federal government created the United States Life-Saving Service."

[VISUAL: Dramatic rescue scene with surfmen launching a boat into heavy surf]

"Stations were built roughly every three miles along the coast. The men who served in them were called surfmen, and later, Storm Warriors. Their unofficial oath became legend."

[PAUSE]

[TEXT OVERLAY: "You have to go out. Nothing says you have to come back."]

"You have to go out. Nothing says you have to come back."

[VISUAL: The Monmouth Beach Life-Saving Station, historic photograph]

"The Monmouth Beach station was built in eighteen ninety-five. Over four decades, the Life-Saving Service aided more than twenty-eight thousand vessels and rescued nearly one hundred and eighty thousand people. That station still stands today. It is the Monmouth Beach Cultural Center."

[TRANSITION: Crossfade, 1.5s]

[VISUAL: Small fishing boats at a wooden dock, nets hanging, weathered Scandinavian fishermen at work]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "Galilee -- The Fishing Village"]

NARRATION:
"But Monmouth Beach was never only a playground for the rich. At its northern edge, in an area called Galilee, a different community thrived. These were fishermen -- mostly of Norwegian and Swedish descent -- hardworking families who had come to the Jersey Shore not for leisure, but for the sea."

[VISUAL: Fish market scene, boats heading out at dawn]

"In eighteen eighty-four, they chartered the Galilee Fishing Association and became a major supplier of fresh fish to the New York markets. While the millionaires sipped cocktails in their wooden palaces, the men of Galilee hauled nets in the Shrewsbury River before dawn."

## [SEGMENT 9: Rum Runners - 5:25-6:05]

[VISUAL: Dark water at night. A low, fast boat cuts through the waves without lights. In the distance, a larger vessel waits.]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "Prohibition -- 1920-1933"]

NARRATION:
"Then came Prohibition. And the same skills that made the men of Monmouth County legendary fishermen made them exceptional criminals."

[PAUSE]

"Monmouth County was perfectly suited for smuggling -- riddled with small inlets and streams, home to experienced seamen and boat builders. The fishermen's traditional Sea Bright skiff was modified into something new: souped-up speedboats that could outrun Coast Guard cutters."

[VISUAL: Modified Sea Bright skiff racing across dark water, Coast Guard vessel in pursuit]

"On the darkest nights, the rum-runners would race into the shallows of Raritan Bay, run their flat-bottomed skiffs up onto the beach, hand off crates of whiskey to a waiting truck, and vanish before the law arrived."

"When the government extended the Rum Line from three miles offshore to twelve, it became too dangerous for the small craft. The golden age of bootlegging along the Monmouth shore came to an end."

## [SEGMENT 10: The Ocean Always Wins - 6:05-6:40]

[VISUAL: Massive waves crashing over a seawall. Aerial view of a narrow barrier beach between ocean and river.]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "October 29, 2012 -- Hurricane Sandy"]

NARRATION:
"Monmouth Beach has always understood one fundamental truth: the ocean always wins. The German three-master Flottbek wrecked here in nineteen-oh-one. Storms destroyed the wooden seawall in nineteen thirty-one. Engineers rebuilt, reinforced, and rebuilt again -- making this stretch one of the most heavily engineered coastlines in the world."

[VISUAL: Hurricane Sandy destruction -- flooded streets, damaged buildings, sand-covered roads]

"Then, on October twenty-ninth, twenty-twelve, Hurricane Sandy proved that none of it was enough. Scientists called it one of the most powerful storms in recorded history. Millions of cubic yards of sand were swept away in a single night. Ocean and river met -- with people in between."

[TRANSITION: Slow fade to quieter imagery]

The rum-runners are the stuff of grandfathers' stories."
