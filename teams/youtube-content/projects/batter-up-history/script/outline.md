# Video Outline: Batter Up -- The Last Piece of Jolly Roger's

## Target: Documentary, 6-7 minutes, Long Island nostalgia crowd (35-65) + baseball/softball families

---

## Chapter 1: The Hook (0:00 - 0:45)
### Summary
Open with the sensory experience of Batter Up today -- the crack of bats, the whir of pitching machines -- then reveal the surprising secret hiding in plain sight: this modest batting cage is the last surviving piece of a legendary Long Island amusement park.
### Key Points
- Sensory hook: sounds and sights of Batter Up on a summer evening
- The question: "What if you've been standing on a piece of Long Island history and never knew it?"
- Tease the connection to Jolly Roger's / Nunley's Happyland
### Scenes: 3
### Visual Approach
Modern-day imagery of batting cages (AI-generated or sourced), warm evening light, then a dramatic reveal transition to vintage-style visuals.

---

## Chapter 2: Happyland (0:45 - 2:15)
### Summary
Travel back to 1951 and the opening of Nunley's Happyland -- a massive family amusement park built at the corner of Hempstead Turnpike and Hicksville Road. Introduce William Nunley, his death before opening day, and Miriam Nunley's remarkable 13 years running the park.
### Key Points
- William Nunley: third-generation amusement park entrepreneur, dies April 1951
- Park opens October 12, 1951 -- Miriam and Norman Russell take over
- Rides: Schiff Ferris wheel, roller coaster, 48-horse carousel, Sky Fighter, bumper cars, miniature railway
- The 1910 German mechanical organ with animated musician figures
- Up to 4,000 kids on a summer Saturday; 400-car parking lot
- Designed for year-round operation with heated indoor pavilion
### Scenes: 4
### Visual Approach
AI-generated period illustrations of 1950s Long Island, the park entrance, rides in action. Ken Burns pan across archival-style images. Warm, golden color palette.

---

## Chapter 3: Jolly Roger's (2:15 - 3:15)
### Summary
In 1952, the Jolly Roger restaurant opens next door and accidentally gives the entire complex its most famous name. Explore how "Jolly Roger's" became the name everyone used, and how the Tarnow and Giddens families expanded the complex in the 1960s -- including batting cages and mini golf across Hicksville Road.
### Key Points
- 1952: Jolly Roger restaurant built by Max Lander, owned by Rubin and Feldstein
- Glass-walled passageway connecting restaurant to park
- "Jolly Roger's" becomes the name everyone remembers
- 1964: New owners expand; Wild Mouse roller coaster, bumper car building added
- Batting cages and mini golf appear across Hicksville Road -- the exact spot where Batter Up stands today
### Scenes: 3
### Visual Approach
AI-generated illustrations of the Jolly Roger restaurant facade, families streaming between the park and restaurant. Aerial-style illustration showing the full complex with Hicksville Road dividing the two sides.

---

## Chapter 4: The End of an Era (3:15 - 4:15)
### Summary
The 1970s bring decline and closure. The restaurant becomes Robin Hood, then closes in 1976. The park follows in 1978. Demolition in 1979. "Not a trace remains" -- except one thing, across the street.
### Key Points
- 1974: Jolly Roger renamed to Robin Hood
- 1976: Robin Hood restaurant closes
- 1978: The park closes after nearly three decades
- 1979: Demolition -- Robert Berkowitz captures final photos before the wrecking ball
- A strip mall rises where thousands of kids once rode the carousel
- "Not a trace remains" -- the haunting reality of how completely it vanished
- But wait: across Hicksville Road, the batting cages survive
### Scenes: 4
### Visual Approach
Transition to muted, cooler tones. Pre-demolition photography style (referencing the Berkowitz photos). Then a dramatic visual shift -- Google Earth/aerial view showing the strip mall where the park was, panning across the street to where Batter Up still stands.

---

## Chapter 5: Batter Up (4:15 - 5:30)
### Summary
In 1979, the batting cage facility is rebuilt. In 1984, the De Marco family takes over and names it Batter Up. For 40+ years, they have been the stewards of this last piece of the Jolly Roger's legacy -- a family business keeping a family tradition alive.
### Key Points
- 1979: Batting cage facility at 130 Hicksville Road rebuilt/expanded
- 1984: The De Marco family takes over, names it Batter Up
- M&M Batter Up Inc. incorporated February 21, 1986
- 11 fully automatic batting cages (baseball and softball, 35-80 MPH)
- 18-hole mini golf course
- 40+ years of continuous De Marco family ownership
- Three generations of Long Islanders have swung a bat here
### Scenes: 3
### Visual Approach
Return to warm, vibrant colors. Modern-day imagery of the facility. Emphasis on the family business feel -- handpainted signs, well-worn batting helmets, the mini golf course.

---

## Chapter 6: The Legacy Lives On (5:30 - 6:30)
### Summary
The emotional payoff: grandparents who rode the carousel at Jolly Roger's now bring their grandkids to hit at Batter Up. The batting cages are literally the last living piece of that entire world. Close with a call to action -- come visit, share your memories, and keep the tradition alive.
### Key Points
- Intergenerational thread: grandparents to grandkids, same corner of Bethpage
- The carousel survives too (at the Long Island Children's Museum in Garden City) -- but only Batter Up is still in its original spot
- The Facebook group "Remembering Nunley's & Jolly Roger's" has thousands of active members sharing memories
- Call to action: visit Batter Up, share your Jolly Roger's memories in the comments
- Subscribe tease for future Long Island history content
### Scenes: 3
### Visual Approach
Split-screen style: vintage park scenes on one side, modern Batter Up on the other. Warm golden-hour light. End on an uplifting, celebratory image of kids at the batting cages today.
