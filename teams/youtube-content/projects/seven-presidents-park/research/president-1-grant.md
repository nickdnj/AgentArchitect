# President 1: Ulysses S. Grant (1869-1877)
## The Man Who Made Long Branch the Summer Capital

---

## Overview

Ulysses S. Grant's connection to Long Branch is the foundational story of the entire "Seven Presidents" narrative. He was the first sitting president to vacation there (1869), he returned every summer of his presidency, and he continued visiting until shortly before his death in 1885. More than any other figure, Grant put Long Branch on the map as a place of national significance.

---

## Timeline of Grant's Long Branch Connection

### 1867: The First Visit
- Grant, then **Commanding General of the Army** (not yet president), first visited Long Branch.
- He reportedly stated: **"In all of my travels I have never seen a better place for a summer residence than Long Branch."**
- He and Julia stayed at the newly built **Stetson Hotel** during early visits.

### 1868: George Childs's Invitation
- **George W. Childs**, a wealthy Philadelphia newspaper publisher (owner of the *Philadelphia Public Ledger*), had owned a cottage in Long Branch since 1866.
- Childs invited General Grant and Julia to his cottage shortly after Grant became the Republican nominee for president.
- The Grants thoroughly enjoyed the visit. Childs suggested Grant buy his own cottage, but Grant said he couldn't afford even a "modest" property.
- Childs offered to buy one for him; Grant declined, considering it inappropriate.

### 1869: The Gift of the Cottage
- After Grant's inauguration, Childs and **four other wealthy Long Branch neighbors** pooled their resources:
  - **George W. Childs** (publisher)
  - **George Pullman** (railroad sleeping car industrialist)
  - **Moses Taylor** (bank magnate)
  - Two additional contributors (names vary in sources)
- Each contributed approximately **$8,000**, totaling **$40,000** (some sources say $32,000).
- Knowing Grant would likely refuse a direct gift, they cleverly presented the cottage to **"Mrs. Grant"** instead. Julia accepted without hesitation.
- The cottage was located at **991 (or 995) Ocean Avenue** in the Elberon section of Long Branch.
- Grant became an official Long Branch resident in **February 1870**.

### 1869-1877: The Summer White House Years
- Grant summered at the cottage **every year of his two-term presidency**.
- Long Branch was officially recognized as the nation's **"Summer Capital."**
- The cottage functioned as a working executive office: **"messengers constantly visited President Grant's ocean-bound villa with bundles of official papers."**
- Grant held **cabinet meetings** at the cottage.

### 1877-1885: Post-Presidency
- Grant continued summering at Long Branch after leaving office, with breaks for his famous world tour (1877-1879).
- **1884:** Negotiated his famous *Personal Memoirs* contract with Century magazine while at the cottage. Also experienced the first symptoms of throat cancer there.
- **1885:** Final summer; Grant died on July 23, 1885, at Mount McGregor, NY, while finishing his memoirs.

---

## The Cottage: Physical Description

The Grant cottage was described as a **28-room mansion** in Stick Style architecture, despite being called a "cottage" (as was the convention for wealthy shore homes).

**First floor features:**
- Presidential office
- Sitting room
- Large parlor with fireplaces and a piano
- Wide, restful piazzas (porches) overlooking the ocean

**Underground infrastructure:**
- Dumbwaiters connecting to a basement kitchen
- An icehouse capable of storing "several hundred pounds of ice"

**Location:** 991/995 Ocean Avenue, Elberon, Long Branch, on a bluff overlooking the Atlantic Ocean.

---

## Daily Life at Long Branch

### The Routine
- Grant took **carriage rides twice daily** along Ocean Avenue
- Morning drive, then breakfast, then reading mail on the veranda
- **Poker nights with friends every Friday** during summer months
- The family swam in the Atlantic Ocean
- Julia described "glorious drives on that enchanting beach"

### The Horses
- Grant was an accomplished horseman and passionate about horses
- He attended **Monmouth Park** racetrack regularly, holding a box there
- Local legend has him speeding his carriage (the famous speeding arrest actually occurred in Washington, D.C. in 1872, where he was fined $20)
- The town reportedly built Monmouth Racetrack nearby partly for Grant's enjoyment

### Community Standing
- Local residents viewed Grant as Long Branch's **"first citizen"**
- Tourists and residents strolled past the Ocean Avenue property hoping to glimpse the president and first lady on their porch
- Grant became acquainted with most prominent residents

### Julia Grant's Perspective
From her memoirs: **"What a boon our cottage at Long Branch was to the President! Tired and weary as he was with his monotonous official duties, he hastened with delight, as soon as Congress adjourned, to its health-giving breezes and its wide and restful piazzas."**

---

## The Social Circle

Grant's presence attracted an extraordinary social network to Long Branch:

- **George W. Childs:** His closest Long Branch friend and neighbor. Childs later said: **"There never was a day when we were together there on which either I was not in his house or he in mine."**
- **George Pullman:** Railroad industrialist, one of the cottage donors
- **Moses Taylor:** Banking magnate, cottage donor
- **Jay Gould:** Railroad tycoon
- **General Thomas Eckert:** Civil War telegraph chief
- **General Winfield Scott:** Military hero

The cottage helped **popularize Elberon** as a gathering place for the elite -- a status that faded only when the railroad made the area more broadly accessible.

---

## The Financial Ruin: Grant & Ward Scandal (1884)

One of the most poignant chapters of Grant's post-presidential life played out partly at Long Branch.

### The Scheme
- Around 1880, Grant's son **Ulysses "Buck" Grant Jr.** partnered with **Ferdinand Ward** to form the brokerage firm **Grant & Ward**.
- Ward was secretly running what we now recognize as a **Ponzi scheme** -- recruiting investors with promises of high returns, using new investors' money to pay earlier ones.
- The scheme collapsed on **May 4, 1884**, bankrupting the Grants, the Marine National Bank, illustrator **Thomas Nast**, and many others.
- Ward took $150,000 from William Vanderbilt and fled New York City.

### The Long Branch Connection
- Grant made a **public statement about the failure** from his Long Branch cottage.
- The scandal cast a shadow over his final years at the shore.
- Ward was later found guilty of fraud and sentenced to 10 years in prison.

### Writing the Memoirs
- Facing financial ruin and battling throat cancer, Grant began writing his **Personal Memoirs** partly at the Long Branch cottage in 1884.
- The contract with Century magazine (later completed through Mark Twain's publishing house) was negotiated at Long Branch.
- Grant died on July 23, 1885, days after completing the manuscript. The memoirs earned his family approximately $450,000 in royalties.

---

## Fate of the Cottage

- **1941:** The **Sisters of St. Joseph of Peace** purchased the cottage to establish the **Stella Maris Retreat Center**.
- The home fell into disrepair and the Sisters could not afford renovations.
- **1963:** The cottage was demolished "with little fanfare."
- The Library of Congress documented the cottage through the **Historic American Buildings Survey (HABS)** before demolition; photographs from 1933 exist.
- **Today:** The site is a grassy field. Modern luxury homes are under construction on Grant's former land.

---

## Key Anecdotes for the Documentary

1. **The "Mrs. Grant" trick:** How wealthy friends circumvented Grant's refusal to accept gifts by presenting the cottage to Julia instead.

2. **The first citizen:** The image of tourists walking past the cottage hoping to catch a glimpse of the president and first lady on their porch -- an early precursor to modern presidential celebrity culture.

3. **The poker games:** Friday night poker with America's wealthiest men, at the beach, while governing the nation.

4. **Julia's memoir quote:** Her emotional description of how the cottage restored her exhausted husband.

5. **Grant and Garfield:** According to NJ Monthly, ex-president Grant reportedly **ignored President Garfield** in the Elberon Hotel dining room -- a possible snub worth exploring (needs verification).

6. **The cancer diagnosis:** Grant first experienced throat cancer symptoms at the cottage in 1884, the same year he faced financial ruin. The convergence of personal catastrophe at his beloved retreat is deeply dramatic.

7. **The demolition:** An American president's Summer White House, demolished in 1963 without ceremony, now a grassy lot. A commentary on how we preserve (or fail to preserve) history.

## Fact-Check Notes

- [ ] The exact purchase price varies by source: $32,000 vs. $40,000. The $8,000 per contributor / 5 contributors = $40,000 math works, but some sources say $32,000.
- [ ] Grant's speeding arrest: This happened in Washington, D.C., not Long Branch, though some Long Branch sources conflate the two. The NJ Monthly article mentions a $20 fine at Long Branch, which may be confusing the D.C. incident.
- [ ] The "snub" of Garfield at the Elberon Hotel needs verification beyond the single NJ Monthly reference.
- [ ] Cottage address varies: 991 vs. 995 Ocean Avenue. The LOC records say 995.
