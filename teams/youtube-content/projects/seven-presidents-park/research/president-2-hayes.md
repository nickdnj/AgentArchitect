# President 2: Rutherford B. Hayes (1877-1881)
## The Most Contested Presidential Connection

---

## Overview

Rutherford B. Hayes is traditionally listed as the second of the "Seven Presidents" who visited Long Branch, but his connection to the area is the **most historically disputed** of all seven. While his name appears on monuments and in park brochures, scholarly research has found little to no convincing documentary evidence that Hayes ever vacationed in Monmouth County.

This controversy is itself a compelling story for the documentary -- it reveals how local legends are created and sustained for economic and cultural purposes.

---

## What the Traditional Narrative Claims

The standard story holds that:
- Hayes visited Long Branch during his presidency (1877-1881)
- He stayed at or visited the Elberon Hotel
- He worshipped at St. James Chapel (Church of the Presidents)
- His visits helped boost the Elberon Hotel's popularity

These claims appear in:
- Park brochures and signage at Seven Presidents Park
- The Church of the Presidents markers
- Local tourism materials
- Various encyclopedic and general history sources

---

## What the Evidence Actually Shows

### The Monmouth Timeline Investigation

The Monmouth Timeline website conducted detailed research and found:

1. **Hayes's diary:** His personal diary shows he summered at the **Soldiers' Home** in Washington, D.C. -- the traditional presidential summer retreat located 4 miles from the White House. Buchanan and Lincoln also used this retreat.

2. **No contemporary documentation:** No newspaper accounts, diary entries, letters, or hotel registers have been found documenting a Hayes visit to Long Branch or Elberon during his presidency.

3. **No Monmouth County visit evidence:** The researchers concluded that Hayes's inclusion was "unsupported by any evidence."

### How Hayes Was Added to the List

The inclusion of Hayes in the "Seven Presidents" appears to have originated in **1925** when the Church of the Presidents was facing financial difficulties:

- The chapel was threatened by a $650 road assessment it could not pay.
- Jewish businessman **Ernest Levy** donated funds to save the chapel.
- Levy's attorney, **Bernard Sandler**, simultaneously announced the chapel had been attended by **"six presidents"** -- adding Hayes and Arthur to the previously claimed Grant, Garfield, Harrison, McKinley, and Wilson without providing documentary evidence.
- Sandler called the chapel **"the Westminster Abbey of America"** -- clearly a promotional rather than historical claim.
- The press repeated the claims uncritically, and they became accepted as fact.

---

## Hayes: Historical Context

### The 1876 Election Controversy
- Hayes won the presidency in one of the most contested elections in American history.
- Democrat Samuel Tilden won the popular vote, but disputed electoral votes from Florida, Louisiana, and South Carolina were resolved by a congressional commission that awarded all contested votes to Hayes.
- Hayes was nicknamed **"Rutherfraud B. Hayes"** and **"His Fraudulency"** by opponents.
- He entered office with a legitimacy cloud that followed his entire presidency.

### Presidency (1877-1881)
- Hayes focused on civil service reform, a major issue of the era.
- He ended Reconstruction by withdrawing federal troops from the South.
- He was known for hosting the first Easter Egg Roll on the White House lawn (1878).
- His wife Lucy was nicknamed "Lemonade Lucy" for banning alcohol from the White House.
- Hayes served only one term by choice, honoring a campaign pledge.

### Why He Might (or Might Not) Have Visited
**Arguments for a visit:**
- Long Branch was the premier presidential resort during his era
- His predecessor Grant had established the tradition
- The Elberon social scene would have been natural for a Republican president
- Other prominent Republicans frequented the area

**Arguments against:**
- His diary is extensively preserved and makes no mention of Long Branch
- He preferred the Soldiers' Home in D.C.
- His personality was notably more austere and less social than Grant's
- "Lemonade Lucy" and the no-alcohol policy suggest a different social style than the Long Branch gambling-and-racing scene

---

## What This Means for the Documentary

The Hayes story is actually a fascinating **meta-narrative** about how history gets made:

1. **The power of branding:** A 1925 financial crisis led to the creation of a historical claim that became accepted fact for a century.
2. **The seven vs. six question:** If Hayes is removed, the "Seven Presidents" narrative becomes the "Six Presidents" narrative -- which is less marketable.
3. **The role of promotion:** The naming of the park, the church, and the monuments all reinforced the "seven" number, making it increasingly difficult to challenge.
4. **Honest history-telling:** The documentary can present both the traditional narrative and the scholarly challenge, letting viewers decide -- and showing how local history evolves.

---

## Key Talking Points

- Hayes is the **weakest link** in the Seven Presidents chain, but removing him would undermine a century of local identity.
- His inclusion reveals how **economic interests** (saving a church, promoting tourism) shape historical memory.
- The contrast between Hayes's austere, reform-minded presidency and Long Branch's gambling-fueled glamour is interesting regardless of whether he visited.
- The **Compromise of 1877** that made him president was a pivotal moment that ended Reconstruction -- a story worth telling even without a Long Branch connection.

## Fact-Check Notes

- [ ] **CRITICAL:** Multiple sources list Hayes as visiting Long Branch, but the Monmouth Timeline research finding zero evidence in his diary is significant. This should be presented as a genuine historical debate.
- [ ] Some sources mention Hayes's visits boosted the Elberon Hotel's popularity -- but this may be circular reasoning (the hotel claimed his visits to boost its prestige, and later sources cited the hotel's claims as evidence).
- [ ] The NPS article on Long Branch lists Hayes among the seven presidents but provides no specific details about his visits.
