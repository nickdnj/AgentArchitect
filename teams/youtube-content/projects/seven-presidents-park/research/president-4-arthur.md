# President 4: Chester A. Arthur (1881-1885)
## The Accidental President Who Came to Mourn

---

## Overview

Chester Alan Arthur's connection to Long Branch is defined by a single, powerfully dramatic moment: the night he traveled to Elberon to visit the widow of the president whose death made him president. Arthur was a Stalwart Republican whose assassin had literally shouted "Arthur is now President!" as he shot Garfield. The cloud of suspicion, the emotional visit to Mrs. Garfield, and Arthur's surprising transformation in office make for a compelling story of redemption and duty.

---

## Arthur's Pre-Presidential Connection to the Area

- Arthur had been an **Elberon homeowner since 1874**, giving him one of the longest personal connections to the Long Branch area of any of the seven presidents.
- Before his presidency, Arthur summered at **Spring Lake Beach** in 1877-1878 while serving as the Port Collector of New York (the federal customs chief, a major patronage position).
- His familiarity with the Monmouth County shore was thus well-established before the Garfield crisis.

---

## The Garfield Crisis: Arthur's Impossible Position

### The Stalwart Problem
- Arthur had been chosen as Garfield's running mate specifically to **appease the Stalwart faction** led by Senator Roscoe Conkling of New York.
- Arthur was a Conkling protege and a product of the spoils system -- he had been removed from the Port Collector job by President Hayes for corruption.
- When Guiteau shot Garfield and shouted **"I am a Stalwart of the Stalwarts! Arthur is now President!"**, it created an immediate cloud of suspicion around Arthur.
- Though Arthur had absolutely nothing to do with the assassination, the optics were devastating.

### The 79-Day Vigil
- During the 79 days between the shooting (July 2) and Garfield's death (September 19), Arthur was in an agonizing position:
  - He could not assume presidential authority without appearing to be seizing power
  - He could not be seen as eager for Garfield to die
  - He had no constitutional guidance for a president who was alive but incapacitated
  - He largely stayed out of public view at his home in New York City

---

## The Night of September 19-20, 1881

### The Swearing-In
- Garfield died at **10:35 PM on September 19, 1881**, at Francklyn Cottage in Elberon.
- Arthur received word and was sworn in as the **21st President** at approximately **2:15 AM on September 20** at his private residence in New York City.
- A more formal ceremony was held in Washington, D.C. on September 22.

### The Journey to Elberon
- Shortly after his swearing-in, Arthur traveled to Elberon with:
  - **Secretary of State James G. Blaine**
  - **Secretary of War Robert Todd Lincoln** (Abraham Lincoln's eldest son)
- Their purpose: to pay respects to **Lucretia Garfield**, the widowed first lady.

### The Meeting
- The local press described the encounter: **"The meeting between Mrs. Garfield and Mr. Arthur is described as an affecting one."**
- The emotional weight of this moment -- the man elevated to power by her husband's murder, standing before the grieving widow -- is extraordinary.

---

## Arthur's Presidency and the Area

### Summer of 1884
- In 1884, **President Arthur and his family** returned to Monmouth County.
- They occupied **"a suite of rooms" at the Octagon House hotel in Sea Bright** for the summer.
- Sea Bright is adjacent to Long Branch, about 3 miles north.

### Church of the Presidents
- Arthur is listed among the presidents who attended St. James Chapel (Church of the Presidents).
- He visited Elberon after Garfield's death to see the widow.
- However, the Monmouth Timeline research found **no documented vacation time in Monmouth County during his presidency** beyond the 1884 Sea Bright stay.

---

## Arthur's Presidency: Key Context

### The Transformation
Arthur underwent one of the most dramatic personal transformations of any American president:

- **Before the presidency:** A machine politician, a Stalwart, a product of the spoils system. His appointment as Port Collector of New York was pure patronage.
- **After taking office:** Arthur shocked everyone by championing **civil service reform**, the very cause Garfield's assassination had energized.
- He signed the **Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act of 1883**, which created the merit-based federal civil service system and ended the worst abuses of the spoils system.
- Former president Hayes wrote in his diary: "Arthur for President! Chet Arthur? Good heavens!"

### "The Gentleman Boss"
- Arthur was known for his **impeccable personal style** -- he owned 80 pairs of pants and was considered the best-dressed president.
- He was a widower (his wife Ellen died in 1880, before his inauguration) and kept the White House in elegant style.
- He ordered a **complete redecoration** of the White House, hiring Louis Comfort Tiffany.
- He was diagnosed with Bright's disease (kidney failure) during his presidency but kept it secret.

### One Term
- Arthur sought the Republican nomination in 1884 but lost to James G. Blaine.
- He died on November 18, 1886, just 20 months after leaving office, from complications of Bright's disease.

---

## Key Narrative Threads for the Documentary

### 1. The "Accidental President" Arc
- A machine politician nobody expected to be president, elevated by assassination, who then governed with unexpected integrity and principle.
- The irony: the spoilsman who signed the anti-spoils law.

### 2. The Elberon Visit
- The emotional weight of Arthur visiting Garfield's widow in the very cottage where the president died, with Lincoln's son at his side.
- Three degrees of presidential assassination: Lincoln (killed 1865, son present), Garfield (killed 1881, the victim), Arthur (the beneficiary who never wanted it this way).

### 3. The Guiteau Shadow
- Arthur had to govern under the suspicion that his faction had inspired the assassination, even though he personally had nothing to do with it.
- Guiteau claimed at trial that Arthur should be grateful to him.

### 4. The Civil Service Legacy
- Garfield's murder created the political will for reform; Arthur, improbably, was the one who signed it into law.
- Long Branch was the site where one president died for civil service reform and another president began his unexpected journey toward achieving it.
