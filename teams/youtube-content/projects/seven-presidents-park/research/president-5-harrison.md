# President 5: Benjamin Harrison (1889-1893)
## The Cape May President with an Elberon Epilogue

---

## Overview

Benjamin Harrison is an interesting case in the Seven Presidents story because his primary Jersey Shore connection was **Cape May, not Long Branch**. He established his "Summer White House" at Congress Hall in Cape May in 1891, and his friends gifted him a cottage at Cape May Point. His connection to the Long Branch/Elberon area appears to have been limited to post-presidential visits, making his inclusion among the "Seven Presidents" somewhat generous.

---

## Harrison's Primary Jersey Shore Connection: Cape May

### Congress Hall as Summer White House (1891)
- In 1891, Harrison made international headlines when he chose the first floor of **Congress Hall** in Cape May as his **Summer White House**.
- This was a significant moment in Cape May's history, reinforcing its status as a presidential resort.

### The Wanamaker Cottage Scandal
- **John Wanamaker**, the Philadelphia department store magnate who also served as Harrison's **Postmaster General**, decided to ensure Harrison would return to Cape May every summer.
- Wanamaker built the Harrisons a **23-room villa** at Beach and Harvard Avenues in Cape May Point, at a cost of **$10,000**.
- Harrison told Wanamaker he could not accept such an extravagant gift -- it would be unethical.
- Wanamaker, echoing the Grant cottage playbook, **gifted the home to the First Lady, Caroline Harrison**, in a White House ceremony in June 1890.

**The Scandal:**
- The cottage became a **Washington scandal**, with Wanamaker and his business partners accused of buying presidential favor for railroad and housing developers.
- To alleviate the controversy, Harrison sent Wanamaker a check for $10,000.
- Despite the controversy, Harrison continued to vacation at Cape May.

### Multiple Cape May Summers
- Harrison spent **at least three summers** at Cape May during and after his presidency.
- His wife Caroline relaxed at the Cape May Point cottage while he conducted business at Congress Hall.
- The Wanamaker cottage still exists today -- it was moved inland and is now the **Marianist Retreat** at Cape and Yale avenues.

## Harrison's Connection to Long Branch/Elberon

### The Evidence

The documentary evidence for Harrison's time in Long Branch is **thin and disputed**:

**What is documented:**
- **July 7, 1898:** Harrison (as a former president) stayed at the **Elberon Club**. A summer dinner party was hosted to honor and welcome him.
- **August 1894:** A dinner party was hosted in Elberon in Harrison's honor.
- Some sources claim he attended services at St. James Chapel (Church of the Presidents), but the evidence is based on **1927 letters from "relatives"** -- nearly 30 years after the fact.

**What is disputed:**
- Local newspapers reported in 1927 that "President Benjamin Harrison spent his summers in a cottage on the circle during the gay nineties" -- but this is a **single newspaper report** from decades later.
- **Monmouth Beach Inn:** A single newspaper account claims he summered there in the 1890s.
- The Monmouth Timeline research describes these as **"unverified"** claims.

### How Harrison Was Added as the Seventh President

Harrison was added to the "Seven Presidents" designation around **1930**, making him the last addition:
- The Church of the Presidents originally claimed six presidential visitors.
- Harrison was added based on letters from "relatives" claiming he attended services as a former president.
- This made the number a more marketable "seven."

## Harrison's Presidency: Key Context

### Background
- Born August 20, 1833, in North Bend, Ohio
- Grandson of President **William Henry Harrison** (9th President)
- The only president who was the grandson of another president
- Lawyer and Civil War veteran (brigadier general)
- U.S. Senator from Indiana (1881-1887)

### The 1888 Election
- Harrison defeated incumbent **Grover Cleveland** in the Electoral College despite **losing the popular vote** -- a controversial outcome that echoed the Hayes-Tilden dispute.
- Cleveland won the popular vote by about 100,000 votes but lost in the Electoral College 233-168.

### Key Accomplishments
- Signed the **Sherman Antitrust Act** (1890) -- the first federal antitrust law
- Signed the **McKinley Tariff** (1890) -- raised import duties dramatically
- Admitted six new states (the most of any president in a single term)
- Strengthened the Navy significantly
- Lost re-election to Cleveland in 1892, making Cleveland the only president to serve non-consecutive terms

### Personal Tragedy
- First Lady **Caroline Harrison** died in the White House in October 1892, just weeks before the election.
- Harrison remarried in 1896 to **Mary Scott Lord Dimmick**, Caroline's niece, which scandalized his adult children.

### Later Life and Death
- After leaving office, Harrison returned to law practice in Indianapolis.
- He represented Venezuela in an international boundary dispute before the Permanent Court of Arbitration in Paris (1898-1899).
- He died on March 13, 1901, at age 67, from pneumonia.

## Key Narrative Threads for the Documentary

### 1. The Cape May vs. Long Branch Question
- Harrison's story illustrates the **competition between Jersey Shore resort towns** for presidential prestige.
- Cape May can legitimately claim Harrison as its presidential resident; Long Branch's claim is weaker.
- This tension itself is worth exploring in the documentary.

### 2. The Wanamaker Cottage Parallel to Grant
- History repeating: wealthy friends gifting a presidential family a shore cottage.
- Grant's cottage was given to "Mrs. Grant"; Harrison's cottage was given to "Mrs. Harrison."
- Both cases generated controversy (though Harrison's was more overtly scandalous).

### 3. The "Seventh President" Addition
- Harrison was the last president added to the list, in 1930, completing the "seven" that gave the park its name.
- Without Harrison, it would be "Six Presidents Park" -- a less compelling name.
- This is a story about how historical narratives are constructed.

### 4. The Grandson President
- The only president who was the grandson of a president.
- William Henry Harrison died after just 31 days in office (1841); Benjamin served a full term but was defeated for re-election.
- A family dynasty story of presidential ambition and loss.
