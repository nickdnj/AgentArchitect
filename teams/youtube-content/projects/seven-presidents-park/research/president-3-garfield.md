# President 3: James A. Garfield (1881)
## The President Who Died at Long Branch -- The Richest Story

---

## Overview

James Garfield's story is the dramatic heart of the entire Seven Presidents narrative. Shot in Washington on July 2, 1881, just four months into his presidency, Garfield lingered for 79 days while doctors (who likely killed him through unsanitary treatment) searched in vain for a bullet. Alexander Graham Bell invented a metal detector to try to find it. Railroad workers and volunteers built a half-mile rail spur overnight to bring him to the oceanfront. And on September 19, 1881, the 20th President of the United States died at the Francklyn Cottage in Elberon, Long Branch, New Jersey.

This is a story of assassination, medical malpractice, technological innovation, community heroism, and a nation watching a president die in real time via telegraph.

---

## Part 1: Garfield Before the Shooting

### Early Life
- Born November 19, 1831, in Orange Township, Cuyahoga County, Ohio
- Raised in poverty after his father died when James was two
- Self-educated; worked as a canal boat driver, then teacher
- Rose to become a college president (Hiram College), Civil War general, and congressman

### The 1880 Election
- Garfield was a **dark horse candidate** -- he wasn't even running when the Republican convention began
- Won on the **36th ballot** after delegates deadlocked between Grant (seeking a third term) and James G. Blaine
- His running mate, **Chester A. Arthur**, was a Stalwart (the patronage-supporting faction) chosen to balance the ticket

### The Patronage Crisis
- The Republican Party was bitterly divided between **Stalwarts** (pro-patronage, led by Senator Roscoe Conkling of New York) and **Half-Breeds** (reform-minded, led by James G. Blaine)
- Garfield's presidency was consumed by battles over government appointments
- This factional warfare would directly lead to his assassination

## Part 2: Garfield's Connection to Long Branch Before the Shooting

### Garfield's Love of the Sea
From Garfield's diary, **June 19, 1881:** **"I have always felt the ocean was my friend and the sight of it brings rest and peace."**

### Lucretia's Illness
- First Lady **Lucretia Garfield** contracted malaria at the White House in the spring of 1881.
- On the advice of physicians, Garfield brought her to the **Elberon Hotel** in mid-June 1881 to recuperate.
- She arrived on **June 18, 1881**.

### Garfield's Visit
- Garfield himself visited Elberon on **June 26, 1881**, attending services at **St. James Church on Broadway** (later the Church of the Presidents) -- a bronze tablet marks his pew.
- From his diary that Sunday: "Passed a restful day with manifest betterment in her strength... The work and worry of Washington seem very far away and I rest in the large silence of the sea air."

### The Tension with Grant
- According to NJ Monthly, ex-president Grant reportedly **ignored Garfield** in the Elberon Hotel dining room during this visit -- a possible reflection of the Stalwart-Half-Breed divide.

## Part 3: The Assassination -- July 2, 1881

### The Morning
- Garfield was scheduled to leave Washington for his summer vacation, departing from the **Baltimore and Potomac Railroad Station** (located where the National Gallery of Art now stands on the National Mall).
- His travel plans had been published in the Washington newspapers.

### Charles J. Guiteau: The Assassin

**Background:**
- Born in Freeport, Illinois, the fourth of six children
- His mother reportedly suffered from psychosis; Charles may have inherited mental illness
- Father was affiliated with the **Oneida Community**, a utopian religious sect in New York
- In 1860, Guiteau joined the Oneida Community but was rejected socially -- the women created the nickname **"Charles Gitout"**
- Left Oneida, attempted to start a newspaper (*The Daily Theocrat*) in Hoboken, NJ -- it failed
- Returned to Oneida, then left again and sued the community
- Failed as a lawyer, a bill collector, and a theologian

**Political Delusions:**
- During the 1880 campaign, Guiteau wrote a speech called "Grant against Hancock," which he revised to **"Garfield against Hancock"** when Garfield won the nomination
- He distributed copies at campaign events, then delusionally believed he had been personally responsible for Garfield's victory
- He demanded appointment as **U.S. Consul to Paris** as his reward
- Repeatedly visited the White House and State Department to press his claim
- **Secretary of State James G. Blaine** finally snapped: **"Never speak to me again on the Paris consulship as long as you live!"**

**The Decision to Kill:**
- Guiteau convinced himself that Garfield was "destroying the Republican Party" by fighting the patronage system
- He believed killing Garfield would elevate Vice President Chester Arthur (a Stalwart) and restore patronage
- He purchased a **British Bull Dog revolver** (choosing the ivory-handled version because he thought it would look better in a museum)
- He stalked Garfield for weeks, including once at a church service and once at a train station (where he chose not to shoot because Mrs. Garfield was present)

### The Shooting
- **Time:** 9:30 AM, Saturday, July 2, 1881
- **Location:** Ladies' waiting room of the Baltimore and Potomac Railroad Station, Washington, D.C.
- **What happened:** Guiteau approached from behind and fired two shots
  - First shot: Grazed Garfield's arm
  - Second shot: Entered his back, passing through the first lumbar vertebra and lodging behind the pancreas
- Garfield collapsed, exclaiming: **"My God, what is this?"**
- Guiteau was immediately seized by bystanders. He said: **"I am a Stalwart of the Stalwarts! Arthur is now President!"**
- He had already engaged a cab to take him to jail and written a letter explaining his actions.

## Part 4: The Agonizing Summer -- 79 Days

### The Medical Disaster

What followed was one of the great medical catastrophes in American history:

- Garfield was carried to the White House, where a parade of doctors examined him.
- **None sterilized their hands or instruments.** Germ theory was still gaining acceptance; Joseph Lister's antiseptic techniques were widely dismissed in America.
- Doctors repeatedly **probed the wound with unwashed fingers and unsterilized instruments**, trying to locate the bullet.
- **Dr. Doctor Willard Bliss** (yes, his first name was "Doctor") became the lead physician and jealously guarded control of the case.
- At one point, doctors made a **20-inch incision** to search for the bullet.
- The bullet had actually missed vital organs, but the infections introduced by the doctors' probing caused massive abscesses, blood poisoning, and sepsis.
- Garfield lost over 100 pounds during his ordeal, going from about 210 lbs to under 130 lbs.

### Alexander Graham Bell's Metal Detector

One of the most dramatic subplots of the Garfield story:

**The Invention:**
- Alexander Graham Bell, famous for inventing the telephone, developed an **"induction balance" device** -- essentially the world's first metal detector.
- Based on earlier work by his friend and fellow inventor **David Hughes**, Bell discovered that metal objects near the device caused a sound in a telephone receiver.
- Working with assistant **William Tainter**, Bell assembled the device in just days.
- He tested it on **Civil War veterans who still had bullets in them**, on wooden boards, and on animal carcasses.

**First Attempt -- July 26:**
- Bell visited the White House to try the device on Garfield.
- A new condenser he'd added caused interference in the sound receiver, rendering results unreliable.
- The attempt failed.

**Second Attempt -- August 1:**
- Bell returned after laboratory refinements.
- **Problem 1:** Dr. Bliss was "convinced that the bullet was located somewhere on the right side of Garfield's torso, and refused to entertain any notions to the contrary." He only allowed Bell to scan the right side.
- **Problem 2:** Bell suspected that the **metal springs of Garfield's mattress** may have interfered with the readings.
- The attempt appeared to fail again.

**The Autopsy Revelation:**
- When Garfield's body was autopsied, **the bullet was found on the left side** of his chest -- exactly opposite where Dr. Bliss had insisted it was.
- Bell's device had been working correctly; it was the search location that was wrong.
- An improved version of Bell's induction balance later **saved countless lives on battlefields** in the decades before X-rays came into general use.

## Part 5: The Journey to Long Branch -- September 5-6, 1881

### The Decision
- By late August, Garfield's doctors concluded that the sweltering Washington heat was killing him.
- They proposed moving him to the Jersey Shore, where the ocean air might aid recovery.
- The Elberon area was chosen because the Garfield family already had connections there.

### The Overnight Railroad

This is one of the most extraordinary feats of community effort in American presidential history:

**The Problem:**
- Doctors feared Garfield could not tolerate a horse-drawn carriage ride from the Elberon train station to the oceanfront.
- The solution: build a railroad spur directly from the station to the cottage door.

**The Construction:**
- On **September 5, 1881**, word spread that the president was coming.
- Railroad workers and **citizen volunteers** began laying approximately **3,200 feet (about half a mile)** of temporary track, running down what is now **South Lincoln Avenue** from the Elberon station to the oceanfront.
- They worked **through the night** by torchlight.
- The Elberon Hotel sent wagons of sandwiches.
- Volunteers poured gallons of lemonade for the sweating workers.
- Residents set up refreshment tables along the route.
- **The track was completed with time to spare.**

**The Arrival:**
- The presidential train arrived the evening of **September 6, 1881**.
- When the locomotive reached a sharp curve near the cottage, it proved too tight for the engine.
- **The crowd pushed the president's train cars around the bend by hand** to the front of the house.
- More than twenty men gently rolled the car to the cottage.
- As Garfield was carried into the **Francklyn Cottage**, he reportedly sighed: **"Thank God, it is good to be here."**

### The Francklyn Cottage

**Owner:** **Charles G. Francklyn**, a shipping executive. His maternal grandfather was **Samuel Cunard**, the Canadian shipping magnate who founded the Cunard Line. Francklyn had served as Cunard Line's agent in New York.

**The Cottage:**
- A **20-room mansion** designed by **Charles F. McKim** (of McKim, Mead & White)
- Located on the oceanfront adjacent to the Elberon Hotel
- Offered to the Garfield family by Francklyn for the president's recovery

**Later fate:**
- The Francklyn Cottage **burned down on June 14, 1920** (some sources say 1914).
- A granite marker was erected on the site in 1961 (see below).

## Part 6: Death at Long Branch -- September 19, 1881

### The Final Days
- Despite the fresh ocean air, Garfield's condition continued to deteriorate.
- The infections and abscesses from weeks of unsanitary medical probing had taken their toll.
- He suffered from sepsis and bronchial pneumonia.

### The Death
- **Time:** 10:35 PM, Monday, September 19, 1881
- **Location:** Francklyn Cottage, Elberon, Long Branch, New Jersey
- **Cause:** Ruptured splenic artery aneurysm, following sepsis and bronchial pneumonia
- **Age:** 49 (exactly two months before his 50th birthday)
- He had survived **79 days** after the shooting.

### The Immediate Aftermath
- Vice President **Chester A. Arthur** was sworn in as the 21st President at approximately **2:15 AM on September 20** at his private residence in New York City.
- Arthur traveled to Elberon with **Secretary of State James G. Blaine** and **Secretary of War Robert Todd Lincoln** (Abraham Lincoln's son) to visit Mrs. Garfield. Their meeting was described as "affecting."
- A more formal swearing-in ceremony was held in Washington on September 22.

### Guiteau's Trial and Execution
- Guiteau's trial began in November 1881 and lasted until January 1882.
- His defense was insanity; he insisted God had commanded him to kill Garfield.
- He was found guilty on January 25, 1882.
- **Executed by hanging on June 30, 1882**, at the D.C. jail.
- At the gallows, he recited a poem he had composed for the occasion: "I am going to the Lordy."

## Part 7: Memorials and Legacy at Long Branch

### The Garfield Death Site Marker
- **Location:** Garfield Road, off Ocean Avenue, Elberon section of Long Branch
- **Description:** A granite marker on a narrow strip of lawn between Garfield Road and a residence's garage
- **Inscription:** "JAMES A. GARFIELD / Twentieth president of the United States / Born Nov. 19, 1831 at Orange, Ohio / Died on this site, Sept. 19, 1881"
- **Origin:** In 1957, an **8-year-old boy named Bruce Frankel** of Asbury Park began a campaign to memorialize the site. The marker was erected in **1961**. Frankel later became a lawyer in Fort Myers, Florida.

### The Garfield Tea House
- **What it is:** A gazebo-like structure built from the actual railroad ties used in the emergency rail spur of September 1881.
- After Garfield's death, the tracks were torn up and the ties purchased by actor **Oliver Doud Byron**.
- Byron hired carpenter **William Presley** to construct the tea house.
- **One of the original iron rails serves as the ridgepole** supporting the roof.
- **Current location:** 1260 Ocean Avenue, on the Church of the Presidents museum grounds.
- **Planned move (2026):** Slated to be relocated to a new library park in Elberon as part of $500,000 in improvements funded by Congressman Frank Pallone.

### The Garfield Statue at Seven Presidents Park
- A statue of President Garfield stands at the Boardwalk at the end of Laird Street, surrounded by bronze tablets commemorating the other six presidents.

### The Church of the Presidents
- A bronze tablet marks Garfield's pew at St. James Chapel (Church of the Presidents) on Ocean Avenue.

## Key Narrative Threads for the Documentary

### 1. The Medical Malpractice Story
- Modern doctors agree the bullet wound itself was **not fatal**. The bullet missed the spine and major organs.
- It was the **doctors who killed Garfield** through unsanitary probing and treatment.
- This became a major catalyst for the acceptance of **antiseptic surgical techniques** in America.
- At Guiteau's trial, his lawyers argued: "The doctors killed Garfield; I just shot him."

### 2. The Bell Metal Detector
- Alexander Graham Bell, just five years after inventing the telephone, created another revolutionary device to try to save a president.
- The device worked -- it was the doctor's stubbornness about the bullet's location that prevented success.
- The technology went on to save lives on battlefields for decades.

### 3. The Community Railroad
- An entire community mobilizing overnight to build a railroad track to save their president -- the torchlight, the sandwiches, the lemonade, the crowd pushing the train by hand.
- This is the single most cinematic moment in the entire Seven Presidents story.

### 4. The Civil Service Legacy
- Garfield's assassination by a "disappointed office seeker" outraged the nation and led directly to the **Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act of 1883**, which created the modern merit-based civil service system.
- Garfield's death accomplished what he could not achieve in life.

### 5. The "Thank God" Moment
- The dying president, carried into a seaside cottage after an extraordinary community effort, whispering "Thank God, it is good to be here" -- knowing, perhaps, that he was going to die.
