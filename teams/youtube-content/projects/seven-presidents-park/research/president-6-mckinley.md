# President 6: William McKinley (1897-1901)
## The Flag Speech, the Dying Friend, and the Second Assassination

---

## Overview

William McKinley's connection to Long Branch centers on a single, well-documented visit in August 1899 -- a visit that intertwined patriotic oratory with personal tragedy. He came to deliver a rousing speech at Ocean Grove's Great Auditorium and to visit his gravely ill Vice President, Garrett Hobart, who was dying at his summer home in West Long Branch. Two years later, McKinley himself would be assassinated -- the second presidential assassination connected to the Seven Presidents story (after Garfield), adding a haunting symmetry to Long Branch's history.

---

## The Ocean Grove Speech: August 25, 1899

### The Setting
- **Location:** The Great Auditorium, Ocean Grove, New Jersey
- **Date:** Friday, August 25, 1899
- **Context:** The United States had just concluded the **Spanish-American War** (1898), acquiring Puerto Rico, the Philippines, and Guam. The country was debating its new role as an imperial power.

### The Arrival
- McKinley arrived via a **presidential parade through the streets of Asbury Park** before entering the Ocean Grove auditorium.
- Ocean Grove was a Methodist camp-meeting community -- the Great Auditorium was (and remains) one of the largest enclosed auditoriums in the country.

### The Speech
McKinley gave a patriotic address about the meaning of the American flag in the wake of the Spanish-American War:

> **"Wherever that flag is raised, it stands not for despotism but for liberty, opportunity and humanity."**

He also addressed the controversy over territorial expansion:

> "It does not mean one thing in the United States, and another thing in Puerto Rico and the Philippines."

McKinley was deeply moved by the reception:

> **"I was simply overwhelmed when I first came into that auditorium. It was one of the grandest ovations I ever saw."**

### Significance
- This was McKinley's **only known speech** in the Monmouth County area during his presidency.
- The speech was significant in the national debate over American imperialism.
- Ocean Grove's religious character lent moral weight to McKinley's defense of expansion.

## The Visit to Vice President Garrett Hobart

### Hobart's Connection to the Area
- **Garrett Augustus Hobart** (1844-1899) was McKinley's Vice President and one of the most influential VPs in history up to that point.
- Hobart was a **New Jersey native** who had served as president of the NJ Senate.
- He maintained a summer home called **"Normanhurst"** in the **Norwood Park neighborhood of West Long Branch**.
- He was nicknamed "the Assistant President" for his unusual level of influence in the McKinley administration.

### The Illness
- In 1899, Hobart was diagnosed with **Bright's disease** (a kidney ailment).
- His condition deteriorated rapidly during the summer.
- McKinley combined his Ocean Grove speech with a personal visit to the ailing Hobart at Normanhurst.

### Hobart's Death
- Hobart died on **November 21, 1899**, at age 55.
- He was the sixth Vice President to die in office.
- His death created the vacancy that was filled by **Theodore Roosevelt** on the 1900 ticket -- a decision that would have enormous consequences when McKinley was assassinated.

---

## McKinley's Assassination: September 6, 1901

### The Shooting
- On **September 6, 1901**, McKinley was shot at the **Pan-American Exposition** in Buffalo, New York.
- The assassin was **Leon Czolgosz** (pronounced "CHOL-gosh"), a 28-year-old anarchist.
- McKinley was shaking hands with the public in the Temple of Music when Czolgosz approached with a pistol concealed under a handkerchief and shot him twice.

### The Death
- McKinley initially seemed to recover, but gangrene set in.
- He died on **September 14, 1901**, at age 58.
- **Theodore Roosevelt** became the 26th President.

### National Mourning
- McKinley's assassination prompted a worldwide outpouring of grief.
- British King Edward VII and other monarchs declared national periods of mourning.
- His body lay in state at the Capitol Rotunda on September 17.
- Cities ground to a halt as the funeral train passed on its way to Canton, Ohio.

### Long Branch Connection to the Mourning
- While no specific documentation of Long Branch's mourning activities has been found, the community that had hosted McKinley just two years earlier would have felt a particular connection to the tragedy.
- McKinley was the **second president assassinated** who was connected to the Seven Presidents story -- Garfield had died at Long Branch just 20 years earlier.
- The parallel between the two assassinations is striking: both presidents lingered for days after being shot, and both died from infections rather than the direct effects of the bullets.

## McKinley's Presidency: Key Context

### Background
- Born January 29, 1843, in Niles, Ohio
- Civil War veteran; enlisted as a private, mustered out as a brevet major
- Served 14 years in the U.S. House of Representatives from Ohio
- Governor of Ohio (1892-1896)
- Won the 1896 presidential election decisively over William Jennings Bryan

### Major Events
- **Spanish-American War (1898):** "A splendid little war" that lasted just 10 weeks. The U.S. acquired Puerto Rico, the Philippines, and Guam; Cuba gained nominal independence.
- **Economic prosperity:** McKinley presided over recovery from the Depression of 1893.
- **Gold Standard Act (1900):** Established gold as the sole basis for U.S. currency.
- **Philippine-American War (1899-1902):** The brutal suppression of Filipino independence fighters became a major controversy.
- Won **re-election in 1900** (again defeating Bryan), with Theodore Roosevelt as his new VP.

## Key Narrative Threads for the Documentary

### 1. The Flag Speech
- McKinley defending American imperialism in a Methodist auditorium at the Jersey Shore -- the intersection of religion, patriotism, and geopolitics.
- The quote about the flag is strong visual/audio material.

### 2. The Dying Vice President
- McKinley visiting Hobart at Normanhurst while Hobart was dying.
- Hobart's death opened the door for Theodore Roosevelt -- one of history's great "what if" moments.
- If Hobart had lived, TR would not have been VP, and the assassination of McKinley would have elevated someone else entirely.

### 3. The Two Assassinations
- Garfield (1881) and McKinley (1901): Two presidents connected to Long Branch, both assassinated, both dying from infection rather than from the bullets themselves.
- The 20-year bookends of the Seven Presidents era.
- Both assassins (Guiteau and Czolgosz) were mentally unstable individuals acting on radical beliefs.

### 4. The End of an Era
- McKinley's assassination in 1901 marked the effective end of the Gilded Age.
- Theodore Roosevelt's ascension to the presidency launched the Progressive Era.
- Long Branch's presidential resort days were numbered.
