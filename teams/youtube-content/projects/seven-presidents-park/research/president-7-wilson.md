# President 7: Woodrow Wilson (1913-1921)
## Shadow Lawn, the "Summer White House," and the Last Presidential Chapter

---

## Overview

Woodrow Wilson is the final president in the Seven Presidents narrative, and along with Grant, he is one of only two presidents who spent **entire summers** in the Long Branch area. His story is centered on **Shadow Lawn**, the 52-room mansion in West Long Branch that served as his Summer White House in 1916 during one of the most consequential presidential campaigns in American history. Wilson's connection to New Jersey runs far deeper than a summer vacation -- he was a Princeton professor, university president, and New Jersey governor before reaching the White House.

---

## Wilson's Deep New Jersey Roots

### Princeton Connection
- Wilson attended the **College of New Jersey** (later Princeton University) as a student.
- Returned as a **professor** in 1890.
- Named **president of Princeton University** in 1902 -- served until 1910.
- His tenure at Princeton was marked by both academic innovation and bitter political battles over the university's social clubs and graduate school.

### Governor of New Jersey (1910-1912)
- Elected governor in November 1910 as a reform Democrat.
- Quickly established a reputation as an independent progressive, breaking with the party bosses who had recruited him.
- His reform achievements as governor became his springboard to the presidency.

### Sea Girt Connection (1912)
- As governor, Wilson used the **governor's summer residence at Sea Girt**, NJ.
- In **August 1912**, Wilson awaited the formal Democratic nomination notification at Sea Girt.
- Sea Girt is about 15 miles south of Long Branch.

---

## Shadow Lawn: The Summer White House

### The Estate's History

**Original Construction:**
- The original Shadow Lawn mansion was built in the early 1900s by **John A. McCall**, president of the **New York Life Insurance Company**.
- McCall's tenure at New York Life ended in scandal when he was forced to resign over financial irregularities; he died shortly after.
- The estate was located at **Cedar and Norwood Avenues** in what is now West Long Branch (then considered part of the Long Branch area).

**The 52-Room Mansion:**
- The original house was a sprawling **52-room mansion** set on expansive grounds.
- It included the grandeur expected of Gilded Age estates: formal gardens, carriage houses, and oceanfront views.

### Wilson's Use of Shadow Lawn (1916)

**Arrival:**
- President Wilson arrived at Shadow Lawn on **September 1, 1916**.
- He occupied the mansion as both his summer residence and his **re-election campaign headquarters**.
- The mansion was officially designated the **"Summer White House."**

**The Acceptance Speech -- September 2, 1916:**
- One day after arriving, Wilson delivered his **formal acceptance of the Democratic nomination** at Shadow Lawn.
- **Senator Ollie M. James** delivered the speech of notification.
- A large crowd gathered on the front lawn of the mansion.
- Wilson spelled out his first-term achievements and reiterated the country's position of neutrality in World War I.
- He warned Germany that submarine warfare taking American lives "would not be tolerated" -- carefully avoiding an absolute promise of neutrality.

**The "He Kept Us Out of War" Campaign:**
- The Democrats' 1916 campaign slogan was **"He Kept Us Out of War"** -- a reference to Wilson's maintenance of American neutrality since the outbreak of World War I in July 1914.
- During the convention, delegates cheered this new slogan, which "world conditions made more of a hope than a promise."
- Wilson's campaign also used the slogan **"America First."**
- Campaign advisor **Edward M. House** managed the entire campaign structure and strategy from Shadow Lawn.

**The Front Porch Campaign:**
- Wilson ran a "front porch campaign" from Shadow Lawn, receiving delegations and making speeches from the mansion's steps.
- This strategy earned Shadow Lawn its famous nickname and cemented its place in presidential history.
- Wilson stayed at Shadow Lawn for **approximately six weeks**, from September 1 to November 8, 1916.

**Church Attendance:**
- Wilson attended **St. James Church on Broadway** (Church of the Presidents) on **October 9, 1916**.
- A bronze plaque marks his pew -- one of two presidential plaques in the church (the other is Garfield's).

### The 1916 Election Results
- Wilson won re-election in one of the **closest elections in American history**.
- He defeated Republican **Charles Evans Hughes** (a Supreme Court Justice).
- **Popular vote:** Wilson won by nearly 600,000 votes (52% to 46%).
- **Electoral College:** Wilson won 277-254.
- The key moment came on election night when Hughes went to bed believing he had won. The next morning, returns from California (which Wilson won by just 3,773 votes) tipped the election.
- The irony: Wilson's "He Kept Us Out of War" slogan became painfully hollow. In **April 1917**, just five months after his re-election, Wilson asked Congress to declare war on Germany.

### Wilson's Departure
- **November 9, 1916:** Wilson left Shadow Lawn and **never returned**.

---

## The Fate of Shadow Lawn

### Post-Wilson Ownership
- In **1918**, the estate was purchased by **Hubert T. Parson**, president of the **F.W. Woolworth Company** (the five-and-dime retail empire).

### The 1927 Fire
- In **January 1927**, the original 52-room mansion **burned to the ground**.
- The loss was estimated at **$1,250,000**.
- The flames were reportedly **visible for 20 miles**.

### The Replacement Mansion
- Parson and his wife Maysie immediately commissioned a replacement.
- They hired **Horace Trumbauer**, a noted Gilded Age architect.
- The design is credited to Trumbauer and his assistant, **Julian Abele** -- one of the first prominent African American architects in the United States.
- The new mansion was completed in **1929** in an opulent Beaux-Arts style.

### Monmouth University
- The Shadow Lawn estate eventually became the campus of **Monmouth University** (originally Monmouth Junior College, founded 1933).
- The replacement mansion became the centerpiece of the campus.
- It was renamed **Woodrow Wilson Hall** in honor of Wilson's use of the original building.

### The 2020 Renaming
- In **2020**, Monmouth University renamed the building to **"The Great Hall at Shadow Lawn."**
- The university cited **Wilson's well-documented racist policies** as the reason for removing his name.
- During Wilson's presidency, he had segregated the federal workforce, screened the pro-KKK film *The Birth of a Nation* at the White House, and made other decisions reflecting his white supremacist views.
- The renaming reflected a broader national reckoning with Wilson's racial legacy that also led Princeton to remove his name from its School of Public and International Affairs.

## Wilson's Presidency: Key Context

### First Term (1913-1917)
- **Federal Reserve Act (1913):** Created the Federal Reserve System.
- **Clayton Antitrust Act (1914):** Strengthened antitrust enforcement.
- **Federal Trade Commission (1914):** Created the FTC.
- **Federal Income Tax:** The 16th Amendment had been ratified; Wilson signed the Revenue Act of 1913.
- **World War I neutrality:** Maintained American neutrality despite German submarine warfare and the sinking of the Lusitania (1915).

### Second Term (1917-1921)
- **U.S. entry into WWI (April 1917):** Wilson asked Congress for a declaration of war, just months after campaigning on neutrality.
- **Fourteen Points (January 1918):** Wilson's vision for post-war peace.
- **Treaty of Versailles (1919):** Wilson personally negotiated in Paris; included the League of Nations.
- **League of Nations fight:** The Senate rejected the Treaty; Wilson campaigned nationally for it.
- **Stroke (October 1919):** Wilson suffered a severe stroke that left him partially paralyzed. His wife Edith effectively managed the presidency for the remainder of his term -- sometimes called "the first woman president."
- Wilson died on **February 3, 1924**, at age 67.

## Key Narrative Threads for the Documentary

### 1. The Shadow Lawn Campaign
- A presidential election campaign run from a mansion at the Jersey Shore.
- The "front porch" strategy adapted for the beach.
- The irony of "He Kept Us Out of War" -- a campaign promise that became a lie within months.

### 2. Wilson's New Jersey Identity
- Unlike the other six presidents, Wilson was deeply rooted in New Jersey.
- Princeton student, professor, university president, governor, then president.
- Shadow Lawn was not a vacation destination for Wilson -- New Jersey was his home state.

### 3. The Fire and the Legacy Building
- The original mansion where Wilson campaigned burned in 1927 -- just like the Francklyn Cottage where Garfield died (burned 1914/1920).
- Long Branch's presidential history kept being consumed by flames.
- The replacement was designed partly by Julian Abele, an African American architect -- an ironic twist given Wilson's racism.

### 4. The Renaming Controversy
- The 2020 removal of Wilson's name from the building reflects how we continually re-evaluate historical figures.
- Wilson: the president who created the Federal Reserve, won a world war, and championed the League of Nations -- but also a virulent racist.
- This is a powerful closing theme for the Seven Presidents series: how we remember, honor, and reconsider the past.

### 5. The End of the Presidential Shore Era
- Wilson was the last president to use the Long Branch area as a summer capital.
- After Wilson, presidential summer retreats shifted to Camp David, the Western White House, Kennebunkport, etc.
- Wilson's departure from Shadow Lawn in November 1916 -- never to return -- symbolically marks the end of Long Branch's presidential era.
