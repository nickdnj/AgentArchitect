# Seven Presidents Park & Long Branch: America's Summer Capital

## Complete Overview for Documentary Series

---

## Part 1: Seven Presidents Oceanfront Park

### Establishment and Naming

Seven Presidents Oceanfront Park is a 38-acre beachfront park located at 221 Ocean Avenue, Long Branch, New Jersey 07740. It is maintained by the Monmouth County Park System.

- **1974:** The city of Long Branch acquired the land (formerly "The Reservation," see below) via condemnation, paying $3.6 million and demolishing the remaining houses.
- **1976:** Monmouth County and Long Branch received approval from the State EPA for a 35-acre county-run oceanfront park.
- **1977:** Seven Presidents Oceanfront Park opened.
- **May 1984:** The present-day beachfront park was formally dedicated with a 4,700-foot shoreline. Total project cost: $2 million, mostly funded by Green Acres state grants.

The park is named after the seven U.S. Presidents who vacationed in or visited the Long Branch area during the Gilded Age:

1. **Ulysses S. Grant** (18th President, 1869-1877)
2. **Rutherford B. Hayes** (19th President, 1877-1881)
3. **James A. Garfield** (20th President, 1881)
4. **Chester A. Arthur** (21st President, 1881-1885)
5. **Benjamin Harrison** (23rd President, 1889-1893)
6. **William McKinley** (25th President, 1897-1901)
7. **Woodrow Wilson** (28th President, 1913-1921)

### What Was the Land Before?

The park sits on land that was once **"The Reservation"** -- a beachfront cottage colony built in 1900 by **Nathan "Nate" Salsbury**, the business manager and co-owner of **Buffalo Bill's Wild West Show**.

- Salsbury spent $200,000 in 1900 to build nine Queen Anne and Shingle-style cottages on what was described as "sandy waste near North Branch."
- Each cottage was named after a Native American person or tribe -- a nod to the "Show Indians" in Buffalo Bill's performances.
- Notable performers connected to the show included **Buffalo Bill Cody**, **Annie Oakley**, and **Chief Sitting Bull**.
- Distinguished summer residents later included **George B. McClellan Jr.**, mayor of New York City.
- Salsbury died in December 1902 at age 57; his daughter Rachael sold the houses in January 1920.
- By the early 1970s, most houses had fallen into disrepair. The city condemned and acquired the property in 1974.

### The Park Today

**Facilities:**
- Year-round Skateplex (weather-permitting) for skateboarders and inline skaters
- Tony's Place -- an accessible playground for people of all abilities
- Summer: snack bar, showers, changing area, sand volleyball courts
- Guarded swimming areas
- Designated zones for surfing, fishing, and launching watercraft
- 4,700-foot shoreline

**Hours (as of 2026):** Opens at 8 AM; closes at 8 PM (seasonal variation)

**Contact:** 732-229-0924 (Seasonal Pavilion) / 732-229-7025 (Park Rangers)

### Monuments, Plaques, and Markers

- **Garfield Statue:** A statue of President Garfield stands at the Boardwalk at the end of Laird Street.
- **Six Bronze Tablets:** Surrounding the Garfield statue are bronze tablets commemorating the other six presidents: Grant, Hayes, Arthur, Harrison, McKinley, and Wilson.
- **Historical Registry:** The park itself is not separately listed on the National Register, but the broader Long Branch presidential history sites have various designations.

## Part 2: Long Branch -- "America's Summer Capital"

### Origins as a Resort

Long Branch's development as a summer coastal resort began in the **1780s**. By the **1830s**, gambling and horse racing had already begun bringing an influx of visitors. The town was accessible by steamboat from New York City, making it an attractive escape for the urban elite.

**Key early milestones:**
- **1861:** First Lady **Mary Todd Lincoln** visited Long Branch on the recommendation of the Lincoln family physician, significantly boosting the town's prominence.
- **1860:** The first railroad service reached Long Branch via the Raritan & Delaware Bay Railroad (later the NJ Southern Railroad).
- **1863:** The Long Branch & Sea-Shore Railroad was incorporated, connecting to steamers at Sandy Hook.
- **1867:** General Grant first visited Long Branch and declared it an ideal summer residence.
- **1869:** Wealthy citizens gifted President Grant a cottage, and he declared Long Branch the nation's **"Summer Capital."**

### The Role of Railroads

The railroads transformed Long Branch from an exclusive retreat into a nationally significant destination:

- **1860:** First rail service via the Raritan & Delaware Bay Railroad.
- **1863:** Long Branch & Sea-Shore Railroad incorporated; first tracks from Long Branch to Sandy Hook, connecting with steamers.
- **1868:** A round trip from New York to Long Branch (steamship + train, 1 hour 45 minutes one-way) cost $2.
- **1875:** The **New York and Long Branch Railroad (NY&LB)** opened from Perth Amboy, providing the "all-rail route" via the Central Railroad of New Jersey. Built primarily as a tourist line for Jersey Shore beach resorts.
- **1889:** Monthly passes cost $24.

The railroads were a double-edged sword: they brought the elite, but eventually also brought the "day-tripper of limited means," beginning in the 1870s. This democratization of access would gradually erode Long Branch's exclusivity.

### The Great Hotels

Long Branch was sometimes called "Hotel City" -- researchers have identified over **150 hotels, motels, boarding houses, and inns**.

**The Continental Hotel:**
- Completed in 1867; a combination of three buildings with an oceanfront piazza stretching more than half a mile.
- Could accommodate 1,000 guests; employed 28 cooks and 150 waiters.
- The Leland family took control in 1872 and made it a major success.
- Closed in 1903; the city converted the area into a public park in 1906.

**The Elberon Hotel:**
- Built by Lewis B. Brown in 1876 in the newly developed Elberon section.
- Became the smartest and most exclusive hotel in the area.
- Hosted presidents and heads of state.
- Former US Senator James Smith Jr. (D-NJ) bought the property in 1904 for $87,000.
- The Garfield family stayed here before the assassination.

**The Hollywood Hotel:**
- One of the "lost Long Branch lodging legends."
- Referenced in photographs from 1908.

**The Stetson Hotel:**
- Where the Grant family stayed before receiving their cottage.

### Elberon: "The Playland of Millionaires"

The Elberon neighborhood was the crown jewel of Long Branch's resort era.

**Development:**
- **Lewis B. Brown** (born Rahway, NJ, December 1813) made his fortune in the dry goods business in the South. He was an ardent abolitionist.
- In **1866-1867**, Brown acquired approximately 400 acres and created "Brown's Park," later renamed Elberon (a contraction of "L.B. Brown").
- He built Ocean Avenue and the grand Elberon Hotel.
- Brown was appointed to the inaugural Long Branch Commission in 1867 -- the first municipal government in the area.
- He died in Elberon in June 1900.

**Architecture:**
- The famed NYC architectural firm of **McKim, Mead & White** essentially got its start in Elberon, designing many of the massive seaside mansions.
- Their work included the **Victor Newcomb house** (1880-1881), considered a brilliant early Shingle Style design.
- Stanford White designed **Golden Crest Estate** for the president of First National Bank of Jersey City.
- The **Francklyn Cottage** (where Garfield died) was designed by Charles F. McKim.

**Famous Residents and Visitors:**
- Presidents Grant, Hayes, Garfield, Arthur, Harrison, McKinley, Wilson
- **Lillian Russell** (actress)
- **Lily Langtry** (actress, "The Jersey Lily")
- **Diamond Jim Brady** (financier, legendary bon vivant)
- **Edwin Booth** (actor, brother of John Wilkes Booth)
- **Jay Gould** (railroad magnate)
- **George Pullman** (industrialist, railroad sleeping car inventor)
- **George W. Childs** (Philadelphia newspaper publisher)
- **Frederick Douglass** (abolitionist leader)
- **General Winfield Scott**
- **Dan Rice** (circus performer)
- **Nate Salsbury** (Buffalo Bill's business partner)

### The Gambling Era: "Monte Carlo of America"

Long Branch had a notorious gambling culture that ran parallel to its genteel presidential image.

- **Col. John Chamberlain and Phil Daly** operated a gambling house at the corner of Brighton and Ocean Avenues, opened in the late 1860s.
- **Phil Daly** took over in 1877 and ran the **Pennsylvania Club** in West End, which the Asbury Park Press called "easily the highest class and most exclusive gambling club in the United States" (1910).
- The Pennsylvania Club was known as the **"Monte Carlo of America."**
- Games included roulette, faro, cards, and dice.
- Daly kept a French chef on staff and fed patrons for free (women and locals were not welcome).
- **1897:** Gambling was outlawed by the state.
- **1902:** The Pennsylvania Club was finally closed after a police raid.
- **1909:** The gilded-dome palace was demolished.
- **March 1910:** Phil Daly died.

### Monmouth Park Racetrack

Horse racing was central to Long Branch's entertainment scene:

- **July 30, 1870:** The original Monmouth Park opened in Eatontown to boost summer tourism. The effort was led by John F. Chamberlain, NJ Senate President Amos Robbins, and Adams Express Company president John Hoey.
- **President Grant** was a regular in his box at the track. The NJ Monthly article notes he was once fined $20 for speeding his horse-drawn carriage (though the famous arrest for speeding actually occurred in Washington, D.C.).
- The track achieved distinction as "The Newmarket of America" -- referencing England's famed racecourse.
- Financial difficulties closed the first track after three years.
- **1882:** A rebuilt Monmouth Park reopened under George Lorillard, D.D. Withers, and others.
- **1890:** A second, adjacent course opened.
- **March 21, 1894:** New Jersey banned wagering on horses, killing the track.

### The Jersey Shore Resort Hierarchy

In the 1800s, Jersey Shore towns occupied distinct social tiers:

- **Cape May:** The earliest elite resort; "Queen of the Seaside Resorts." Attracted the Philadelphia set. President Henry Clay visited in 1847. Five presidents vacationed there (Pierce, Buchanan, Lincoln, Grant, Harrison).
- **Long Branch:** Rivaled Newport, Rhode Island and Saratoga Springs, New York. Attracted the New York set. Home to seven presidential visitors. More glamorous and risque than Cape May.
- **Atlantic City:** Rose dramatically after rail access arrived. Surpassed Cape May in popularity by 1900. More democratic and commercial -- the boardwalk resort for the masses.
- **Asbury Park/Ocean Grove:** Religious camp-meeting origins. Ocean Grove's Great Auditorium hosted presidents McKinley and Theodore Roosevelt.

### The Church of the Presidents

The **Church of the Presidents** (originally St. James Episcopal Chapel) is one of Long Branch's most significant historical landmarks.

- **Built:** 1879, in the Carpenter Gothic / Shingle Style
- **Architects:** William Appleton Potter and Robert Henderson Robertson (NYC firm)
- **Imposing tower:** Added in 1895
- **Presidential worshippers:** Claimed to have been attended by all seven presidents associated with Long Branch (though scholarly research disputes some claims -- see "Historical Controversies" below)
- **Bronze tablets** mark the pews of Presidents Garfield and Wilson
- **1949:** Services ceased as the congregation dwindled to fewer than 40 members
- **1953:** Acquired by the Long Branch Historical Museum Association
- **1999:** Closed due to disrepair
- **2003:** Private donations helped stabilize the building
- **2024-2025:** Final phase of restoration underway with NJ Historic Trust and federal grants
- **Status (2026):** Closed during restoration; opens for special events

### The Decline of Long Branch's Resort Era

The decline was gradual and multi-causal:

1. **Late 1890s-1900s:** Anti-gambling legislation shut down the casinos (1897) and eventually the racetrack (1894). The Pennsylvania Club was demolished (1909).
2. **Early 1900s:** Long Branch lost its theatrical scene. The grand hotels began aging. Atlantic City emerged as the glamorous alternative.
3. **1920s-1930s:** The steamboat era ended in the late 1920s, devastating Pleasure Bay. The automobile changed vacation patterns.
4. **Post-World War II:** Long Branch's fame as "the Nation's First Seaside Resort" waned significantly. Suburban development drew people inland.
5. **1960s:** Civil unrest and riots in neighboring Asbury Park caused flight from shore cities.
6. **June 8, 1987: The Great Pier Fire.** A gas leak and faulty wiring at a McDonald's under the pier sparked a blaze. 30 mph winds fed the fire, destroying the landmark amusement pier, the Haunted Mansion, "Kid's World" Amusement Park, and other businesses. Estimated $8 million in damages. 200 jobs lost. Owners had little insurance and couldn't rebuild.
7. **2001:** The damaged pier remnants were finally torn down.
8. **2005:** **Pier Village** opened on the site, beginning Long Branch's modern renaissance.

### The Garfield Tea House

One of the most unique memorial structures in American presidential history:

- Built from the **railroad ties** used in the emergency rail spur constructed overnight to bring the dying President Garfield to the Francklyn Cottage in September 1881.
- After Garfield's death, the tracks were torn up and the wooden ties were purchased by actor **Oliver Doud Byron**.
- Byron hired local carpenter **William Presley** to build a gazebo-like tea house from the ties.
- **One of the original rails serves as the ridgepole** supporting the roof.
- Currently located at 1260 Ocean Avenue on the Church of the Presidents museum grounds.
- **Planned move (2026):** The Garfield Tea House is slated to be moved to a new library park in the Elberon section.

## Part 3: Historical Controversies

### The "Seven Presidents" Myth

The Monmouth Timeline website presents a well-researched challenge to the traditional narrative. Key findings:

**Strongly Documented Presidential Connections:**
- **Ulysses S. Grant:** Extensively documented. Summered in Long Branch from 1869 through the 1880s. Undisputed.
- **James A. Garfield:** Extensively documented. His wife stayed at Elberon Hotel; he died at Francklyn Cottage. Undisputed.
- **Woodrow Wilson:** Extensively documented. Used Shadow Lawn as Summer White House in 1916. Undisputed.
- **William McKinley:** Documented. Gave speech at Ocean Grove (1899); visited VP Hobart in West Long Branch.

**Weakly Documented Claims:**
- **Rutherford B. Hayes:** His diary shows he summered at the Soldiers' Home in Washington, D.C. No convincing evidence of any Monmouth County visit has been found. The Elberon Hotel claims about Hayes's visits boosted its prestige but may be apocryphal.
- **Chester A. Arthur:** He was an Elberon homeowner since 1874 (before his presidency), and visited Garfield's widow in September 1881. He summered at the Octagon House in Sea Bright in 1884. His connection is real but not as extensive as traditionally claimed.
- **Benjamin Harrison:** His primary Jersey Shore vacation spot was **Cape May**, not Long Branch. He stayed at the Elberon Club as a former president in 1898. Claims about his summer residency in Elberon rest on thin evidence from 1927 newspaper accounts.

**Origin of the "Church of the Presidents" Label:**
- St. James Chapel was consecrated in **1879** -- two years after Grant left office.
- The "Seven Presidents" designation originated from **financial desperation in 1925** when the chapel faced a $650 road assessment.
- Jewish businessman **Ernest Levy** donated funds to save the chapel. His attorney, **Bernard Sandler**, simultaneously announced the chapel had been attended by "six presidents" (adding Hayes and Arthur without evidence) and called it "the Westminster Abbey of America."
- By **1930**, Benjamin Harrison was added as the seventh, based on letters from "relatives."

> **[FACT-CHECK FLAG]:** The exact number of presidents who meaningfully visited Long Branch is debatable. Grant, Garfield, and Wilson are undisputed. McKinley's visit is well-documented but brief. Hayes, Arthur, and Harrison have varying levels of evidence. The traditional "seven" is a compelling narrative that has become accepted history, but the documentary evidence is uneven. For the documentary, this controversy itself is a compelling story angle.

## Part 4: Supporting Historical Context

### The Telegraph and Telephone

The telegraph made Long Branch viable as a summer capital. The **New York, Long Branch and Sandy Hook Telegraph Co.** operated lines connecting the shore to New York, allowing President Grant to receive official communications and dispatches. Messengers "constantly visited President Grant's ocean-bound villa with bundles of official papers."

### African American Community

- **Frederick Douglass** was among the notable visitors to Long Branch in the 1870s.
- The broader African American history of Monmouth County is documented in Graham Russell Gao Hodges' "Slavery and Freedom in the Rural North: African Americans in Monmouth County, New Jersey, 1660-1870."
- The resort economy created service employment for Black workers, though detailed documentation of this community in the Long Branch context is less well-preserved.

### Fort Monmouth

- Located about 10 minutes from Long Branch Beach.
- Established as a Signal Corps training facility in Little Silver in 1917; gained permanent status in 1925.
- Named for soldiers who died at the Battle of Monmouth Court House during the American Revolution.
- Built on the site of an old racetrack and luxury hotel -- remnants of the Gilded Age resort era.
- Played a massive role in WWII and the Cold War.
- Developed the first U.S. aircraft detection radar (1938).
- Final closing ceremony: September 15, 2011.
