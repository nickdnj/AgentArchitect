# Seven Presidents Documentary Series -- v2 Structure

## 5-Video Series

Restructured from the original 8-video plan. Hayes and Harrison have been cut as standalone episodes -- their connections to Long Branch are too weak to sustain dedicated videos. Their stories are adequately covered in the overview (Video 1, Segment 5: "The Disputed Presidents"). A new episode on the resort era replaces them, giving the series a broader cultural scope beyond just the presidents.

## Video 2: GRANT
### "The General's Summer White House"

**Status:** Stub outline. Research complete (see `research/president-1-grant.md`).
**Target Length:** 10-12 minutes
**Tone:** Grand, personal, bittersweet

**Key Story Arc:**
- The clever gift: wealthy friends buy a 28-room mansion for "Mrs. Grant" because the general refuses the gift himself. Julia accepts without hesitation.
- The Summer White House years: eight summers of cabinet meetings on the porch, poker Fridays with Pullman and Childs, carriage rides on Ocean Avenue, tourists walking past hoping to glimpse the president.
- Julia Grant's perspective: her memoirs reveal the cottage as the emotional center of the Grant marriage -- "What a boon our cottage at Long Branch was to the President!"
- The fall: Grant & Ward Ponzi scheme (1884) ruins the family. Broke and dying of throat cancer, Grant writes his memoirs at the cottage in a race against death. The memoirs earn Julia nearly $450,000. Mark Twain's role as publisher.
- The demolition: the cottage demolished in 1963 with little fanfare. LOC HABS photos from 1933 are all that remain. Today it is a grassy lot with luxury homes under construction.

**Link to overview:** The overview (Video 1) covers Grant's story in 90 seconds; this episode expands it to the full arc from 1867 to the demolition.

## Video 3: GARFIELD
### "The President Who Died at the Shore"

**Status:** Stub outline. Research complete (see `research/president-3-garfield.md`).
**Target Length:** 15-18 minutes
**Tone:** Dramatic, tragic, cinematic

**Key Story Arc:**
- The shooting: July 2, 1881. Charles Guiteau at the Baltimore and Potomac Railroad Station. A delusional failed lawyer who believed he won the election for Garfield and demanded the Paris consulship. The ivory-handled revolver he chose because "it would look better in a museum."
- The medical disaster: 79 days of doctors probing with unsterilized instruments. Dr. "Doctor" Willard Bliss (his first name was literally Doctor) and his fatal stubbornness. Garfield wasted from 210 pounds to 130.
- Alexander Graham Bell's metal detector: the induction balance, tested on Civil War veterans, brought to the White House. It worked -- but Bliss only let Bell scan the wrong side. The autopsy confirmed the bullet was on the left.
- The overnight railroad: September 5, 1881. 3,200 feet of track laid by torchlight. Sandwiches from the Elberon Hotel. Lemonade tables. The crowd pushing the train car around a bend by hand.
- "Thank God, it is good to be here." Thirteen days at the Francklyn Cottage. Death at 10:35 PM on September 19. Arthur sworn in at 2:15 AM with Blaine and Robert Todd Lincoln at his side.
- The legacy: "The doctors killed Garfield. I just shot him." -- Guiteau at trial. The Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act. The Garfield Tea House built from the railroad ties.

**Link to overview:** The overview intentionally keeps Garfield to ~60 seconds and nods to the Netflix show. This episode is where the full story lives -- the deep details that the Netflix show covered from Washington's perspective, told here from Long Branch's perspective.

**Netflix differentiation:** The Netflix show focuses on the political drama in Washington. This episode focuses on the New Jersey story -- the overnight railroad, the Francklyn Cottage, the community response, Bell's visits, and the death at the shore. Our unique angle is the place.

## Video 4: WILSON
### "Shadow Lawn and the War"

**Status:** Stub outline. Research complete (see `research/president-7-wilson.md`).
**Target Length:** 10-12 minutes
**Tone:** Political, dramatic, reflective

**Key Story Arc:**
- Wilson's New Jersey roots: Princeton student, professor, university president, governor. The only president in the Seven Presidents story who was a New Jersey native (in political identity if not birth).
- Shadow Lawn: the 52-room mansion in West Long Branch, originally built by the president of New York Life Insurance Company (who resigned in scandal). Wilson arrives September 1, 1916.
- The front-porch campaign: Wilson runs his entire re-election from Shadow Lawn's steps. "He Kept Us Out of War" -- a slogan that was "more of a hope than a promise." Campaign advisor Edward House manages strategy from the estate.
- Election night 1916: one of the closest in American history. Hughes goes to bed believing he won. California -- won by Wilson by just 3,773 votes -- tips the election the next morning.
- The broken promise: five months after winning on peace, Wilson asks Congress for war. April 1917.
- Wilson's departure: he leaves Shadow Lawn November 9, 1916 and never returns. The last president to use the Long Branch area as a summer capital.
- The fire: January 1927, Shadow Lawn burns. Flames visible for 20 miles. Hubert Parson (Woolworth president) commissions a replacement designed by Horace Trumbauer and Julian Abele -- one of the first prominent African American architects.
- The renaming: Monmouth University. Woodrow Wilson Hall. Then in 2020, Wilson's name removed because of his racist legacy -- segregating the federal workforce, screening Birth of a Nation at the White House. Now "The Great Hall at Shadow Lawn."
- The irony: a building designed partly by a Black architect, stripped of the name of a white supremacist president.

**Link to overview:** The overview covers Wilson in ~45 seconds. This episode expands the campaign, the election night drama, and the fire/legacy arc.

**Estimated Length:** 10-12 minutes

## Video 5: THE RESORT ERA
### "When Long Branch Was America's Playground"

**Status:** Stub outline. Research available across multiple files (see `research/overview.md`, `research/president-6-mckinley.md`).
**Target Length:** 10-12 minutes
**Tone:** Epic, nostalgic, cultural

**Key Story Arc:**
- The rise: Long Branch's origins in the 1780s. Mary Todd Lincoln's 1861 visit. The steamboat era. How the railroads transformed a fishing village into the most glamorous resort in America.
- The grand hotels: the Continental (1,000 guests, 28 cooks, 150 waiters), the Elberon Hotel, the Hollywood Hotel. "Hotel City" -- over 150 hotels and boarding houses.
- Elberon and Millionaire's Row: Lewis B. Brown's 400-acre development. McKim, Mead & White designing the mansions. Ocean Avenue as the most powerful address in America. The celebrities: Diamond Jim Brady, Lillian Russell, Lily Langtry, Edwin Booth, Jay Gould.
- The gambling empire: Phil Daly's Pennsylvania Club, "the Monte Carlo of America." Roulette, faro, free French chef, women not welcome. The 1897 gambling ban and the 1909 demolition of the gilded-dome palace.
- The racetrack: Monmouth Park opens 1870. "The Newmarket of America." Grant in his box. The 1894 wagering ban kills it.
- The decline: gambling outlawed, automobiles change vacation patterns, Atlantic City steals the spotlight. The grand hotels age and close. The steamboat era ends in the 1920s.
- The fire: June 8, 1987. A gas leak under the pier. 30 mph winds. The amusement pier, the Haunted Mansion, Kid's World -- all destroyed. $8 million in damages. The pier remnants torn down in 2001.
- The rebirth: Pier Village opens 2005. Long Branch's modern renaissance. The public beach where anyone can visit.

**Link to overview:** The overview gives the resort era 90 seconds (Segment 2). This episode makes it the main character -- not the presidents, but the place itself.

## Series Production Notes

### Release Order
1. **Overview** -- hook the audience with the myth and the seven stories
2. **Grant** -- the foundation; the first Summer White House
3. **Garfield** -- the peak drama; the assassination told from Long Branch's perspective
4. **Wilson** -- the political drama; the era's end
5. **The Resort Era** -- the cultural capstone; the place itself as the main character

### Why This Order Works
- The overview plants seven hooks; then we pay off the four strongest ones.
- Grant comes first because his story is the foundation -- without Grant, there is no Summer Capital.
- Garfield comes second because it is the most dramatic and the most likely to go viral.
- Wilson comes third because it closes the presidential era and transitions to...
- The Resort Era, which broadens the story beyond politics into culture, celebrity, and place.

### Consistent Elements Across All Videos
- **Opening title sequence:** Waves at Seven Presidents Park + "Seven Presidents" title treatment
- **Map graphics:** Recurring map of Long Branch/Elberon showing presidential locations
- **Closing sequence:** Return to the park; the Garfield monument; ocean views
- **Subscribe CTA:** Tease the next episode in the series

### What Happened to Hayes and Harrison?

**Hayes** has no verifiable connection to Long Branch. His diary places him at the Soldiers' Home every summer. His inclusion in the "Seven Presidents" appears to originate entirely from the 1925 chapel fundraising campaign. An 8-10 minute video about a president who probably never visited would be an uncomfortable stretch.

**Harrison** is a Cape May president. His Summer White House was Congress Hall, over 100 miles south. His connection to Long Branch is a single dinner party in 1898 as a former president. Again, not enough for a dedicated episode.

Both presidents get their due in the overview (Segment 5: "The Disputed Presidents") -- and that is the right amount of coverage for connections this thin. If the series performs well and there is audience demand, a "myth-busting bonus video" about the disputed three (Hayes, Harrison, Arthur) could be considered later.
