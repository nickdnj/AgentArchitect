## Corrections Made to Script

### 1. CORRECTED: Garfield's destination when shot
- **Script said:** "He was heading to the Jersey Shore for the summer."
- **Correction:** "He was heading to his alma mater, Williams College, before continuing to the Jersey Shore for the summer."
- **Source:** Wikipedia (Assassination of James A. Garfield), Miller Center, History.com. Garfield was en route to deliver a speech at Williams College and introduce his sons to his alma mater before continuing to Long Branch.

### 2. CORRECTED: Garfield weight loss
- **Script said:** "Garfield lost more than eighty pounds."
- **Correction:** "Garfield wasted from two hundred and ten pounds to just one hundred and thirty."
- **Source:** PBS NewsHour, Britannica, Wikipedia. He went from approximately 210 lbs to 130 lbs -- exactly 80 pounds, not "more than" 80. Using the specific numbers is more dramatic and more accurate.

### 3. CORRECTED: Guiteau's occupation description
- **Script said:** "a failed journalist"
- **Correction:** "a failed evangelist"
- **Source:** Wikipedia (Charles J. Guiteau), National Archives, Biography.com. Guiteau was not a journalist. He attempted to start a newspaper called *The Daily Theocrat* (which failed immediately) but his actual career attempts were as a lawyer, evangelist/itinerant preacher, bill collector, and insurance salesman. "Evangelist" is the most accurate parallel to "lawyer" and "theologian."

### 4. CORRECTED: Guiteau quote attribution
- **Script said:** "At Guiteau's trial, his lawyers made a chilling argument."
- **Correction:** "At Guiteau's trial, even the assassin made a chilling argument."
- **Source:** Wikipedia, History.com, NPS. The quote "The doctors killed Garfield. I just shot him" came from Guiteau himself during trial proceedings, not from his defense attorneys. While the defense team also argued malpractice, the famous quote is Guiteau's own words.

### 5. CORRECTED: Garret Hobart's name and circumstances
- **Script said:** "his dying Vice President, Garrett Hobart, at his estate in West Long Branch"
- **Correction:** "his ailing Vice President, Garret Hobart, at a summer home he leased in West Long Branch"
- **Sources:** Wikipedia (Garret Hobart), Britannica, Monmouth Timeline, Monmouth Beach Life.
- **Three errors fixed:**
  - "Garrett" (two t's) corrected to "Garret" (one t) -- the correct spelling per all authoritative sources.
  - "dying" changed to "ailing" -- Hobart was seriously ill in August 1899 but did not die until November 21, 1899, three months later, at his home in Paterson, NJ.
  - "his estate" changed to "a summer home he leased" -- Hobart leased the Normanhurst property from the widow of publisher Norman Munro. He did not own it.

### 6. CORRECTED: Cape May distance
- **Script said:** "Congress Hall, fifty miles to the south"
- **Correction:** "Congress Hall, over a hundred miles to the south"
- **Source:** Distance calculator sites. Cape May is approximately 106 miles (straight line) or 114 miles (driving) from Long Branch. "Fifty miles" is off by more than double.

### 7. CORRECTED: Number of Grant cottage donors
- **Script said:** "Five of Long Branch's wealthiest men"
- **Correction:** "A group of Long Branch's wealthiest men"
- **Source:** Multiple sources list different numbers and names. The Grant Cottage National Historic Landmark site lists donors including George Childs, Adolph Borie, Thomas Murphy, John Hoey, Moses Taylor, George Pullman, and Horace Porter -- seven names, not five. The exact number of contributors varies by source, so "a group" is more accurate. The $40,000 figure was also removed because sources conflict ($32,000 vs $40,000).

### 8. RESOLVED: Two VERIFY comments
- `<!-- VERIFY: Exact number of Bell's visits and sequence of events -->` -- Resolved. Bell visited the White House on July 26 and August 1, 1881. Both attempts failed: the first due to equipment issues, the second because Dr. Bliss restricted scanning to the wrong side. The autopsy confirmed the bullet was on the left.
- `<!-- VERIFY: Robert Todd Lincoln's exact title at the time -->` -- Resolved. Robert Todd Lincoln served as Secretary of War under both Garfield and Arthur (1881-1885). Confirmed by Miller Center, Wikipedia, NPS.

## Acceptable Simplifications (Not Corrected)

| Claim | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| "Ponzi scheme" for Grant & Ward fraud | Technically anachronistic -- Charles Ponzi's scheme was 1920. But "Ponzi scheme" is universally understood and commonly used to describe Grant & Ward in modern sources. Acceptable for a documentary. |
| Bell built "the world's first metal detector" | The induction balance was based on earlier work by David Hughes. Bell adapted it for bullet detection. Most popular sources credit Bell with creating the first practical metal detector. Acceptable simplification. |
| "Three degrees of presidential assassination" | A narrative device, not a factual claim. Robert Todd Lincoln was present at or near three presidential assassinations (Lincoln 1865, Garfield 1881, McKinley 1901). Arthur was the beneficiary of Garfield's assassination. The "three degrees" framing is poetic license and works for the documentary. |
| Guiteau's "ivory-handled revolver" | Sources conflict slightly on whether Guiteau actually purchased the ivory-handled version or the wood-handled one. Most popular sources say he chose ivory. Some say he wanted ivory but couldn't afford the extra dollar. The Smithsonian's records are ambiguous since the gun disappeared. The ivory detail is the most widely repeated version. |
| "The real number might be four. Maybe five." | This is the script's editorial assessment, not a factual claim. Grant, Garfield, Wilson, and McKinley have strong connections (4). Arthur has a real but modest connection (possibly 5). This is a defensible interpretation based on the Monmouth Timeline research. |

## Unverifiable Claims

| Claim | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| "Grant held cabinet meetings on the porch" | Widely repeated but specific documentation of porch cabinet meetings is limited. The White House Historical Association says messengers "constantly visited" with official papers. Cabinet meetings at the cottage are referenced in research but primary documentation is thin. |
| Grant played poker "every Friday night" | Referenced in research file, sourced to general Long Branch history. Plausible but specific frequency ("every Friday") may be embellished over time. |
