[PAUSE]

"By nineteen thirty, a seventh name was added. The park that opened nearby was christened Seven Presidents Oceanfront Park. But the evidence behind those seven names? It ranges from undeniable to almost certainly fiction."

[VISUAL: AI-generated panoramic view of Long Branch oceanfront circa 1880s -- grand hotels, horse-drawn carriages, elegantly dressed figures on a wide boardwalk]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "Long Branch -- 'America's Summer Capital' -- 1870s"]

NARRATION:
"To understand how presidents ended up at this beach, you need to understand what Long Branch was. In the decades after the Civil War, this was the most glamorous resort in America. Richer than Newport. More daring than Saratoga Springs."

[VISUAL: LOC photograph of the Elberon Hotel, circa 1900, Detroit Publishing Company]

"The railroads brought it all. By eighteen seventy-five, a New Yorker could reach Long Branch in under two hours. The great hotels rose along the bluffs -- the Continental, which fed a thousand guests. The Elberon, the smartest and most exclusive hotel on the coast."

[VISUAL: AI-generated Victorian gambling parlor -- roulette wheel, faro table, men in formal evening wear, gaslight chandeliers]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "Phil Daly's Pennsylvania Club -- 'The Monte Carlo of America'"]

[PAUSE]

"Into this world of money and spectacle walked the most powerful man on Earth."

## [SEGMENT 3: Grant -- The First Summer White House - 3:00-4:30]

[VISUAL: LOC stereograph -- President Grant seated on the porch of his Long Branch cottage with Julia and son Jesse, circa 1872]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "President Grant at Long Branch -- 1872"]

NARRATION:
"Ulysses S. Grant first visited Long Branch in eighteen sixty-seven, two years before his inauguration. He declared it ideal for a summer residence. But when friends offered to buy him a cottage, the general refused. It would be improper."

[PAUSE]

"So they bought it for Mrs. Grant instead."

[VISUAL: LOC HABS photograph of the Grant Cottage exterior, 1933 -- the 28-room Stick Style mansion at 995 Ocean Avenue]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "The Grant Cottage -- 995 Ocean Avenue, Elberon"]

"A group of Long Branch's wealthiest men -- including George Pullman and publisher George W. Childs -- pooled their money for a twenty-eight-room mansion on Ocean Avenue. Julia accepted without hesitation. For eight years, it served as the nation's Summer White House."

"Grant held cabinet meetings on the porch. He played poker with America's wealthiest men every Friday night. Tourists strolled past hoping to glimpse the president on his veranda."

[VISUAL: Winslow Homer painting "Long Branch, New Jersey" -- women on the bluff overlooking the beach]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "Winslow Homer -- 'Long Branch, New Jersey' -- 1869"]

"Julia Grant later wrote in her memoirs: What a boon our cottage at Long Branch was to the President! Tired and weary as he was with his monotonous official duties, he hastened with delight to its health-giving breezes and its wide and restful piazzas."

[VISUAL: LOC HABS photograph of the Grant Cottage -- later view showing deterioration before demolition]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "Demolished 1963 -- Now a construction site"]

"But the story does not end well. In eighteen eighty-four, Grant was ruined by a Ponzi scheme. Broke and battling throat cancer, he began writing his memoirs at the cottage -- a race against death. The memoirs earned Julia nearly half a million dollars. The cottage was demolished in nineteen sixty-three. Today, multi-million dollar homes are rising where the Summer White House once stood."

[TRANSITION: Crossfade, 1.5s]

## [SEGMENT 4: The Garfield Story - 4:30-5:30]

[VISUAL: LOC composite photograph -- nine images showing Garfield, the assassination site, Guiteau, the gun, the coffin in the Capitol]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "July 2, 1881"]
[MUSIC: Shift to darker, more dramatic underscore]

NARRATION:
"On July second, eighteen eighty-one, President James Garfield was shot at a Washington train station. A delusional failed lawyer named Charles Guiteau fired two shots from behind."

[VISUAL: LOC Frank Leslie's Illustrated Newspaper engraving -- the assassination scene in the train station]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "'I am a Stalwart of the Stalwarts! Arthur is now President!'"]

"What followed was one of the worst medical disasters in American history. A parade of doctors probed the wound with unsterilized instruments. The bullet had missed every vital organ. It was the infections that killed him."

[VISUAL: LOC Frank Leslie's engraving -- "Workmen laying the railroad track" -- torchlight, volunteers, track being laid at night along a suburban street]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "September 5, 1881 -- Elberon, New Jersey"]

"By September, the doctors decided to move Garfield to the ocean. But he could not survive a carriage ride to the shore. So on the night of September fifth, volunteers laid thirty-two hundred feet of temporary track from the train station to the oceanfront. They worked through the night by torchlight. The Elberon Hotel sent sandwiches."

[VISUAL: LOC Frank Leslie's engraving -- "Removal of President Garfield to Francklyn Cottage" -- the presidential train car, crowds of people, the cottage in the background]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "Francklyn Cottage -- Elberon, Long Branch"]

"The crowd pushed the president's train car around a bend by hand. As Garfield was carried inside, he is said to have whispered -- Thank God, it is good to be here."

[VISUAL: LOC broadside -- "The Last Bulletin. Elberon, N.J., Sept. 19, 1881"]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "September 19, 1881 -- 10:35 PM"]

"Thirteen days later, James Abram Garfield died. He was forty-nine years old. He had been president for less than seven months."

[PAUSE]

"The full story of the assassination will get its own video. But what matters here is this place. The stretch of shoreline where a community built a railroad overnight to bring a dying president to the sea."

[TRANSITION: Crossfade, 1.5s]

## [SEGMENT 5: The Disputed Presidents - 5:30-6:10]

[VISUAL: Hayes portrait (LOC Brady-Handy) with a subtle question mark overlay, followed by Harrison portrait and Arthur portrait]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "The Disputed Three"]
[MUSIC: Shift to lighter, more inquisitive tone]

NARRATION:
"So what about the other presidents? Hayes spent his summers in Washington. Researchers have found no evidence placing him near Long Branch. Harrison is a Cape May man -- his Summer White House was over a hundred miles to the south. Arthur? His most documented visit was the night he came to comfort the widow of the man whose murder made him president."

[PAUSE]

"Three of the seven are, at best, on thin ice. But try renaming it Four Presidents Park."

## [SEGMENT 6: Wilson, McKinley, and the End of an Era - 6:10-7:30]

[VISUAL: LOC photograph -- Wilson notification ceremony at Shadow Lawn, 1916 -- crowd gathered on the front lawn of the 52-room mansion]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "Shadow Lawn -- West Long Branch -- 1916"]

NARRATION:
"Two more presidents have strong connections to Long Branch. In eighteen ninety-nine, William McKinley came to deliver a defense of the American flag at Ocean Grove's Great Auditorium -- and to visit his ailing Vice President, Garret Hobart, in West Long Branch. Two years later, McKinley was assassinated. The second president in the Seven Presidents story killed in office."

[VISUAL: LOC photograph -- Shadow Lawn exterior, Bain Collection / Harris & Ewing, 1916]

"And then Woodrow Wilson. In nineteen sixteen, Wilson moved into Shadow Lawn, a fifty-two-room mansion, and ran his entire re-election campaign from its front porch. His slogan: He Kept Us Out of War."

[PAUSE]

"Five months after winning, he asked Congress to declare war on Germany."

[VISUAL: Wilson accepting the 1916 Democratic nomination at Shadow Lawn]
[TEXT OVERLAY: "'He Kept Us Out of War' -- a promise broken five months later"]

"Election night was one of the closest in history. Wilson's opponent went to bed believing he had won. California -- which Wilson won by just three thousand seven hundred and seventy-three votes -- tipped the election the next morning."

"Wilson left Shadow Lawn that November and never returned. He was the last president to summer at Long Branch. The era that began with Grant in eighteen sixty-nine ended with Wilson -- forty-seven years of American power on this stretch of coast."

[TEXT OVERLAY: "Shadow Lawn burned in 1927. The replacement became Monmouth University. Wilson's name was removed in 2020."]

[TRANSITION: Crossfade, 1.5s]
