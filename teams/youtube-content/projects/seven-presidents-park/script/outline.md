# Video Outline: Seven Presidents Park -- The Myth and The Shore

## Target: Documentary, 7-8 minutes, general YouTube -- history buffs, Jersey Shore locals, documentary fans

## Series Context
This is **Video 1 of 5** in the Seven Presidents documentary series. This video serves as the overview/hook that teases four deep-dive episodes. It must be self-contained and compelling on its own while planting seeds for the series.

**Series plan:** See `series-plan-v2.md` for the full 5-video structure.

---

## Chapter 1: The Myth (0:00 - 1:30)
### Summary
Open strong with the myth-busting hook: the park claims seven presidents vacationed here, but the real number may be as low as four. Introduce the park, the name, and the question that drives the video.
### Key Points
- Seven Presidents Oceanfront Park -- 38 acres, 4,700-foot shoreline in Long Branch, NJ
- The seven presidents listed: Grant, Hayes, Garfield, Arthur, Harrison, McKinley, Wilson
- The myth: all seven "vacationed" here. The reality: the evidence is uneven.
- The 1925 origin of the "Seven Presidents" branding -- a financial crisis at a chapel
- Tease: "The stories behind these men are even better than the myth."
### Scenes: 4
### Visual Approach
Modern park atmosphere transitioning to vintage sepia tones. Title card. Presidential portrait montage.

## Chapter 2: America's Summer Capital (1:30 - 3:00)
### Summary
Set the stage: how Long Branch became the premier presidential resort in the Gilded Age. The railroads, the grand hotels, the social scene that drew the most powerful people in America.
### Key Points
- Long Branch's origins as a resort (1780s), Mary Todd Lincoln's 1861 visit
- The railroads transform access -- steamboat + rail from NYC in under 2 hours
- The grand hotels: Continental (1,000 guests), Elberon Hotel, Hollywood Hotel
- Elberon as "The Playland of Millionaires" -- Lewis B. Brown's development
- The gambling era: Phil Daly's Pennsylvania Club, "Monte Carlo of America"
### Scenes: 3
### Visual Approach
Sweeping Gilded Age imagery -- grand hotels, horse-drawn carriages on Ocean Avenue, elegant Victorian society. Period photographs from LOC where available.

## Chapter 3: Grant -- The First Summer White House (3:00 - 4:30)
### Summary
Ulysses S. Grant's story -- the president who put Long Branch on the map. The clever gift of a cottage, the Summer White House years, Julia's perspective from her memoirs, and the bittersweet final chapter. Expanded from previous version to include Julia's quote and Winslow Homer painting.
### Key Points
- 1867: Grant visits as Commanding General, declares it ideal for a summer residence
- 1869: Wealthy friends gift a 28-room cottage to "Mrs. Grant" (circumventing his refusal)
- The Summer White House: cabinet meetings, poker nights, carriage rides on Ocean Avenue
- Julia Grant's memoir: "What a boon our cottage at Long Branch was to the President!"
- Winslow Homer's 1869 painting of Long Branch -- the resort in art
- The Grant & Ward financial ruin (1884), the cancer diagnosis at the cottage
- The memoirs earned Julia nearly $450,000 in royalties
- The cottage demolished in 1963 -- now a grassy lot
### Scenes: 4
### Visual Approach
LOC HABS photographs of the Grant Cottage (1933). Stereograph of Grant family on porch. Winslow Homer painting. Period illustrations from Harper's Weekly.

## Chapter 4: The Garfield Story (4:30 - 5:30)
### Summary
TRIMMED. The Garfield story is told in headline beats only -- the shooting, the medical disaster (one sentence), the overnight railroad (the emotional peak), the death, and a nod to the Netflix show. The full deep dive is saved for Video 3. This version focuses on what makes Long Branch unique: the railroad and the cottage, not the assassination details.
### Key Points
- July 2, 1881: Garfield shot at the Baltimore and Potomac Railroad Station
- Guiteau: one-sentence characterization ("delusional failed lawyer")
- Medical disaster summarized in one beat: "unsterilized instruments... it was the infections that killed him"
- The overnight railroad: 3,200 feet of track, torchlight, sandwiches, lemonade
- The crowd pushes the train car by hand
- "Thank God, it is good to be here."
- September 19, 1881: Garfield dies at 49
- Netflix nod: "If you have seen the Netflix show, you know the full story"
- CUT: Bell's metal detector, Guiteau's background, Bliss's stubbornness, weight loss, Arthur swearing-in, Guiteau trial quote
### Scenes: 5
### Visual Approach
Focus on the Long Branch visuals -- Frank Leslie's engravings of the overnight railroad and the removal to Francklyn Cottage. The assassination composite for the hook. The Last Bulletin broadside for the death.

## Chapter 5: The Disputed Presidents (5:30 - 6:10)
### Summary
Quick myth-busting run through the weaker connections -- Hayes (no evidence), Harrison (primarily Cape May), Arthur (real but limited).
### Key Points
- Hayes: his diary shows summers at the Soldiers' Home in D.C. -- no evidence of any Long Branch visit
- Harrison: his Summer White House was Congress Hall at Cape May, over 100 miles to the south
- Arthur: most documented visit was comforting Garfield's widow
- "Three of the seven are, at best, on thin ice."
### Scenes: 2
### Visual Approach
Presidential portraits with question-mark overlays. Quick, punchy editing. The Church of the Presidents photograph.

## Chapter 6: Wilson, McKinley, and the End of an Era (6:10 - 7:30)
### Summary
The final two presidents with strong connections. McKinley's flag speech and dying VP. Wilson's Shadow Lawn campaign, "He Kept Us Out of War," the election night drama, and the end of the presidential shore era. Expanded from previous version to include Hughes election night anecdote and the 2020 renaming context.
### Key Points
- McKinley: August 1899 Ocean Grove speech ("Wherever that flag is raised..."), visiting ailing VP Hobart at Normanhurst
- McKinley assassinated 1901 -- the second assassination connected to Seven Presidents
- Wilson: Shadow Lawn as Summer White House, 1916 front-porch campaign
- "He Kept Us Out of War" -- a promise broken five months later
- Hughes goes to bed thinking he won; California's 3,773 votes tip it
- Wilson was the last president to summer at Long Branch -- the era ends 1916
- Shadow Lawn burned 1927, rebuilt, became Monmouth University; Wilson's name removed in 2020
### Scenes: 4
### Visual Approach
Shadow Lawn photographs (LOC). Wilson notification ceremony photo. Wilson accepting nomination. McKinley implied through narration over Wilson visuals.

## Chapter 7: What Endures (7:30 - 8:10)
### Summary
What remains of the presidential era today. The park, the tea house, the church. Tease the five-part series with specific upcoming episodes.
### Key Points
- Seven Presidents Park today: 38 acres of public beach where anyone can visit
- The Garfield Tea House -- a railroad rail as its ridgepole
- Four undeniable, three debatable -- but the stories are real
- Series tease: Grant's poker games, the full Garfield story, Wilson's broken promise, the resort era
### Scenes: 3
### Visual Approach
Warm, golden-hour tone. AI-generated modern park scenes. The tea house. End card with series branding.
