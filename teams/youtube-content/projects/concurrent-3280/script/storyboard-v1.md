# Storyboard v1 — The Concurrent 3280

**Project:** Jersey Stack candidate, Episode 2 (after "The Transistor")
**Target runtime:** ~12 minutes
**Drafted:** May 6, 2026
**Status:** First-pass storyboard. Narration is rough — Nick will tighten on a pass-through.

---

## Story-frame guardrails (non-negotiable)

- This video is about the **machine** and its **architect, Ken Yeager**. Nick is behind the camera under Vistter.
- No first-person narration. No "I remember." Yeager observations are 3rd-person observational ("He rode his bike to work…").
- Only Yeager and Mike Martone are named individually. Everyone else is "the engineering team," "the four-board CPU group," "the technician who built it."
- The phone-list reveal is the closing personal grace note. **No narration over it.** Hold the artifact, let the viewer notice.
- Yeager's death is stated factually. No elegy, no swelling music.

## 1. Cold open (0:00–0:30) — three options for Nick to pick

### Option A — "30 feet apart" (recommended)
- **Visual:** Slow push through InfoAge floor. Static shot of the Concurrent 3280 cabinet, hold 4 seconds. Cut to the SGI Onyx 10000 across the room. Hold 4 seconds. Cut back to a wide that frames both, with a measuring distance overlay reading **"~30 ft."**
- **Narration:** "These two machines sit thirty feet apart in a museum in Wall, New Jersey. One is the last great minicomputer of an era that's about to end. The other is the chip that ended it. The same engineer designed both."
- **Why it works:** Front-loads the thesis. The R10000 payoff is no longer a twist — it's the question the rest of the video answers.

### Option B — "The architect on a bike"
- **Visual:** Period-style photo treatment. A 1985-coded NJ industrial-park exterior. Bike leaned against a brick wall. Slow zoom on the bike. Cut to interior: empty engineering office, an open notebook on a desk with a torn scrap of paper.
- **Narration:** "In 1985, in a Tinton Falls engineering lab, an MIT-trained architect rode his bicycle to work, hummed at his desk, and handed engineers scraps of paper with the design of one of the most powerful minicomputers ever built. His name was Ken Yeager. This is the story of the machine he built — and what happened next."
- **Why it works:** Humanizes Yeager up front. Sets the tone for the observational details that pay off later.

### Option C — "The phone list"
- **Visual:** Tight macro on a printed phone list, dot-matrix print quality. Slow scroll. Names blur past. Stop on one name. Push in. Hard cut to black.
- **Narration:** "Forty years after a New Jersey engineering team built a machine called the Concurrent 3280, one of them posted this phone list to a Facebook group. This is the story behind it."
- **Why it works:** Mysterious, low-key, leads with the artifact. But — risks burying the lede; the phone list is more powerful as a closer than an opener.

**Recommendation:** Option A. It frontloads the thesis from the museum sign and earns the rest of the runtime. Option B as fallback if the InfoAge dual-shot doesn't land visually.

### Scene 7 — The architect at work (observational)
- **Time:** 3:05–3:50
- **Visual:** Photo of Yeager (find from MIT, obit, or SGI-era headshot). Then cutaway shots evoking the observations: a bicycle leaning against a wall, ankle-cuffed jeans, a checkered shirt on a coatrack, a torn scrap of notebook paper with a hand-drawn logic sketch. Don't show actors — use objects and stills.
- **Narration:** "The architect of the 3280 was Ken Yeager. MIT, class of '72. He'd come to Concurrent — then still Perkin-Elmer — in 1979. He rode his bicycle to work every day. His pants cuffs stayed rolled up at the ankle, even at his desk. He wore the same kind of checkered bowling-style shirt every week. He hummed, almost constantly, while he worked. He stayed in his office. Engineers had audiences with him — they'd go in, come out with a scrap of paper covered in Yeager's handwriting, and they'd execute on it. The 3280 was designed on those scraps."
- **Assets:** Yeager portrait (search MIT alumni / Legacy.com obit / SGI archives). Stylized stills for bike, shirt, scrap of paper. Possibly a one-frame illustration of a "Yeager scrap" — a hand-sketched ALU stage on lined paper.
- **Tone:** Observational. Third person throughout. No nostalgia — this is what a quiet kid in the corner would have noticed.

### Scene 8 — The four-board pipeline
- **Time:** 3:50–5:05
- **Visual:** Animated diagram: **FETCH → DECODE → ALU → WRITE-BACK.** Each stage lights up as it's named. Then dissolve to: photo macros of Schottky TTL boards (reshoot from the InfoAge unit). Each board card flies in with its label.
- **Narration:** "The 3280's CPU was four boards. Each board was one stage of a scalar pipeline: fetch, decode, ALU, write-back. Four hundred nanoseconds per stage. One instruction every clock cycle. The whole thing was built from Schottky TTL — discrete logic chips, soldered to printed-circuit boards, hand-routed. By 1988 this was anachronistic. The rest of the industry was moving to single-chip CPUs. But for real-time work — refineries, defense, simulators — the 3280's discrete-logic pipeline was deterministic, predictable, and proven. The engineering team was small — four boards, one architect, a senior engineer consulting across all of them, an engineer on loan from another group, and the technician who actually built the prototype on the bench. The architect handed out the scraps. The team turned the scraps into silicon and copper."
- **Assets:** Custom 4-board pipeline animation (see open question #3). Macro photography of InfoAge 3280 boards. Possibly a diagram callout for the S-Bus (F-PATH/T-PATH).
- **Tone:** Teaching moment. Slow down here — this is the technical heart of the video.
- **Length flag:** ⚠ This scene is ~75 seconds. If the four-board animation runs long, this could push to 90s. Consider trimming the closing "scraps to silicon and copper" line if pacing tight.

### Scene 13 — The R10000 payoff
- **Time:** 7:30–8:35
- **Visual:** Yeager's R10000 paper cover (IEEE Micro 1996). R10000 die shot. Hardware photo of the R10000 chip in package. Animated callout: "4-issue out-of-order superscalar." Then a wide shot of an SGI Onyx workstation. Cut to InfoAge: a real Onyx 10000 in the museum.
- **Narration:** "In 1996, Yeager publishes a paper in IEEE Micro. The title: 'The MIPS R10000 Superscalar Microprocessor.' Four-instruction issue. Out-of-order execution. Speculative execution. A textbook RISC processor — the kind they teach computer architecture grad students with. One chip, one piece of silicon, more performance than the 3280 had with twelve CPUs and a million-eight-hundred-thousand-dollar price tag. Yeager went on to architect the R12000 and the R18000. He held four sole patents and ten team patents during those years. The chips he designed at MIPS shipped inside Silicon Graphics workstations — the machines that rendered Jurassic Park, Toy Story, the early web."
- **Assets:** R10000 paper. Die shot from public sources. Onyx imagery. Possibly a Jurassic Park / Toy Story render reference (fair-use clip — keep it brief).
