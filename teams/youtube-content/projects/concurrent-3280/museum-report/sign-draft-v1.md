## Bullets

- The **last great pre-RISC scalar minicomputer.**
- Designed in **New Jersey** during the **Perkin-Elmer → Concurrent Computer transition** (Nov 1985 spinoff).
- Released **January 26, 1988** — 3280SP single-processor, 6 MIPS.
- **November 29, 1988** — 3280E MPS, up to 12 processors, **76.8 MIPS, 256 MB RAM, $1.8 million**.
- Built from **Schottky TTL discrete logic** — anachronistic by 1988, but proven for real-time work.
- **4-stage scalar pipeline:** FETCH → DECODE → ALU → WRITE-BACK.
- Architect: **Ken Yeager** (MIT '72), who left Concurrent for SGI/MIPS — and went on to architect the legendary **MIPS R10000**, the same chip that powers the **Onyx 10000** sitting in this museum.
- Direct lineage from the **Interdata 7/32** (Edison, NJ — 1966) and **8/32** (1975).
- Two operating systems: **OS/32** (real-time) and **XELOS** (Unix V).
- Concurrent itself spun off from Perkin-Elmer in November 1985, pivoted to streaming media, and was acquired in 2017 — the same year **Yeager passed away.**

## "The Backstory:" sub-section

- When Edison NJ engineers founded **Interdata in 1966**, they pioneered 32-bit minicomputing for industrial and real-time applications — refineries, missile defense, telemetry.
- **Perkin-Elmer** acquired them in 1973 for $73M.
- By the mid-1980s the Interdata lineage was matured, battle-tested, and quietly powering critical systems worldwide while DEC and IBM got the headlines.
- The 3280 was Concurrent's answer to the late-80s push for more MIPS — and **the last machine they'd build before the silicon revolution made discrete-logic minis obsolete.**
- That silicon revolution? **It came partly from Concurrent's own chief architect.** Ken Yeager left for SGI/MIPS and architected the R10000 — a chip that delivered more performance on a single die than the 3280 had with its 12-CPU MPS configuration.
