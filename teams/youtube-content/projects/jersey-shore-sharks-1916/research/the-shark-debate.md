# The Shark Debate: Great White vs. Bull Shark

## The Central Question

More than a century after the 1916 attacks, scientists still cannot definitively answer two fundamental questions:

1. **What species of shark was responsible?**
2. **Was it one shark or multiple sharks?**

The evidence is circumstantial, the physical specimens are long gone, and the debate "will likely continue to generate spirited debate" for the foreseeable future, according to ichthyologist George H. Burgess of the Florida Museum of Natural History.

---

## The Shark That Was Caught

### The Specimen

On July 14, 1916, Michael Schleisser caught a shark in Raritan Bay, approximately 4 miles from the mouth of Matawan Creek:

- **Length:** 7.5 feet (approximately 2 meters)
- **Weight:** 325 pounds
- **Species Identified at the Time:** Juvenile great white shark (*Carcharodon carcharias*)
- **Identified by:** Scientists at the American Museum of Natural History (Dr. Frederic Lucas, John Treadwell Nichols)

### Stomach Contents

When Schleisser opened the shark:
- Found "suspicious fleshy material and bones"
- Material filled approximately **two-thirds of a milk crate**
- Total weight: **15 pounds**
- Dr. Frederic Lucas examined the remains and confirmed they were human: "They are the parts of the left radius and ulna [lower arm bones] and one of the anterior left ribs, all human. There is no doubt about this."

### What Happened to the Specimen

Schleisser, a professional taxidermist, mounted the shark and displayed it in a Manhattan shop window on Broadway. The specimen is now lost. No tissue samples, teeth, or preserved remains survive for modern DNA analysis.

### After the Catch

No further shark attacks occurred along the Jersey Shore that summer. This timing strongly suggests -- but does not prove -- that Schleisser caught the responsible shark.

## The Case for Great White Shark (*Carcharodon carcharias*)

### Arguments For

1. **The Identified Specimen:** The shark caught by Schleisser was identified as a great white by scientists at the American Museum of Natural History in 1916. While identification techniques were less sophisticated than today, these were among the most qualified ichthyologists in the country.

2. **Stomach Contents:** Human remains were confirmed inside. This is the strongest single piece of evidence.

3. **The International Shark Attack File (ISAF)** at the Florida Museum of Natural History officially lists the attacks as perpetrated by a great white shark.

4. **Size and Behavior:** Great whites are among the few shark species capable of inflicting the massive injuries described -- severing limbs, removing 10+ pounds of flesh in a single bite.

5. **Ocean Attacks:** The first two attacks (Beach Haven and Spring Lake) occurred in open ocean conditions typical of great white feeding territory.

6. **Coastal Waters:** While not a freshwater species, examination of the Matawan Creek site reveals that "the size of the 'creek,' its depth, and salinity regime were closer to a marine embayment." During the 1916 drought, salinity levels were unusually high deep into the creek. A small great white could have navigated these conditions.

7. **George Burgess's Assessment:** Despite the bull shark arguments, Burgess maintains that "the evidence points to the young great white" and notes that "a smallish white clearly could have wandered into the area."

### Arguments Against

1. **Freshwater Intolerance:** Great white sharks are exclusively saltwater animals. They cannot regulate their body chemistry in freshwater the way bull sharks can. Matawan Creek, even with elevated salinity, was brackish -- challenging territory for a great white.

2. **Size Question:** At 7.5 feet, the caught shark was small for a great white. Some experts have questioned whether it was large enough to inflict all the described injuries.

3. **Possible Misidentification:** In 1916, the science of shark taxonomy was less refined. It is theoretically possible the specimen was misidentified. However, no one has definitively proven this.

## The Case for Bull Shark (*Carcharhinus leucas*)

### Arguments For

1. **Freshwater Capability:** The bull shark is the only large shark species that routinely enters freshwater. It is **euryhaline** -- able to adapt to a wide range of salinities. This is the strongest argument for the bull shark theory.

2. **Documented Freshwater Presence:** Bull sharks have been found:
   - **4,000 km (2,500 miles)** up the Amazon River to Iquitos, Peru
   - In freshwater **Lake Nicaragua**
   - In the **Ganges and Brahmaputra Rivers** of India and Bangladesh
   - In the **Tigris River** as far as Baghdad, Iraq
   - In the **Mississippi River** as far as Alton, Illinois (over 1,000 miles from the ocean)
   - In **Sydney Harbour** inlets, Australia

3. **Attack Pattern:** Bull sharks are responsible for a disproportionate number of unprovoked attacks on humans worldwide -- at least 100+ documented, 27+ fatal. They frequently attack in shallow, murky, brackish water -- exactly the conditions of Matawan Creek.

4. **Bite Analysis:** Joseph Dunn's injury pattern -- the type of bite on his leg -- was later cited as more consistent with **bull shark dentition** than great white.

5. **Habitat Match:** Matawan Creek was a narrow, shallow, tidal waterway with murky water. This is classic bull shark territory. Great whites typically prefer deeper, clearer water.

6. **Behavioral Consistency:** Bull sharks are known for their aggressive, territorial behavior in confined waterways. A bull shark in a narrow creek would be more likely to attack multiple targets than a great white, which tends to be an ambush predator in open water.

### Arguments Against

1. **No Bull Shark Was Caught:** The only shark caught with human remains was identified as a great white. No bull shark was captured or identified.

2. **Range Question:** While bull sharks can enter freshwater, their presence in northern New Jersey tidal creeks in 1916 was not documented. (However, absence of documentation does not mean absence.)

3. **The Attacks Stopped:** After Schleisser's great white was caught, attacks ceased. If a bull shark was responsible, why did it stop?

## The Multi-Shark Theory

Some researchers believe **more than one shark** was involved:

### Evidence Supporting Multiple Sharks

1. **Geographic Spread:** The attacks spanned approximately 70 miles of coastline (Beach Haven to Matawan) plus an inland creek. For one shark to cover this ground in 12 days, while possible, would be unusual.

2. **Different Injury Patterns:** The ocean attacks (Vansant and Bruder) involved massive bite wounds more consistent with a great white, while the Matawan Creek injuries -- particularly Dunn's -- showed patterns more consistent with a bull shark.

3. **Species Capability:** A great white for the ocean attacks, a bull shark for the creek attacks, would elegantly explain the full pattern.

4. **Expert Opinion:** Richard Ellis, author of *The Book of Sharks*, notes: "The evidence is long gone, and we will never really know if it was one shark or several, one species or another, that was responsible."

### Evidence Against Multiple Sharks

1. The attacks stopped after Schleisser caught a single shark.
2. Human remains from multiple victims were found in one stomach.
3. The geographic progression (south to north, then inland) is consistent with one animal following prey or currents.

## The "Rogue Shark" Theory

### Victor Coppleson's Hypothesis

In the mid-20th century, Australian surgeon and shark researcher Victor Coppleson developed the **"rogue shark" theory** -- the idea that individual sharks can develop a taste for human flesh, much like rogue lions or tigers. The 1916 attacks were a cornerstone of his argument.

Coppleson proposed that a single shark, having tasted human blood, became increasingly bold and aggressive, traveling up the coast and eventually into the creek in pursuit of human prey.

### Modern Assessment

Modern shark science has largely **rejected** the rogue shark theory. Peter Benchley himself acknowledged this evolution: "What I now know, which wasn't known when I wrote Jaws, is that there is no such thing as a rogue shark which develops a taste for human flesh" (2000).

Most researchers today believe the attacks were opportunistic, driven by environmental factors (warm water, high salinity, human presence) rather than predatory preference.

---

## Environmental Factors That May Explain the Attacks

1. **Unusually Warm Water Temperatures:** The summer of 1916 was extremely hot. Warm waters may have pushed sharks further north than usual.

2. **Drought Conditions:** Reduced rainfall in 1916 led to unusually high salinity in estuaries and creeks like Matawan Creek, making them more hospitable to marine predators.

3. **Polio Exodus:** The polio epidemic drove unprecedented numbers of people to the beaches, putting more humans in the water than ever before.

4. **WWI Maritime Activity:** Some theorized (without strong evidence) that increased shipping, depth charges, and U-boat warfare disturbed marine ecosystems, driving sharks toward shore. A letter to the *New York Times* suggested sharks "may have devoured human bodies in the waters of the German war zone and followed liners to this coast."

5. **Commercial Fishing Waste:** Fishing boats dumping chum and castoffs near shore may have attracted sharks to areas with human swimmers.

## What the Theories Tell Us About the 1916 Attacks

### Ocean Attacks (Beach Haven, Spring Lake)

**Most Likely:** Great white shark. The injuries, the open-water location, and the predatory behavior are all consistent with great white attacks. Both the ISAF and the majority of experts attribute these to a great white.

### Creek Attacks (Matawan)

**Genuinely Uncertain.** This is where the debate gets heated:
- A bull shark fits the habitat perfectly -- narrow, brackish, inland water.
- But a small great white could have navigated the creek during drought-elevated salinity.
- The caught great white had human remains, suggesting it was at least involved in the creek attacks.
- Joseph Dunn's bite pattern suggests bull shark dentition.

### The Most Likely Scenario (Expert Consensus)

Most experts lean toward **one or two sharks**:
- A juvenile great white responsible for the ocean attacks and possibly the creek attacks
- Possibly a bull shark involved in the Matawan Creek incidents
- The great white caught by Schleisser was likely responsible for at least some of the attacks

As George Burgess summarizes: "The species involved has always been doubtful and likely will continue to generate spirited debate."

---

## Modern Scientific Tools That Could Resolve the Debate

If the Schleisser specimen had survived:
- **DNA analysis** could definitively identify the species
- **Stable isotope analysis** of teeth could reveal habitat and dietary history
- **Bite mark analysis** with modern forensic techniques could match injuries to specific jaw structures

Unfortunately, the taxidermied specimen is lost. No tissue, teeth, or photographic records sufficient for species identification are known to survive.
