# Historical Context: America in the Summer of 1916

## A Nation Between Two Storms

The summer of 1916 found America suspended between multiple crises. Understanding the world these attacks occurred in is essential for conveying why they had such an outsized impact on the national psyche.

---

## World War I and the Preparedness Movement

### The War in Europe

By July 1916, World War I had been raging for two years. The Western Front was locked in a catastrophic stalemate. The Battle of the Somme -- one of the bloodiest battles in human history -- began on July 1, 1916, the same day Charles Vansant was attacked in Beach Haven. Over 1 million casualties would result before it ended in November.

### America's Position

The United States had not yet entered the war (that would come in April 1917), but the conflict dominated American life:

- **Submarine Warfare:** German U-boats prowled the Atlantic. The sinking of the RMS Lusitania on May 7, 1915, killing 1,198 people (128 Americans), had outraged the nation and fueled anti-German sentiment.
- **The Preparedness Movement:** A growing faction demanded military readiness. Woodrow Wilson, running for re-election on the slogan "He Kept Us Out of War," faced immense pressure from both interventionists and isolationists.
- **U-Boats off the Coast:** In July 1916, the German submarine U-53 would visit Newport, Rhode Island -- a chilling reminder that the war was not as far away as Americans wanted to believe.
- **Conspiracy Theories:** When the shark attacks began, some newspapers and letter-writers blamed German U-boats for driving sharks to shore or even suggested the Germans had "trained" sharks to attack Americans. Cartoonists depicted U-boats with shark fins and jaws attacking Uncle Sam.

### Wilson's Summer White House

President Woodrow Wilson spent the summer of 1916 at **Shadow Lawn**, a 52-room mansion in West Long Branch, Monmouth County, New Jersey -- just miles from where the attacks occurred. He rented the estate as his "Summer White House" and used it as a base for his re-election campaign.

- **September 2, 1916:** Wilson delivered his renomination acceptance speech from Shadow Lawn.
- **November 1916:** Wilson narrowly defeated Republican Charles Evans Hughes.
- Some political scientists have argued (controversially) that the shark attacks may have slightly depressed Wilson's vote share in affected New Jersey communities. [NEEDS FACT-CHECK -- the academic paper "Blind Retrospection" from Vanderbilt explores this claim]

## The 1916 Polio Epidemic

### The Crisis

The summer of 1916 saw the worst polio epidemic in American history up to that point:

- **27,000+ cases** across the United States
- **6,000+ deaths** nationwide
- **Over 2,000 deaths** in New York City alone
- Officially declared in **June 1916** (just weeks before the shark attacks)
- Concentrated in New York City, particularly **Brooklyn**

### Public Health Response

The response to polio in 1916 was drastic and often panicked:

- **Quarantine:** Names and addresses of infected individuals published daily in newspapers. Homes were marked with placards. Families were quarantined.
- **Social distancing:** Public gatherings banned. Movie theaters closed. Meetings canceled.
- **Children targeted:** Children warned away from swimming pools, amusement parks, water fountains, and beaches.
- **Fear and stigma:** Immigrant communities, particularly Italian Americans in Brooklyn, were disproportionately blamed.
- **No understanding of transmission:** The virus was poorly understood. No vaccine existed. Doctors didn't know how it spread or why it peaked in summer.

### The Connection to the Shore

The polio epidemic created a paradox that directly set the stage for the shark attacks:

1. Wealthy New Yorkers fled the city for the Jersey Shore, seeking "clean air" and escape from the epidemic.
2. Working-class families also headed to the beaches in unprecedented numbers.
3. More people were in the water along the Jersey Shore in July 1916 than perhaps any previous summer.
4. The very beaches people fled to for safety from one threat became the hunting ground for another.

## Beach Culture in 1916

### The Rise of Recreational Swimming

The early 1900s saw a transformation in Americans' relationship with the ocean:

- **Before ~1900:** The ocean was for work (fishing, shipping) and "bathing" (wading in shallow water for supposed health benefits). True swimming in the surf was rare.
- **1900-1916:** Recreational ocean swimming became increasingly popular, driven by:
  - The rise of seaside resorts catering to the middle class (not just the wealthy)
  - Improved railroad connections to beach towns
  - A cultural shift toward athletic recreation
  - The influence of figures like Annette Kellerman

### Bathing Suits and Modesty

Swimwear in 1916 was undergoing a revolution:

- **Women's suits:** Heavy, wool-knit tunics over bloomers or tights. Annette Kellerman, the Australian swimmer, had been **arrested in the United States in 1909** for wearing a form-fitting one-piece suit. By 1910-1916, her style was becoming more accepted but suits were still heavy, covering arms and legs.
- **Men's suits:** One-piece tank-style suits covering the torso and upper thighs.
- **Jantzen** coined the term "swim suit" in 1915 (replacing "bathing suit") and introduced the Red Diving Girl line.
- **1916 milestone:** The first annual bathing-suit day at New York's Madison Square Garden was held, marking swimming's cultural ascendancy.

### The Jersey Shore as Playground

The Jersey Shore by 1916 was a thriving summer destination:

- **Beach Haven** (Long Beach Island): Home to the elegant Engleside Hotel, built in 1876. A favorite of Philadelphia's upper-middle class.
- **Spring Lake:** Known as the "Irish Riviera" -- a refined resort town with the prestigious Essex & Sussex Hotel. Wealthy clientele from New York and Philadelphia.
- **Long Branch / West Long Branch:** Location of Shadow Lawn (Wilson's Summer White House). One of the most fashionable resorts on the East Coast.
- **Asbury Park:** A major resort destination with boardwalks, hotels, and entertainment.

Tourism was the economic lifeblood of these communities. Everything depended on the summer season.

## What People Knew About Sharks in 1916

### Almost Nothing.

The public and scientific understanding of sharks in 1916 was remarkably limited:

1. **Sharks were considered harmless.** The mainstream scientific view held that sharks lacked the jaw strength or inclination to attack humans in temperate waters. This was not a minority opinion -- it was the consensus at institutions like the American Museum of Natural History.

2. **Hermann Oelrichs' Challenge:** In 1891, millionaire Hermann Oelrichs dove into the ocean alongside a shark to prove its harmlessness. He offered $500 to anyone who could prove a shark had attacked a living person in temperate waters. The prize went unclaimed until his death in 1906. This was cited as definitive proof that sharks were safe.

3. **Alternative Explanations Were Preferred:** When Vansant was attacked, scientists suggested sea turtles, orcas, schools of turtles, or even that the shark was targeting a dog. After Bruder's attack, ichthyologist John Treadwell Nichols's first theory was an orca, not a shark.

4. **No Concept of a "Man-Eater":** The idea that a shark could deliberately prey on humans simply did not exist in American scientific thinking. The concept was considered tabloid sensationalism.

5. **No Understanding of Bull Sharks' Freshwater Capability:** The ability of bull sharks to navigate freshwater was not well documented. The idea of a shark swimming miles up a creek was considered impossible.

### The Scientific Establishment's Role

The scientific establishment's denial actively contributed to the death toll. After the first attack, their dismissal reassured the public. After the second, their equivocation delayed protective measures. Had scientists taken the threat seriously after Beach Haven, the Matawan Creek attacks might have been prevented.

## The Media Landscape

### Newspapers as the Internet of 1916

In 1916, newspapers were the primary (and virtually the only) mass medium:

- No radio broadcasting (commercial radio began in the 1920s)
- No television
- No internet
- News traveled by telegraph, telephone, and mail

Major newspapers that covered the attacks:
- *New York Times*
- *Philadelphia Inquirer*
- *Philadelphia Public Ledger*
- *Washington Post*
- *Boston Herald*
- *Chicago Sun-Times*
- *San Francisco Chronicle*
- *Cleveland Plain Dealer*
- *Kalamazoo Gazette*
- *Daily Alaska Dispatch*
- *Asbury Park Press*
- *Trenton Evening Times*
- *Wilkes-Barre Times Leader*

### The Role of Sensationalism

By 1916, American newspapers were in the era of "yellow journalism" -- sensational, competitive, often exaggerated reporting designed to sell papers:

- **Headlines:** "Bather Cut to Pieces by a Man-Eating Shark" -- the coverage was vivid, gory, and designed to terrify.
- **Fake sightings:** As panic spread, newspapers reported shark sightings up and down the coast -- many of them false or exaggerated.
- **Sunday editions:** From Texas to Michigan, Sunday papers carried shark stories, sightings, and speculation.
- **Conspiracy theories:** The German U-boat theory gained traction in the press.

Michael Capuzzo described the resulting panic as "unrivaled in American history," spreading "along the coasts of New York and New Jersey and spreading by telephone and wireless, letter and postcard."

## The Government Response

### Local

- Resort towns ordered steel wire mesh nets for beaches
- Matawan's Mayor Henderson posted a $100 bounty
- Governor Fielder mandated wire mesh at all swimming areas

### Federal

- **President Wilson** called a Cabinet meeting -- the first time a president addressed a shark threat
- **Treasury Secretary William Gibbs McAdoo** proposed mobilizing the Coast Guard
- Wilson ordered Coast Guard cutters to "fish for the monsters"
- **Congress appropriated $5,000** (~$150,000 today) for shark eradication
- The *Washington Post* headline: **"U.S. War on Sharks"**
- Hugh M. Smith, U.S. Bureau of Fisheries: "The case is extremely unusual. I don't look for it to happen again."

### The Scale of the Hunt

The shark hunt that followed was described as "the largest scale animal hunt in American history":
- Armed citizens with rifles, harpoons, pitchforks, spears, shotguns, and dynamite
- Motor boat patrols with rifles and harpoon guns
- Sheep guts used as bait
- Hundreds of sharks killed
- Mesh barriers installed at beaches
- Bounties posted along the coast

## The Bigger Picture: 1916 in American History

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| **January 1916** | Pancho Villa raids New Mexico; Pershing's Punitive Expedition follows |
| **March 1916** | Wilson wins New Hampshire primary |
| **May 7, 1915** (prior year) | RMS Lusitania sunk by German U-boat |
| **June 1916** | NYC polio epidemic declared |
| **July 1, 1916** | Battle of the Somme begins (same day as Vansant attack) |
| **July 1-12, 1916** | The shark attacks |
| **July 30, 1916** | Black Tom explosion -- German saboteurs destroy a munitions depot on Black Tom Island in New York Harbor. The explosion is felt across NYC and NJ. |
| **September 1916** | Wilson acceptance speech at Shadow Lawn |
| **November 1916** | Wilson narrowly re-elected |
| **April 1917** | United States enters World War I |

The shark attacks occurred in a summer already defined by fear -- fear of disease, fear of war, fear of foreign sabotage. The sharks added a primal, visceral dimension to an already anxious nation.
