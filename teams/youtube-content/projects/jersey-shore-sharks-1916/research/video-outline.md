# Video Outline: "Twelve Days of Terror" -- The 1916 Jersey Shore Shark Attacks

**Target Length:** 8-10 minutes
**Tone:** Serious, cinematic, suspenseful -- true crime/historical documentary style
**Format:** Narration over historical photos, maps, AI-generated scene recreations, and modern footage

---

## COLD OPEN (0:00-0:45)

**Hook: Start in the middle of the action -- Matawan Creek, July 12.**

> VISUAL: Aerial view of a narrow, murky creek winding through a small American town. Summer. 1916.

> NARRATION (paraphrased concept):
> "On July 12, 1916, in a narrow creek eleven miles from the ocean, something impossible happened. A shark -- a creature of the deep sea -- attacked three people in a freshwater stream in the middle of New Jersey. Two of them died. And it was only the latest horror in twelve days that would change America's relationship with the ocean forever."

> CUT TO: Title card. "TWELVE DAYS OF TERROR: The Real Story Behind Jaws."

---

## ACT 1: "SHARKS DON'T ATTACK PEOPLE" (0:45-2:30)

### Scene 1: The World Before (0:45-1:30)

**Establish 1916 America and the myth of the harmless shark.**

- Summer 1916: WWI raging in Europe. Polio epidemic in New York City. Unprecedented heat wave.
- Families fleeing the city for the Jersey Shore beaches.
- Introduce **Hermann Oelrichs** -- the millionaire who dove in with a shark in 1891 and offered $500 to prove they attack humans. Died in 1906, never had to pay.
- Scientists at the American Museum of Natural History: "Sharks are harmless to humans in temperate waters."
- The *Philadelphia Public Ledger*: "Bathers Need Have No Fear of Sharks."

> KEY POINT: Nobody feared sharks. Nobody was prepared for what was coming.

### Scene 2: Beach Haven -- July 1 (1:30-2:30)

**The first attack -- and the first denial.**

- **Charles Vansant**, 25, Philadelphia businessman. Arrives at the Engleside Hotel with his family.
- Goes for a swim before dinner. A dog follows him in.
- The attack: shark seizes his left leg. Lifeguard Alexander Ott and Sheridan Taylor pull him out. The shark follows into ankle-deep water.
- Vansant bleeds to death on the hotel manager's desk at 6:45 PM.
- The response: silence. Scientists blame the dog. Newspapers barely report it. Life goes on.

> KEY POINT: The first fatal shark attack in East Coast history, and everyone looked the other way.

---

## ACT 2: THE HORROR ESCALATES (2:30-4:00)

### Scene 3: Spring Lake -- July 6 (2:30-3:15)

**The attack that could not be denied.**

- **Charles Bruder**, 27, Swiss immigrant and bell captain at the Essex & Sussex Hotel.
- Regular afternoon swim, 130 yards from shore.
- The shark bites his abdomen, severs both legs. The water turns red.
- "A shark bit me! Bit my legs off!" -- his last words.
- Women faint on the beach as his body is brought ashore.
- Hotel guests raise money for his mother in Switzerland.

> KEY POINT: This was too violent, too public, too undeniable to ignore.

### Scene 4: The Scientists Respond (3:15-4:00)

**The experts get it wrong.**

- July 8: Press conference at the American Museum of Natural History.
- Three scientists -- Lucas, Nichols, Murphy -- try to calm the public.
- Nichols suspects an orca, not a shark.
- Others blame sea turtles -- "a school of turtles."
- Dr. Schauffler alone says: "There is not the slightest doubt that a man-eating shark inflicted the injuries."
- Some newspapers suggest "a shark trained by the Germans."
- Steel nets ordered for beaches. Motor boat patrols. But no beach closures.

> KEY POINT: The experts failed. The beaches stayed open. And the shark wasn't done.

---

## ACT 3: THE CREEK (4:00-7:00) -- THE CENTERPIECE

### Scene 5: The Warning (4:00-4:30)

**Captain Cottrell sees the truth -- and no one listens.**

- July 12. Temperature: 96 degrees.
- Retired sea captain Thomas Cottrell crosses the trolley drawbridge over Matawan Creek -- 11 miles from the ocean.
- He sees an 8-foot dark shape moving upstream.
- He calls the police chief. Dismissed.
- He runs through town shouting. People laugh.

> VISUAL: Map showing Matawan Creek's location, the distance from the ocean, the path from Raritan Bay inland.

> KEY POINT: The Cassandra moment. He was right. Nobody listened.

### Scene 6: Lester Stillwell (4:30-5:15)

**An 11-year-old boy is taken.**

- Lester Stillwell: 11 years old, one week from his birthday. Works at the basket factory with his dad. Has epilepsy.
- His father lets him leave work early because of the heat.
- Six boys swimming at Wyckoff Dock. Ally O'Hara feels something "like sandpaper" graze his leg.
- They see what looks like an old log. Then: Lester is seized. Dragged under. Surfaces screaming. Gone.
- The boys run through town, naked, muddy, terrified.

> KEY POINT: A shark, in a creek, miles from the sea, has taken a child.

### Scene 7: Stanley Fisher -- The Hero (5:15-6:15)

**The most dramatic moment of the entire story.**

- Stanley Fisher, 24. Tailor. Choir singer. Beloved in Matawan.
- Hears the boys screaming. Thinks Lester is having a seizure.
- Recruits two men. They dive into the dark, murky creek.
- Thirty minutes of searching. Arthur Smith is scraped by the shark.
- Fisher makes one final dive. He finds Lester's body on the bottom. Grabs his leg. Starts to pull him up.
- The shark strikes Fisher's right thigh. Hip to knee. Ten pounds of flesh torn away. Femoral artery severed.
- Fisher fights the shark. Dragged under twice. Freed when a deputy hits the shark with an oar.
- His last words on the operating table: **"I found the boy. He is on the bottom."**
- Fisher dies at 6:45 PM.

> KEY POINT: This is the heart of the documentary. A man walked into certain danger to save a child. He found the boy, and the shark found him.

### Scene 8: Joseph Dunn -- The Survivor (6:15-6:45)

**Half a mile away, thirty minutes later.**

- Joseph Dunn, 12, from Manhattan. Visiting relatives. No idea what's happening upstream.
- Swimming with his brother Michael and friend Jerry. Someone shouts "SHARK."
- Joseph is last to the ladder. The shark seizes his left leg.
- His brother and friend grab his arms. A tug-of-war with a shark.
- "I felt my leg going down the shark's throat -- I thought it would swallow me."
- Brickyard supervisor Robert Thress helps pull him free.
- Joseph survives. Released from the hospital 59 days later. Never speaks publicly about it again. Dies in 1982.

> KEY POINT: The sole survivor. The lucky one.

### Scene 9: The Catch (6:45-7:00)

**Quick beat: July 14. The shark is caught.**

- Michael Schleisser -- Harlem taxidermist and Barnum & Bailey lion tamer.
- Fishing in Raritan Bay. A 7.5-foot, 325-pound shark tangles in his net and attacks his boat.
- He kills it with a broken oar.
- Inside: 15 pounds of human remains. Arm bones. A rib.
- He mounts it in a Broadway shop window.
- The attacks stop.

---

## ACT 4: THE AFTERMATH AND LEGACY (7:00-9:00)

### Scene 10: The War on Sharks (7:00-7:30)

**America declares war on a species.**

- President Wilson calls a Cabinet meeting. Coast Guard deployed.
- Congress appropriates $5,000. *Washington Post*: "U.S. War on Sharks."
- Armed mobs with rifles, harpoons, pitchforks, dynamite.
- Hundreds of sharks killed. Most had nothing in their stomachs.
- Resort owners lose $250,000 ($7.4 million today). Sunbathing drops 75%.

### Scene 11: The Great Debate (7:30-8:15)

**We still don't know what did it.**

- The caught shark was identified as a juvenile great white.
- But Matawan Creek was brackish water -- great whites avoid freshwater.
- Bull sharks can navigate freshwater rivers. They've been found 2,500 miles up the Amazon.
- Joseph Dunn's bite pattern was more consistent with a bull shark.
- Was it one shark or multiple? One species or two?
- George Burgess: "The species involved has always been doubtful and likely will continue to generate spirited debate."
- The specimen is lost. No DNA. No definitive answer. Ever.

### Scene 12: The Birth of Shark Fear -- and Jaws (8:15-9:00)

**How twelve days in 1916 changed everything.**

- Before 1916: sharks were harmless. After 1916: man-eating monsters.
- 1974: Peter Benchley publishes *Jaws*. He'd researched the 1916 attacks.
- 1975: Spielberg's film becomes the first summer blockbuster. The Jaws Effect.
- The parallels: a shark terrorizing a beach town; officials refusing to close beaches to protect tourism; a colorful hunter catching the killer.
- But Benchley came to regret it: "I couldn't write Jaws today."
- "The shark in an updated Jaws could not be the villain; it would have to be written as the victim."
- Today: humans kill ~100 million sharks per year. Sharks kill ~5 humans per year.

---

## CLOSING (9:00-9:30)

**Return to Matawan Creek. The memorial. The graves.**

> VISUAL: Modern Matawan Creek. The memorial plaque on Dock Street. The stone monument on Main Street. Rosehill Cemetery.

> NARRATION (paraphrased concept):
> "In the summer of 1916, in twelve terrible days, a shark -- or sharks -- killed four people along the Jersey Shore. They also killed a myth: the myth that the ocean was safe. That myth has never been restored. Lester Stillwell was eleven years old. Stanley Fisher was twenty-four. They lie side by side in Rosehill Cemetery in Matawan, New Jersey. A boy who loved to swim. And the man who died trying to save him."

> END CARD: Dedication to the victims. Links to sources.

## PRODUCTION NOTES

### Tone
- Serious and respectful. These were real people who died.
- Suspenseful but not exploitative.
- The Matawan Creek sequence should build like a thriller -- from Cottrell's ignored warning, through the boys swimming, to the escalating horror.
- Stanley Fisher's story is the emotional climax. His heroism is the heart of the documentary.

### Music
- Period-appropriate underscore for 1916 scenes (ragtime era, but somber)
- Building tension for the attack sequences
- Quiet, reflective piano for the closing

### Narration Style
- Direct, unflinching, factual. Let the story's inherent drama carry the weight.
- Use exact quotes where available ("A shark bit me! Bit my legs off!" / "I found the boy. He is on the bottom.")
- Avoid modern slang or casual tone. This deserves gravity.

### Visual Approach
- Historical photos and newspaper scans for establishing shots
- AI-generated scene recreations for key moments (realistic, period-accurate, muted palette)
- Maps and animated graphics for geographic context
- Modern footage of the memorial sites for the closing

### Potential Titles
1. "Twelve Days of Terror: The Real Story Behind Jaws"
2. "The Summer the Shore Ran Red: 1916"
3. "Before Jaws: The True Story of America's First Shark Panic"
4. "The Man-Eater of Matawan Creek"
5. "Blood in the Water: The 1916 Shark Attacks That Changed America"
