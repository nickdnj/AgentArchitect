# Victims, Heroes, and Key Figures of the 1916 Shark Attacks

## The Victims

### 1. Charles Epting Vansant (1891-1916) -- FIRST VICTIM

**Personal Details:**
- **Age:** 25 (some sources say 23 or 28; most reliable sources say 25)
- **Residence:** 4038 Spruce Street, Philadelphia, PA
- **Occupation:** Businessman (recent University of Pennsylvania graduate)
- **Family:** Father Dr. Eugene L. Vansant (otolaryngologist), sisters Eugenia and Louise
- **Character:** Athletic (college golf and baseball teams), outgoing personality

**The Attack:**
- **Date:** Saturday, July 1, 1916
- **Location:** Beach Haven, Long Beach Island, NJ (in front of the Engleside Hotel)
- **Time:** Approximately 5:30 PM
- **Circumstances:** Had just arrived at the Engleside Hotel at 5:00 PM with his family for vacation. Went for a quick swim before dinner while his family unpacked. A Chesapeake Bay Retriever followed him into the water.
- **Injuries:** Left thigh stripped of flesh, bone exposed from hip to knee
- **Death:** Bled to death on the manager's desk at the Engleside Hotel at 6:45 PM

**Rescuers:**
- Lifeguard **Alexander Ott**
- Bystander **Sheridan Taylor**
- Together they formed a human chain to pull Vansant from the water. The shark reportedly followed them into water so shallow its belly scraped the sand.

**Significance:** First recorded fatal shark attack along the East Coast of the United States. His death was widely dismissed or downplayed by scientists and the press. PA Fish Commissioner James M. Meehan suggested the shark had been targeting the dog, not Vansant.

### 2. Charles Bruder (1888-1916) -- SECOND VICTIM

**Personal Details:**
- **Age:** 27 (some sources say 28)
- **Born:** Switzerland
- **Occupation:** Bell captain at the Essex & Sussex Hotel, Spring Lake, NJ
- **Background:** Former Swiss Army soldier; Swiss immigrant working at a prestigious seaside resort
- **Family:** Mother lived in Switzerland; no mention of other family in the US

**The Attack:**
- **Date:** Thursday, July 6, 1916
- **Location:** Spring Lake, NJ (45 miles north of Beach Haven)
- **Time:** Approximately 2:30 PM
- **Circumstances:** On his regular afternoon break, Bruder went for his routine lunchtime swim. He was a strong swimmer who ventured approximately 130 yards (120 meters) from shore.
- **Injuries:** Shark bit his abdomen and severed both legs -- left leg above the knee, right leg below the knee. Water turned visibly red from shore.
- **Final Words:** "A shark bit me! Bit my legs off!"
- **Death:** Died from blood loss before the lifeboat could reach shore.

**Rescuers:**
- Lifeguards **Chris Anderson** and **George White** rowed out in a lifeboat

**Aftermath:**
- Women on the beach fainted at the sight of his mutilated body
- Hotel guests and workers raised money for his mother in Switzerland
- **Burial:** Atlantic View Cemetery, Manasquan, NJ

**Significance:** This attack could not be dismissed. It triggered the first real public panic and the first scientific response (July 8 press conference at the American Museum of Natural History). The phrase "the nail in the coffin for the summer tourist season" was used to describe its economic impact.

### 3. Lester Stillwell (1904-1916) -- THIRD VICTIM (Matawan Creek)

**Personal Details:**
- **Age:** 11 years old (one week from his 12th birthday)
- **Born:** 1904
- **Residence:** Matawan, NJ
- **Parents:** William and Louella Stillwell
- **Occupation:** Child laborer at the Anderson Basket Factory (worked alongside his father)
- **Health:** Suffered from epilepsy (tonic-clonic seizures) -- this detail proved tragically significant, as adults who heard the boys' warnings initially assumed Lester was having a seizure, not being attacked by a shark
- **Best Friend:** Albert "Ally" O'Hara (also worked at the basket factory)

**The Attack:**
- **Date:** Wednesday, July 12, 1916
- **Location:** Wyckoff Dock, Matawan Creek, approximately 11 miles inland from the open ocean
- **Time:** Approximately 2:05 PM
- **Temperature:** 96 degrees Fahrenheit
- **Circumstances:** Father let him leave work early due to the heat. He went swimming with friends at their usual spot -- a deep section of the creek near the remains of an old dock. He was attacked while in the water with five other boys.
- **Death:** Dragged underwater by the shark. Surfaced briefly, screaming, then pulled under again. Body not recovered until July 14 at 5:30 AM.

**Friends Present at Attack:**
- Albert "Ally" O'Hara (felt "sandpaper" sensation on his leg just before the attack)
- Charles Van Brunt
- Johnson Cartan (parents owned Cartan's Department Store, 92 Main Street)
- Frank Close
- Anthony Bubblin (was executing a backflip off the dock at the moment of the attack)

**Body Recovery:**
- July 14, 5:30 AM, by train conductor **Harry Van Cleaf**, found floating near the railroad trestle, approximately 150 feet west of Wyckoff Dock

**Burial:** Rosehill Cemetery, Matawan, NJ (buried July 15, 1916)

### 4. Watson Stanley Fisher (1892-1916) -- FOURTH VICTIM / THE HERO

**Personal Details:**
- **Age:** 24
- **Born:** April 12, 1892
- **Residence:** Matawan, NJ
- **Occupation:** Owner of a dry cleaning and tailoring shop on Main Street, Matawan
- **Character:** Described as well-known and well-loved in Matawan. Sang in the choir at the Episcopal Church on Main Street. Natural leader, athlete.

**The Attack:**
- **Date:** Wednesday, July 12, 1916
- **Location:** Wyckoff Dock, Matawan Creek (same site as Stillwell attack)
- **Time:** Approximately 2:40 PM (attacked); 6:45 PM (death)
- **Circumstances:** When panicked boys ran past his shop screaming about a shark, Fisher initially believed Lester Stillwell was having a seizure. He recruited Red Burlew and Arthur Smith and dove into the murky creek to search for the boy.
- After ~30 minutes of searching in dark, silty water, Fisher made one final dive. In waist-deep water, he found Lester's body on the creek bottom and began pulling it up.
- The shark seized Fisher's right thigh, tearing a wound from hip to knee, removing at least 10 pounds of flesh, and severing his femoral artery.
- Fisher fought the shark, which dragged him underwater twice before Deputy Arthur Van Buskirk struck the shark with an oar.
- **Reported Last Words:** "I found the boy. He is on the bottom."

**Medical Response:**
- Dr. **George C. Reynolds** treated his femoral wound at the scene
- 5:06 PM: Placed on train to Long Branch
- 5:30 PM: Surgery at Monmouth Memorial Hospital by Dr. **Edwin Field**
- 6:45 PM: Died from blood loss and shock

**Legacy:**
- His family used $7,500 from a life insurance policy to commission a stained glass window for the Methodist Church (some sources say Episcopal Church) on Main Street, dedicated July 1918. The window was damaged by a local blast five months later.
- When the church was demolished in the 1970s, the window was auctioned to a private collector. Its current location is unknown.
- 100th anniversary memorial dedicated in Matawan in 2016.

**Burial:** Rosehill Cemetery, Matawan, NJ (buried July 15, 1916, alongside Lester Stillwell)

### 5. Joseph James Dunn (1902-1982) -- FIFTH VICTIM / THE SURVIVOR

**Personal Details:**
- **Age:** 12 (some sources say 14) [NEEDS FACT-CHECK -- Find a Grave lists birth year as 1902, making him 14 in 1916]
- **Born:** 1902
- **Residence:** Upper Manhattan, New York City (visiting relatives in the Matawan area)
- **Family:** Brother Michael Dunn (14), friend Jerry Hourihan
- **Wife:** Eleanor (died 1972)
- **Death:** April 1982, age 79

**The Attack:**
- **Date:** Wednesday, July 12, 1916
- **Location:** Brickyard Dock (NJ Clay Company), approximately half a mile downstream from Wyckoff Dock, Matawan Creek
- **Time:** Approximately 2:35 PM (roughly 30 minutes after Fisher's attack)
- **Circumstances:** Swimming with his brother and friend. Had no knowledge of the attacks upstream. When someone shouted about a shark, Joseph was farthest from the ladder. The shark seized his left leg as he climbed out.
- **The Rescue:** His brother Michael and friend Jerry engaged in a tug-of-war with the shark, pulling Joseph's arms. Brickyard supervisor **Robert Thress** and fisherman **Jacob Lefferts** also helped pull him free.
- **Injuries:** Left leg severely mangled, stripped of flesh, but not amputated.
- **Joseph's Description:** "I felt my leg going down the shark's throat -- I thought it would swallow me... It was like a big pair of scissors."

**Medical Treatment:**
- Treated initially by Dr. **H.S. Cooley** at the scene
- Transported to **St. Peter's Hospital**, New Brunswick, NJ
- Released **September 15, 1916** (59 days after the attack)

**Later Life:**
- Lived a long, quiet life. Rarely discussed the attacks.
- Never gave public interviews about the incident.
- Married Eleanor (who died 1972).
- "Those who knew him said he always considered himself a lucky man."
- Died April 1982 at age 79.

**Significance:** The sole survivor of the five attacks. His injury pattern (the type of bite on his leg) was later cited as evidence supporting the bull shark theory, as the bite characteristics were more consistent with bull shark dentition.

## Key Figures (Non-Victims)

### Captain Thomas Cottrell -- The Man Who Tried to Warn Them
- Retired sea captain and Matawan resident
- **1:30 PM, July 12:** Spotted the shark from the trolley drawbridge over Matawan Creek
- Called police chief Mulsoff, who dismissed it
- Ran through town shouting warnings -- no one believed him
- Later helped transport Joseph Dunn to medical care
- A tragic Cassandra figure -- he saw the danger and no one listened

### Michael Schleisser -- The Man Who Caught the Shark
- Harlem-based taxidermist and **Barnum and Bailey Circus lion tamer**
- **July 14, 1916:** Fishing with partner John Murphy in Raritan Bay, approximately 4 miles from the mouth of Matawan Creek
- Their boat was jerked backward when the shark became entangled in their net
- Killed the shark with a broken oar handle
- The shark: **7.5 feet long, 325 pounds**, identified as a juvenile great white
- Stomach contained **15 pounds** of "suspicious fleshy material and bones" (filling two-thirds of a milk crate)
- Dr. Frederic Lucas confirmed the remains were human: arm bones and a rib
- Schleisser taxidermied the shark and displayed it in a Broadway shop window in Manhattan
- After his catch, no further attacks occurred

### Dr. Frederic Augustus Lucas -- Director, American Museum of Natural History
- Led the scientific response to the attacks
- Participated in the July 8 press conference at the museum
- Examined the contents of Schleisser's shark's stomach
- Confirmed the remains were human: "They are the parts of the left radius and ulna [lower arm bones] and one of the anterior left ribs, all human. There is no doubt about this."

### John Treadwell Nichols (1883-1958) -- Ichthyologist
- Assistant curator of the Department of Recent Fishes at the American Museum of Natural History
- The only ichthyologist among the three panelists at the July 8 press conference
- Conducted what would become **the first scientific investigation of a man killed by a fish in American history**
- Initially declared the attacker was NOT a shark -- his leading suspect was an **orca (killer whale)**
- Later revised his views after accumulating evidence
- Warned swimmers to stay close to shore and use netted areas

### Robert Cushman Murphy -- Naturalist
- Third panelist at the July 8 press conference at AMNH
- Along with Lucas and Nichols, tried to calm public panic

### Hermann Oelrichs (1850-1906) -- The Millionaire Who Bet Against Sharks
- Wealthy businessman, owner of Norddeutsche Lloyd shipping line
- In 1891, dove into the ocean alongside a shark to prove they're harmless
- Offered **$500** (approx. $18,000 today) to anyone who could prove a shark had attacked a living person in temperate waters
- Died in 1906. The reward went unclaimed.
- Had he lived ten more years, he would have had to pay up.

### President Woodrow Wilson
- Spending the summer at Shadow Lawn in West Long Branch, NJ (his Summer White House)
- Called a **Cabinet meeting** to address the shark crisis
- Ordered Coast Guard cutters to patrol the shore
- Supported Congressional appropriation of **$5,000** for shark eradication
- The *Washington Post* headline: **"U.S. War on Sharks"**

### Governor James Fielder (New Jersey)
- "Virtually mandated" steel wire mesh enclose all popular swimming locations

### Mayor Arris B. Henderson (Matawan)
- Posted **$100 reward** for killing the shark after the Matawan Creek attacks

## Other Named Individuals

**Rescuers and Witnesses at Matawan Creek:**
- **Red Burlew** -- Assisted Fisher in searching for Stillwell
- **Arthur Smith** -- Assisted Fisher; scraped on abdomen by the shark (drew blood, left permanent scar)
- **Johnny Smith** -- Fisher's assistant; witnessed the attacks
- **Arthur Van Buskirk** -- Monmouth County deputy; struck the shark with an oar to free Fisher
- **George Smith** -- Freehold detective, present at the scene
- **Robert Thress** -- Brickyard supervisor who pulled Joseph Dunn from the shark
- **Jacob Lefferts** -- Fisherman who helped rescue Joseph Dunn
- **John Mulsoff** -- Town barber and police chief who dismissed Captain Cottrell's warning

**Medical Professionals:**
- **Dr. George C. Reynolds** -- Treated Fisher's femoral wound at the scene
- **Dr. Edwin Field** -- Surgeon at Monmouth Memorial Hospital who operated on Fisher
- **Dr. H.S. Cooley** -- Treated Joseph Dunn
- **Dr. William G. Schauffler** -- NJ physician who correctly declared a shark was responsible after the Bruder attack
- **James M. Meehan** -- PA Fish Commissioner who dismissed the Vansant attack as the shark targeting a dog

**Scientists:**
- **Hugh M. Smith** -- U.S. Bureau of Fisheries official who stated: "The case is extremely unusual. I don't look for it to happen again."

**Boys Swimming with Lester Stillwell:**
- Albert "Ally" O'Hara (best friend)
- Charles Van Brunt
- Johnson Cartan
- Frank Close
- Anthony Bubblin

**Teens Who Spotted the Shark:**
- Harold Conover (18)
- Ralph Gall (18)
- John Tassini (18)
