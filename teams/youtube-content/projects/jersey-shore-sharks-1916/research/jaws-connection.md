# From Matawan Creek to Amity Island: The Jaws Connection

## The Direct Line from 1916 to 1975

The 1916 Jersey Shore shark attacks are widely cited as the primary inspiration for Peter Benchley's 1974 novel *Jaws* and Steven Spielberg's 1975 blockbuster film adaptation. The parallels are striking, though Benchley himself gave mixed signals about the connection throughout his life.

---

## Peter Benchley and the Genesis of Jaws

### Benchley's Background

Peter Bradford Benchley (1940-2006) was the grandson of humorist Robert Benchley. He worked as a journalist and a speechwriter for President Lyndon B. Johnson before turning to fiction.

### How the Idea Developed

Benchley said he had been "thinking for years about a story about a shark that attacks people and what would happen if it came in and wouldn't go away." The concept crystallized from multiple real-life sources:

1. **The 1916 Attacks:** Benchley acknowledged the Jersey Shore attacks were part of his research and influenced the novel's atmosphere. He "had done some looking back at the attacks" and found them "inspirational in terms of getting the feeling of the social terror."

2. **Frank Mundus (1964):** Benchley cited a 1964 news story about Long Island fisherman Frank Mundus, who caught a great white shark weighing 4,550 pounds off Montauk Point, as a direct catalyst. Mundus became the primary inspiration for the character of Quint, the grizzled shark hunter.

3. **Victor Coppleson's "Rogue Shark" Theory:** The mid-century idea that individual sharks could develop a taste for human flesh -- with the 1916 attacks as a key example -- provided the theoretical framework for Jaws' central premise.

### Benchley's Mixed Statements on 1916

Benchley's position on the 1916 connection evolved over time:

- He acknowledged researching the 1916 attacks and finding them atmospheric inspiration
- He emphasized that the 1964 Mundus catch was the more direct trigger
- The Matawan Historical Society website notes that "Benchley himself stated they did not inspire the work" -- a flat denial
- Yet multiple Benchley biographers and literary scholars cite the 1916 events as foundational to the novel

The most balanced interpretation: the 1916 attacks provided the **thematic DNA** (a rogue shark terrorizing a coastal community, officials refusing to act, escalating panic) while the Mundus catch provided the **narrative hook** (the hunt for a single massive predator).

## Parallel by Parallel: 1916 vs. Jaws

### The Attacks

| Real 1916 Events | Jaws (Novel/Film) |
|---|---|
| A swimmer is attacked and killed near a beach resort (Vansant, Beach Haven) | Chrissie Watkins is attacked and killed while swimming near Amity Island |
| A dog is present near the first victim | A dog disappears near the beach (implied shark victim) |
| A second swimmer is killed in the same area (Bruder, Spring Lake) | Alex Kintner, a young boy, is killed on a crowded beach |
| A child is attacked in shallow water (Stillwell, Matawan Creek) | Multiple attacks target beach-goers in shallow water |
| Five attacks over 12 days | Multiple attacks over a compressed summer timeline |

### The Cover-Up

| Real 1916 Events | Jaws (Novel/Film) |
|---|---|
| After Vansant's death, hotel owners and officials downplayed the danger to protect tourism | Mayor Larry Vaughn refuses to close beaches to protect Amity's "summer dollars" |
| The *Philadelphia Public Ledger* printed: "Bathers Need Have No Fear of Sharks" | Mayor Vaughn insists the beaches are safe |
| Scientists blamed sea turtles, orcas, and "the dog" instead of sharks | Officials offer alternative explanations to avoid panic |
| Tourism was the lifeblood of Jersey Shore communities | Amity Island's economy depends entirely on summer tourism |
| Economic pressure prevented early beach closures | Economic pressure prevents early beach closures |

### The Denial of Science

| Real 1916 Events | Jaws (Novel/Film) |
|---|---|
| Scientists at the AMNH insisted sharks couldn't attack humans in temperate waters | The expert (Hooper) is initially dismissed by authorities |
| Dr. Schauffler correctly identified a shark; was ignored | Chief Brody wants to close the beaches; is overruled |
| Captain Cottrell warned Matawan about the shark; was laughed at | Warnings are ignored by officials |

### The Hunt

| Real 1916 Events | Jaws (Novel/Film) |
|---|---|
| Michael Schleisser, a colorful taxidermist and lion tamer, catches the shark | Quint, a colorful and eccentric shark hunter, leads the hunt |
| Frank Mundus, the real-life shark fisherman Benchley admired | Direct inspiration for Quint's character |
| Coast Guard and armed citizens patrol the coast | Fishing boats flood Amity's waters after a bounty is posted |
| Bounties posted ($100 in Matawan) | A $3,000 bounty is posted |
| Shark is caught and stomach contents examined | The shark is ultimately killed; Brody fires into the compressed air tank |

### The Setting

| Real 1916 Events | Jaws (Novel/Film) |
|---|---|
| Jersey Shore beach towns (Beach Haven, Spring Lake) | Amity Island, a fictional Long Island beach community |
| Summer tourism season, July | Summer tourism season, around July 4th |
| The real communities were small seaside resorts | Amity is a small, picturesque island community |
| Fourth of July was approaching during the attacks | The attacks climax around the Fourth of July holiday |

## Key Differences: Fiction vs. Reality

1. **Number of Sharks:** The novel features one enormous great white; the real events may have involved multiple sharks of different species.

2. **Scale of the Shark:** Jaws' shark is described as 25 feet long; the caught 1916 shark was 7.5 feet.

3. **The Creek Attack:** While the Matawan Creek inland attack is the most dramatic real event, Jaws does not include a freshwater/creek attack. This is arguably the most compelling real detail that was NOT used.

4. **The Hero:** Stanley Fisher's self-sacrificing rescue attempt -- diving into a creek to recover a boy's body and being fatally attacked -- has no direct parallel in Jaws. (This is arguably more heroic and dramatic than anything in the novel.)

5. **The Resolution:** In real life, the shark was caught by accident in a fishing net. In Jaws, the shark is hunted and killed in a dramatic sea battle.

6. **The Science:** The novel portrays the shark as a calculated predator; in reality, the attacks were likely opportunistic.

7. **Location:** Jaws is set on a New England island (filmed on Martha's Vineyard, MA); the real attacks occurred along the New Jersey coast.

## Spielberg's Film (1975)

### Production Details
- **Director:** Steven Spielberg
- **Based on:** Peter Benchley's 1974 novel
- **Filmed on:** Martha's Vineyard, Massachusetts (standing in for fictional Amity Island)
- **Why Martha's Vineyard:** Spielberg chose it because he could "go 12 miles out to sea and still have a sandy bottom only 30 feet below the surface" and "I didn't want to see land." Long Island was considered "too busy."
- **Released:** June 20, 1975
- **Impact:** Became the first "summer blockbuster," earning $472 million worldwide

### Film's Direct References to 1916

In the film, there is a scene where Matt Hooper (Richard Dreyfuss) and Chief Brody (Roy Scheider) discuss the history of shark attacks. While the 1916 attacks are not explicitly named, the pattern of the story -- a shark attacking a resort community, officials refusing to close beaches, and a hunt to destroy the predator -- directly mirrors the 1916 events.

## Benchley's Regret and the Legacy of Fear

### The Consequences of Jaws

The film created what some scientists call a "Jaws Effect" -- a surge in fear and hatred toward sharks that led to:

- Mass shark hunting for sport
- Dramatic declines in shark populations worldwide
- A cultural perception of sharks as malevolent, calculating predators
- Ongoing public fear disproportionate to the actual risk of shark attacks

### Benchley's Evolution

Peter Benchley came to deeply regret the novel's impact on shark populations:

**1995 (Popular Science):** "The extensive new knowledge of sharks would make it impossible for me to create, in good conscience, a villain of the magnitude and malignity of the original. **I couldn't write Jaws today.**"

**2000 (Animal Attack Files):** "What I now know, which wasn't known when I wrote Jaws, is that **there is no such thing as a rogue shark which develops a taste for human flesh.**"

**Later Statement:** "The shark in an updated Jaws could not be the villain; **it would have to be written as the victim**, for worldwide, sharks are much more the oppressed than the oppressors."

### Conservation Advocacy

In his later years, Benchley became a passionate advocate for shark conservation and marine protection. He and his wife Wendy traveled the world speaking with scientists and conservationists, lending their resources and fame to preserving the animals that had made him famous. The **Peter Benchley Ocean Awards** were established in his memory to honor ocean conservation efforts.

Benchley died on February 11, 2006.

---

## The Irony

The 1916 attacks created the first American shark panic. Sixty years later, *Jaws* amplified that fear a thousandfold. And the man who amplified it spent the last decade of his life trying to undo the damage.

The real sharks of 1916 -- whatever species they were -- killed four people and created a fear that has killed millions of sharks. As of today, humans kill approximately **100 million sharks per year**. Sharks kill approximately **5 humans per year**.
