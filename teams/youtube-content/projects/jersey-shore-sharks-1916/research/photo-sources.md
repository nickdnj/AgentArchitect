- **Source:** Bronx Home News (1916) — widely reproduced
- **URL:** Available via AllThatInteresting.com, Wikipedia references, various historical articles
- **License:** Public Domain (pre-1926 newspaper photograph)
- **Topic:** The man who caught the suspected shark (July 14, 1916)
- **Notes:** THE iconic image of the 1916 attacks. Shows Schleisser posing with the 7.5-foot, 325-pound great white shark he caught in Raritan Bay. The shark was gutted and found to contain "suspicious fleshy materials and bones" weighing 15 pounds. Scientists identified it as a young great white that had ingested human remains. The shark was mounted in Schleisser's taxidermy shop but has since been lost — this photograph is the only surviving visual record.

## 4. The Shark

### Schleisser's Shark — Bronx Home News Photo
- **Source:** Bronx Home News, July 1916
- **URL:** Widely reproduced; search "Michael Schleisser 1916 shark" for high-res versions
- **License:** Public Domain (pre-1926 newspaper)
- **Topic:** The 7.5-foot great white shark caught July 14, 1916
- **Notes:** THE only photograph of the shark believed responsible. Schleisser caught it while fishing in Raritan Bay with a friend. He claimed the shark nearly sank his boat until he killed it with a broken oar. The shark was mounted and displayed in his taxidermy shop window but has since been lost. This is the sole surviving visual record.

## 9. The Jaws Connection

### Peter Benchley & Jaws
- **Topic:** Connection between 1916 attacks and the novel/film
- **Notes:** In 2001, Benchley denied the 1916 attacks directly inspired Jaws, claiming his primary inspiration was Frank Mundus catching a 4,550-pound great white off Montauk in 1964. However, the parallels are undeniable: rogue shark targeting a coastal community, attacks in an estuary (Matawan Creek / fictional Amity), a mayor denying the threat, and the fictional ichthyologist Matt Hooper explicitly references the 1916 attacks in the film.
- **License Status:** Benchley's photos, the Jaws novel cover, and the movie poster are all copyrighted. The movie poster uses the famous "shark rising from below" image — DO NOT USE without license.
- **Possible Public Domain:** Benchley died February 12, 2006. Some interviews and public appearances may be available under fair use for documentary commentary.

### Jaws Novel Cover
- **License:** COPYRIGHTED — Universal / Bantam Books
- **Notes:** Reference only. The iconic cover image of the shark rising toward the swimmer is one of the most recognizable images in pop culture but is fully copyrighted.

## 10. Scientific / Educational

### Great White Shark vs. Bull Shark Debate
- **Topic:** Which species committed the attacks?
- **Notes:** The 1916 debate is a key documentary story point. In 1916, the captured shark was identified as a great white. Modern scientists debate whether the Matawan Creek attacks were committed by a bull shark (Carcharhinus leucas), which can survive in freshwater. The beach attacks at Beach Haven and Spring Lake were more consistent with a great white. It's possible multiple sharks were involved.
