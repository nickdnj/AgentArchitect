# The 1916 Jersey Shore Shark Attacks: Complete Narrative

## The Twelve Days of Terror

Between July 1 and July 12, 1916, a series of shark attacks along the New Jersey coast killed four people and critically injured a fifth, triggering the first mass shark panic in American history. These attacks -- unprecedented in U.S. waters -- shattered the scientific consensus that sharks were harmless to humans, devastated Jersey Shore tourism, prompted a federal response from President Woodrow Wilson, and ultimately inspired Peter Benchley's novel *Jaws* nearly sixty years later.

## Setting the Stage: America in the Summer of 1916

The summer of 1916 was one of extremes. A devastating polio epidemic had seized New York City, ultimately infecting 27,000 Americans and killing over 6,000 -- more than 2,000 in New York City alone. Children were warned away from pools, parks, and public gatherings. The wealthy fled the city for the coast, and working-class families packed the Jersey Shore beaches seeking escape from the heat and the disease.

Meanwhile, World War I raged in Europe. America had not yet entered the conflict, but German U-boats prowled the Atlantic -- the RMS Lusitania had been sunk just a year earlier. A preparedness movement gripped the nation. President Woodrow Wilson was spending the summer at Shadow Lawn, a 52-room mansion in West Long Branch, New Jersey, just miles from where the attacks would occur, using it as his Summer White House while planning his re-election campaign.

The summer was brutally hot. Temperatures regularly climbed into the 90s, and the water was unusually warm -- conditions that likely drew sharks further north than usual. Air conditioning didn't exist. The beach was the only relief.

And in 1916, virtually no one feared sharks.

## The Myth of the Harmless Shark

Before these attacks, the prevailing scientific view was that sharks simply did not attack humans in temperate North American waters. This wasn't fringe thinking -- it was mainstream science. In 1891, millionaire businessman Hermann Oelrichs was so confident in sharks' harmlessness that he dove into the ocean alongside one to prove it to his friends, then publicly offered a $500 reward (roughly $18,000 today) to anyone who could provide proof that a shark had ever attacked a living person in temperate waters. By the time Oelrichs died in 1906, the prize had gone unclaimed.

Scientists at the American Museum of Natural History, including director Frederic Augustus Lucas and assistant curator of fishes John Treadwell Nichols, firmly held that sharks lacked the jaw power to seriously injure a human. The Philadelphia Public Ledger ran the headline: **"Bathers Need Have No Fear of Sharks."**

This was the world into which the first attack came.

## Attack 1: Beach Haven -- July 1, 1916

Charles Epting Vansant, 25, arrived at the Engleside Hotel in Beach Haven, on the southern end of Long Beach Island, at 5:00 PM on Saturday, July 1, 1916. He had traveled from his family home at 4038 Spruce Street, Philadelphia, with his father Dr. Eugene L. Vansant (an otolaryngologist) and his sisters Eugenia and Louise.

Charles was a University of Pennsylvania graduate, known for his athleticism -- he'd played on the golf and baseball teams -- and his outgoing personality. While his family unpacked, he changed into his bathing suit and headed for the beach for a quick swim before dinner. A Chesapeake Bay Retriever was playing along the shoreline and followed him into the water.

Shortly after entering the surf, Vansant began shouting. Bathers on the beach thought he was calling to the dog. He was not. A shark had seized his left leg.

Lifeguard Alexander Ott and bystander Sheridan Taylor rushed into the water and formed a human chain to pull Vansant to shore. Witnesses claimed the shark followed them into water so shallow its belly scraped the sand before finally releasing its grip. The flesh of Vansant's left thigh had been stripped away, exposing the bone from hip to knee.

Vansant was carried to the manager's desk in the Engleside Hotel lobby. He bled to death at 6:45 PM before an ambulance could arrive.

It was the first recorded fatal shark attack along the East Coast of the United States.

**The Response:** Almost nothing. Scientists and the press reluctantly mentioned a shark. The New York Times reported Vansant "was badly bitten in the surf ... by a fish, presumably a shark." Pennsylvania Fish Commissioner James M. Meehan suggested in the Philadelphia Public Ledger that the shark had been chasing the dog and bit Vansant by mistake. Life along the Jersey Shore continued as normal.

## Attack 2: Spring Lake -- July 6, 1916

Five days later and 45 miles north, the shark struck again.

Charles Bruder, 27, was a Swiss immigrant and former Swiss Army soldier who worked as the bell captain at the prestigious Essex & Sussex Hotel in Spring Lake. On Thursday, July 6, during his afternoon break, Bruder went for his regular lunchtime swim, venturing about 130 yards (120 meters) from shore.

At approximately 2:30 PM, a shark attacked Bruder with devastating force. It bit his abdomen and severed both legs -- the left above the knee, the right below. The water turned crimson with his blood.

Lifeguards Chris Anderson and George White rowed out in a lifeboat. When they reached Bruder, he cried out: "A shark bit me! Bit my legs off!" He lost consciousness and died from blood loss before they could bring him ashore.

The scene on the beach was pandemonium. The New York Times reported that "women [were] panic-stricken [and fainted] as [Bruder's] mutilated body ... [was] brought ashore." Guests and workers at the Essex & Sussex and neighboring hotels raised money for Bruder's mother back in Switzerland. He was later buried at Atlantic View Cemetery in Manasquan.

**The Response:** This attack could not be ignored. On July 8, a press conference was convened at the American Museum of Natural History with scientists Frederic Augustus Lucas, John Treadwell Nichols, and Robert Cushman Murphy. Nichols initially suspected the killer was an orca (killer whale), not a shark. The scientists tried to calm the public, stressing that a third attack was "unlikely" -- though they admitted surprise that sharks had bitten anyone at all.

Dr. William G. Schauffler, a respected New Jersey physician, declared: "There is not the slightest doubt that a man-eating shark inflicted the injuries." His voice was drowned out by widespread denial. Others blamed sea turtles -- "turtles large enough to inflict just such wounds" with a "vicious disposition." Some suggested it was a "school of turtles."

Resort towns began installing steel wire mesh nets to enclose bathing areas. But the real terror was yet to come.

## Attacks 3, 4, and 5: Matawan Creek -- July 12, 1916

The attacks that shocked the nation didn't happen in the ocean. They happened in a narrow, brackish creek, miles from the sea.

### The Geography

Matawan Creek is a tidal waterway in Monmouth County, New Jersey, that drains into Raritan Bay near Keyport Harbor. The town of Matawan sits roughly 11 miles inland from the open Atlantic, though the creek's tidal mouth is about 1.5 miles from the bay. Below a dam at Ravine Drive, the creek becomes a tidal estuary winding through salt marshes. No one had ever seen a large shark in these waters. The very idea was absurd.

But the summer of 1916 had seen a severe drought. Reduced rainfall pushed unusually high salinity levels deep into the creek, extending brackish conditions far further inland than normal. The creek was essentially an arm of the sea.

### The Warning Ignored

On the morning of July 12, the temperature hit 96 degrees Fahrenheit. At 1:30 PM, retired sea captain Thomas Cottrell was crossing the trolley drawbridge over Matawan Creek when he spotted an enormous dark gray shape moving upstream with the incoming tide. Four workmen on the bridge saw it too. Cottrell estimated it at 8 feet long.

Cottrell ran to the nearest phone and called John Mulsoff, the town barber who also served as police chief, at his barbershop. Mulsoff dismissed it as a prank. Cottrell then ran through town, past shops on Main Street, shouting warnings. People laughed. A shark? In the creek? Impossible.

A motorboat carrying three 18-year-old locals -- Harold Conover, Ralph Gall, and John Tassini -- also spotted the shark and warned a group of boys swimming at Wyckoff Dock. The boys thought it was a joke.

### Attack 3: Lester Stillwell (2:05 PM)

Lester Stillwell was 11 years old, one week shy of his 12th birthday, the son of William and Louella Stillwell. He worked alongside his father at the Anderson Basket Factory -- child labor was common in 1916. Lester suffered from epilepsy (tonic-clonic seizures), a fact that would tragically complicate the response to his attack.

That afternoon, Lester's father let him leave work early because of the oppressive heat. Lester and his best friend Albert "Ally" O'Hara headed for the swimming hole at Wyckoff Dock, a deep section of the creek where the remains of an old dock provided a jumping-off point. They were joined by Charles Van Brunt, Johnson Cartan (whose parents owned Cartan's Department Store at 92 Main Street), Frank Close, and Anthony Bubblin.

The boys were splashing in the murky water when Ally O'Hara felt something rough, "like sandpaper," graze his leg. They noticed what appeared to be "an old, black weather-beaten board or a weathered log" drifting in the dark water. Then Anthony Bubblin executed a backflip off the dock -- just as the water around Lester Stillwell erupted.

The shark seized Lester and dragged him under. He surfaced briefly, screaming, then was pulled down again. The terrified boys scrambled out of the creek and ran through the streets of Matawan, naked and mud-covered, screaming: "A shark got Lester!"

### Attack 4: Stanley Fisher (2:10 PM)

The boys ran past Fisher's dry cleaning and tailoring shop on Main Street. Watson Stanley Fisher, 24, was a well-known and well-loved figure in Matawan -- a member of the church choir, an athlete, and a natural leader. When he heard the boys' screams, his first thought was that Lester had suffered one of his epileptic seizures and was drowning.

Fisher recruited Red Burlew and Arthur Smith, and the three men dove into the murky creek to search for the boy. For roughly 30 minutes they searched the dark, silty water. Arthur Smith felt something scrape his abdomen, drawing blood and leaving a permanent scar.

Then Fisher made one final dive. In waist-deep water, he found Lester's body on the creek bottom. He grabbed the boy's leg and began pulling him to the surface. Witnesses said they briefly saw Lester's body break the surface -- then the shark struck.

The shark seized Fisher's right thigh. It tore a massive wound from hip to knee, removing at least ten pounds of flesh and severing his femoral artery. Fisher fought the shark, which dragged him underwater twice before Arthur Van Buskirk, a deputy with the Monmouth County detective's office, struck the shark with an oar, forcing it to release Fisher.

Dr. George C. Reynolds treated Fisher's severed femoral artery at the scene. At 5:06 PM, Fisher was placed on a train to Long Branch. At 5:30 PM he entered the operating room at Monmouth Memorial Hospital, where Dr. Edwin Field performed surgery. At 6:45 PM, Stanley Fisher died from blood loss and shock.

His last words, reportedly spoken on the operating table: "I found the boy, he is on the bottom."

### Attack 5: Joseph Dunn (2:35 PM)

Approximately 30 minutes after Fisher was attacked -- and half a mile downstream, near the brickyard dock operated by the New Jersey Clay Company -- 12-year-old Joseph Dunn of upper Manhattan was swimming with his 14-year-old brother Michael and their friend Jerry Hourihan. The boys were visiting relatives and had no idea what was happening upstream.

Someone came running, shouting about a shark. The boys scrambled for the dock ladder. Joseph, the farthest out, was the last to reach it. As he climbed, the shark seized his left leg.

What followed was a brutal tug-of-war. Michael Dunn and Jerry Hourihan grabbed Joseph's arms while the shark pulled at his leg. Brickyard supervisor Robert Thress rushed in and helped wrench Joseph free. Fisherman Jacob Lefferts also jumped in to assist.

Joseph later described the bite: "I felt my leg going down the shark's throat -- I thought it would swallow me... It was like a big pair of scissors."

Captain Cottrell transported Joseph and Michael to the Wyckoff dock area, where doctors were treating Fisher. Joseph was then driven to St. Peter's Hospital in New Brunswick. His left leg was severely mangled but not amputated. After 59 days of treatment, Joseph Dunn was released on September 15, 1916 -- the sole survivor of the attacks.

### Recovering Lester

Lester Stillwell's body was not found that day. Townspeople filled the creek with dynamite in an unsuccessful attempt to kill the shark. On July 14, at 5:30 AM, train conductor Harry Van Cleaf discovered Lester's body floating near the railroad trestle, approximately 150 feet west of Wyckoff Dock.

Stanley Fisher and Lester Stillwell were buried side by side at Rosehill Cemetery in Matawan on July 15.

## The Shark Hunt

The Matawan Creek attacks triggered a shark hunt of unprecedented scale -- described by some as "the largest animal hunt in American history." Armed citizens with rifles, harpoons, pitchforks, spears, shotguns, and dynamite descended on the waterways of New Jersey and New York.

Motor boat patrols equipped with rifles and harpoon guns were established along the coast. Sheep guts were used as bait. Hundreds of sharks were killed. Resort towns offered bounties -- Matawan's Mayor Arris B. Henderson posted a $100 reward for killing the shark.

The federal government joined the hunt. President Wilson called a Cabinet meeting to discuss the crisis. Treasury Secretary William Gibbs McAdoo suggested mobilizing the Coast Guard to patrol the Jersey Shore. Wilson agreed, ordering Coast Guard cutters to "fish for the monsters." The Washington Post ran the headline: **"U.S. War on Sharks."** Congress appropriated $5,000 (approximately $150,000 today) for shark eradication efforts.

Governor James Fielder of New Jersey "virtually mandated" that steel wire mesh enclose all popular swimming locations.

## The Capture: July 14, 1916

On July 14 -- two days after the Matawan Creek attacks and the same day Lester Stillwell's body was recovered -- Michael Schleisser and his fishing partner John Murphy set out in a small motorboat in Raritan Bay, near the mouth of Matawan Creek, about four miles from the attack site.

Michael Schleisser was a remarkable character -- a Harlem-based taxidermist and a lion tamer for the Barnum and Bailey Circus. While dragging their nets, the boat was suddenly jerked backward and began to tip. A shark had become entangled in the net and was attacking the boat.

Schleisser fought the shark with the only weapon available -- a broken oar handle -- beating it repeatedly until it stopped moving. The shark measured 7.5 feet long and weighed 325 pounds.

When Schleisser cut open the shark's belly, he found "suspicious fleshy material and bones" that filled about two-thirds of a milk crate and weighed 15 pounds. The remains were examined by Dr. Frederic Lucas, director of the American Museum of Natural History, who identified them as human: "They are the parts of the left radius and ulna [lower arm bones] and one of the anterior left ribs, all human. There is no doubt about this."

The shark was identified as a juvenile great white. Schleisser mounted and taxidermied the specimen and displayed it in the window of a shop on Broadway in Manhattan.

After Schleisser's catch, the attacks stopped. No further incidents occurred that summer.

## The Aftermath

### Economic Devastation
The attacks cost New Jersey resort owners an estimated $250,000 in lost tourism -- approximately $7.4 million in today's dollars. Sunbathing declined by 75% at some beaches. Hotel reservations were canceled en masse.

### Scientific Revolution
The attacks forced American scientists to completely revise their understanding of sharks. Before 1916, sharks were considered "fairly harmless" -- shy creatures incapable of seriously injuring humans. After 1916, they became man-eating predators in both scientific literature and popular imagination.

### Cultural Transformation
The 1916 attacks created what author Michael Capuzzo called a panic "unrivaled in American history," one that spread "along the coasts of New York and New Jersey and spreading by telephone and wireless, letter and postcard." This was the birth of shark panic as a cultural phenomenon -- a fear that would be amplified a hundredfold when Peter Benchley published *Jaws* in 1974 and Steven Spielberg adapted it for the screen in 1975.

### The Unsolved Mystery
To this day, scientists debate whether the attacks were the work of one shark or several, and whether the species was a great white, a bull shark, or some combination. The evidence is long gone. The debate endures.
