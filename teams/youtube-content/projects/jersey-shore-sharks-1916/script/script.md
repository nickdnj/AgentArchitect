# Chapter 2: The World of 1916

## [SCENE 4: America in Crisis - 0:45-1:15]

[VISUAL: Library of Congress period photograph -- crowded Atlantic City beach and boardwalk, Detroit Publishing Company, c.1910-1920. Families packed together on the sand. Ken Burns slow zoom.]

NARRATION:
"The summer of nineteen sixteen was a summer of fear. A polio epidemic had seized New York City -- twenty-seven thousand infected, six thousand dead. Children were banned from parks, pools, and public gatherings. The only escape was the beach. Families poured onto the Jersey Shore in record numbers. Meanwhile, World War One raged in Europe. German U-boats prowled the Atlantic. And President Woodrow Wilson was spending the summer just miles away, at a fifty-two-room mansion in West Long Branch."

[TRANSITION: Crossfade, 1s]

# Chapter 4: Spring Lake -- The Attack That Could Not Be Denied

[VISUAL: Essex & Sussex Hotel -- Library of Congress HABS photograph, 1933 survey. The grand hotel on the Spring Lake beachfront. Slow pan.]

NARRATION:
"Five days later. Forty-five miles north. Charles Bruder was a twenty-eight-year-old Swiss immigrant, a former soldier, working as the bell captain at the Essex and Sussex Hotel in Spring Lake. On his afternoon break, he went for his regular swim, a hundred and thirty yards from shore. The shark hit him with devastating force. It bit his abdomen and severed both his legs below the knee."

# Chapter 9: The Aftermath and The Debate

## [SCENE 23: War on Sharks - 7:30-7:50]

[VISUAL: AI-generated scene -- armed men in a motorboat on a calm bay, rifles raised, nets trailing behind. Other boats visible in the distance. Early 1900s period dress. A headline superimposed: "U.S. WAR ON SHARKS." Grim determination on their faces. Slow pan.]

NARRATION:
"President Wilson called a Cabinet meeting. Congress appropriated five thousand dollars -- about a hundred and fifty thousand in today's money -- to eradicate sharks. The Washington Post headline: 'U.S. War on Sharks.' Armed citizens with rifles, harpoons, pitchforks, and dynamite descended on the waterways of New Jersey. Hundreds of sharks were killed. Resort owners lost two hundred and fifty thousand dollars in tourism. Sunbathing declined seventy-five percent."

[TRANSITION: Crossfade, 1s]

## [SCENE 24: The Great Debate - 7:50-8:15]

[VISUAL: Split composition -- great white shark scientific illustration on the left, bull shark scientific illustration on the right. Both from Wikimedia Commons / BHL Flickr, public domain. Vintage engraving style. Gentle zoom between the two.]

NARRATION:
"The shark Schleisser caught was identified as a juvenile great white. But here is the question that scientists still argue about, more than a century later. Great white sharks do not enter freshwater. Bull sharks do. Bull sharks have been found twenty-five hundred miles up the Amazon River. Matawan Creek was brackish -- part salt, part fresh. Was it really a great white in that creek? Or a bull shark? Was it one shark -- or two? The specimen Schleisser caught was mounted and displayed in a shop window on Broadway. It has since been lost. There is no DNA. There will never be a definitive answer."

# Chapter 10: The Legacy -- Jaws and Beyond

## [SCENE 25: The Birth of Shark Fear - 8:15-8:35]

[VISUAL: AI-generated image of a 1970s bookstore window display -- a bestselling thriller with a dramatic shark cover prominently featured, "Now a Major Motion Picture" banner across the front. Warm amber light, nostalgic 1970s atmosphere. No copyrighted imagery. Slow zoom.]

NARRATION:
"Before nineteen sixteen, sharks were harmless. After nineteen sixteen, they were monsters. That fear lived quietly for sixty years -- until Peter Benchley published Jaws in nineteen seventy-four. He knew about the nineteen sixteen attacks. The parallels are unmistakable: a shark terrorizing a beach town, officials refusing to close the beaches to protect tourism, a colorful hunter who catches the killer."

Today, humans kill a hundred million sharks every year. Sharks kill about ten."
