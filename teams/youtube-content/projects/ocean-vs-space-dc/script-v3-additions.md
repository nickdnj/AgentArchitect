[VISUAL: Company logos appearing on a dark map — Chevron, ExxonMobil, TotalEnergies, Equinor, Aker BP — each pinned to their data center project locations. As each is named, a line of light connects the logo to its project site. The map fills with activity.]

NARRATION:
"Chevron is building four gigawatts of gas-fired power foundries — their word — co-located directly with data centers. Behind the meter. No grid required. And in March of this year, Aker BP and Armada put the first AI data center on a live offshore oil platform on the Norwegian Continental Shelf. They called it a 'reference system.' That is an oil industry term. It means: a template to copy everywhere."

## [SCENE 32: The Stranded Energy — 18:00-18:40]

[VISUAL: Night shot of offshore platforms with gas flares burning. But now the visual shifts — the flares dim and are replaced by blue LED glow from compute containers on the platform decks. The orange waste becomes blue value. The transformation is visual and literal.]
[MUSIC: Building confidence, momentum]

NARRATION:
"The oilfield services companies are moving even faster. Baker Hughes has booked one point two gigawatts of data center work. Halliburton's VoltaGrid subsidiary signed a two point three gigawatt deal with Oracle. SLB's digital division hit one billion dollars in annual recurring revenue — up a hundred and twenty-one percent year over year. Halliburton's CEO put it bluntly: 'The demand for power and AI is like nothing I have ever seen.'"

[PAUSE: 0.5s]

"And the energy is already there. Armada estimates six gigawatts of stranded energy exists in the United States alone — gas that is being flared or shut in because there is no pipeline to take it anywhere. That is enough to power hundreds of data centers. Shell's latest scenarios predict data centers will consume five thousand terawatt-hours by 2050 — roughly eight percent of all global electricity."

"The oil companies do not need to be convinced. They have the platforms, the gas, the workforce, the marine logistics, and the decommissioning budgets. The question is not whether they will enter the data center business. It is whether they are already too late — or just early enough."
