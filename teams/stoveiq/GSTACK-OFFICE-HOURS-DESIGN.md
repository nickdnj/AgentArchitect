## Problem Statement

No affordable retrofit device gives real-time thermal intelligence about a cooktop. Caregivers of elderly or cognitively impaired relatives have zero tools to know if a stove is left on — they just hope. Cooking enthusiasts rely on handheld IR guns or guesswork to know when a pan is ready. The market offers binary on/off shutoff devices (FireAvert, iGuardStove) or expensive thermal cameras focused solely on cooking (Cooksy at $299-$699). Nothing under $200 combines thermal imaging, per-burner intelligence, and safety alerts in a retrofit form factor.

## Demand Evidence

- **Founder is the target user**: Owns an IR thermometer gun, memorizes pan timing, actively monitors cooking temperature. Would use this product daily.
- **Real safety incident**: A neighbor (Jen) with cognitive decline was a fire risk in a multi-unit building. She has since been placed in a home. The building could have burned.
- **No direct demand validation yet**: No pre-orders, waitlists, or customer interviews. This is an idea-stage product driven by personal motivation.
- **Market signal**: Cooksy raised awareness that thermal imaging above the stove works as a consumer product. Their apparent pivot to cookware suggests the $299+ cooking-only positioning may not sustain a business. Lower price + safety features could be the formula.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

**Primary user**: Adult child (40-65) of an aging parent (75+) who lives alone and cooks with a gas stove. They worry about fire risk but don't want to take away their parent's independence by removing the stove.

**Secondary user**: The founder — a cooking enthusiast who wants hands-free thermal monitoring of all burners simultaneously.

**Narrowest wedge**: A device that answers one question — "Is Mom's stove on right now?" — and sends a push notification if it's been on for more than X minutes with no one nearby. This is the MVP. Everything else (heat maps, cooking features, boil detection) is rev 2.

**Key insight**: All safety competitors use binary detection (motion, smoke, or knob position). None use thermal imaging. StoveIQ's thermal approach provides richer information — which burner, how hot, how long — enabling smarter alerts with fewer false positives.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Minimum Viable Safety Device
- **Summary**: Ship only safety features — stove on/off, unattended timer, push notification to caregiver. No heat maps, no cooking features.
- **Effort**: Small (6-8 weeks firmware + basic app)
- **Risk**: Low — narrow scope, proven sensor, simple software
- **Pros**: Fast to market, validates hardware design, clear value proposition
- **Cons**: Less differentiated from FireAvert/iGuardStove, misses cooking enthusiast market

### Approach B: Full Platform (Current Architecture)
- **Summary**: Everything in the existing docs — heat maps, boil detection, cooking timers, Flutter app, AWS backend.
- **Effort**: Extra-large (10 months)
- **Risk**: High — building hardware + firmware + app + cloud simultaneously before demand validation
- **Pros**: Most differentiated, serves both markets, full vision
- **Cons**: Long timeline, high capital risk, no demand signal until Kickstarter

### Approach C: Software-First with FLIR One
- **Summary**: Skip hardware. Build the app to work with a FLIR One ($200 iPhone attachment). Validate UX and algorithms before committing to custom hardware.
- **Effort**: Medium (8-12 weeks, app only)
- **Risk**: Low — no hardware risk, app store distribution
- **Pros**: Fastest path to validating cooking features, no manufacturing
- **Cons**: FLIR One is $200 and requires manual positioning, doesn't validate custom hardware design

## Recommended Approach: Hybrid A→B (Hardware-First, Safety-MVP)

Build the **final hardware** (ESP32-S3 + MLX90640) from day one, but ship with **only safety features** initially.

### Phase 1: Hardware + Safety MVP (8-12 weeks)
- ESP32-S3 + MLX90640 on breadboard/devkit
- Validate sensor at 30° under-cabinet mounting on real stove
- Firmware: sensor acquisition, basic thermal processing, stove on/off detection
- Firmware: unattended timer (configurable, default 30 min)
- Firmware: local buzzer alert
- Mobile app (Flutter): BLE provisioning, WiFi setup, push notifications
- Cloud: minimal — just push notification relay (Firebase Cloud Messaging)
- **Gate**: Can the sensor reliably detect "stove is on" at under-cabinet angles?

### Phase 2: Cooking Intelligence (firmware update, 8-12 weeks)
- Same hardware, new firmware
- Per-burner detection and state machine
- Heat map rendering in app
- Temperature monitoring and "pan ready" alerts
- Boil detection
- Cooking session history

### Phase 3: Full Platform + Kickstarter (8-12 weeks)
- Custom PCB design
- Enclosure design and 3D printing
- FCC pre-scan
- Kickstarter campaign prep (video, page, pricing)
- Campaign launch targeting $50K goal

**Why this is the right path:**
1. You're building hardware no matter what — start now, validate the physical product
2. Safety MVP is a much smaller software build than the full platform
3. When you go to Kickstarter, you can demo a working device, not a rendering
4. Rev 2 cooking features are a firmware update, not a new product
5. Caregiver positioning at $99-$129 has less price competition than cooking-only at $299+

## Open Questions

1. **POC validation**: Can MLX90640 reliably discriminate "stove on" vs "stove off" at 30° under-cabinet mounting? (Phase 1 answers this)
2. **Push notification reliability**: For a safety product, notification latency matters. What's the worst-case delay from detection to phone alert?
3. **False positive rate**: Under-cabinet mounting means hot steam, nearby oven heat, and ambient temperature changes could trigger false alerts. What's acceptable?
4. **Regulatory**: Does a safety monitoring device need UL listing in addition to FCC? What are the liability implications of a device that's supposed to alert on stove fires?
5. **Cooksy learnings**: Can we learn from Cooksy's apparent pivot? What went wrong at $299+ cooking-only? Was it the price, the market, or the product?
