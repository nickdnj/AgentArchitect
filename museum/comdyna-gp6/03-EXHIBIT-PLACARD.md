## ▣ HEADLINE

# Watch a Cannonball Fly — Computed by Wires, Not Code

### Comdyna GP‑6 Analog Computer  ·  with HP 1200B Oscilloscope

---

## ▣ BODY (≈120 words)

The green arc on the screen is a **projectile in flight** — and this machine is computing
it **right now.** But look closer: there is **no microchip and no software** inside.

This is an **analog computer.** Instead of counting in ones and zeros, it uses **voltage**
to stand in for real things. One voltage *is* gravity. Another *is* the projectile's speed.
Another *is* its height. The machine is **wired** so the electricity obeys the very same
physics a real cannonball obeys.

It solves the motion by **integrating** — continuously adding gravity up into speed, and
speed up into height — using op‑amps and capacitors. The answer is drawn live on the
oscilloscope: **height versus time, the flight arc.**

---

## ▣ CALLOUT BOX — "How it thinks"

```
   GRAVITY (a knob = a voltage)
        │  add up over time  (integrate)
        ▼
   SPEED  (a falling straight line)
        │  add up over time  (integrate)
        ▼
   HEIGHT (a parabola — the flight on the screen)
```

**The wiring *is* the equation.** Re‑plug the patch cords and you've changed the physics.

---

## ▣ TRY THIS (docent‑assisted)

**Turn the GRAVITY knob and watch the arc change shape — instantly.**
Weaker gravity → the projectile sails (that's the Moon). Stronger gravity → it slams down.
Nothing is re‑run or reloaded. The trajectory simply *becomes* the new equation the moment
you turn the knob.

> **That is the charm of analog computing — math you can feel with your fingertips.**

---

## ▣ DID YOU KNOW?

For about **30 years** — through the Apollo program, aircraft and missile design — analog
computers like this were how engineers solved the hardest equations: not by counting, but
by building a small **electrical model** of the problem and watching it run. The math
happens at the speed of electricity, all at once, continuously.

---

## ▣ SMALL PRINT / CREDIT LINE

*Analog computers represent numbers as voltages (here, ±10 V = full scale) and solve
differential equations with operational‑amplifier integrators. The Comdyna GP‑6 was a
widely used teaching machine of the 1970s–80s. Programmed entirely by patch cords — the
visible tangle of wires is the "source code."*
