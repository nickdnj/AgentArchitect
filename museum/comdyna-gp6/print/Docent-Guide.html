Section 1

The Big Idea — and why it built the modern world

In one sentence: this machine has no microchip and runs no software, yet it solves a real physics problem in real time — by turning the problem into electricity. The green arc on the oscilloscope is a projectile’s flight, computed live. Turn a knob and the flight changes shape instantly.

That is strange and wonderful, but it’s also historically enormous. The problem this exhibit demonstrates — predicting where a projectile will land — is, quite literally, the problem that gave birth to the computer industry.

From firing tables to your phone

To hit a target miles away, gunners needed firing tables — books listing exactly how to aim for every distance, wind, and powder charge. Each number is the solution to a projectile equation. In World War II the U.S. Army needed these tables by the thousands, and computing them by hand took teams of people (called “computers”) months per table.

That bottleneck drove the invention of the first electronic computers. ENIAC (1945) — often called the first general-purpose electronic computer — was commissioned for exactly this: calculating artillery firing tables. Analog computers like the Comdyna GP-6 in front of you attacked the same equations from a different direction — with amplifiers and capacitors instead of code. Both lineages chased the same prize: knowing where the projectile will go. Everything downstream — spaceflight, weather models, video games, the device in your pocket — grew from that root.

The throughline for visitors: “The reason computers exist at all starts with this exact question — and here’s a machine that answers it by becoming the physics, right in front of you.”

Section 2

Explain It at Any Age

Same truth, four altitudes. Pick the one that fits the visitor in front of you — or climb the ladder as they lean in.

Age 10The curious kid
“See that glowing line? That’s a ball flying through the air, like throwing a baseball. This old machine is drawing it as it figures out the path. But here’s the cool part — there’s no computer chip inside, and no app. It thinks with electricity and wires. Gravity is just a little zap of electricity, and the machine adds it up to figure out how fast the ball goes and how high it gets. Watch — when I turn this knob, I’m changing how strong gravity is, and the whole throw changes! Less gravity, the ball floats way up like on the Moon!”
Age 15The student
“This is an analog computer. Your phone is digital — it solves problems by counting in 1s and 0s, super fast. This one doesn’t count at all. It lets a voltage stand in for a real quantity: one voltage is gravity, one is the ball’s speed, one is its height. You learned in science that if you add up acceleration over time you get speed, and add up speed you get distance. This machine does that adding-up — it’s called integration — with an electronic part called an integrator. Two integrations turn gravity into the flight path you see.”
Age 20The college mind
“Projectile motion is the differential equation d²y/dt² = −g. Rather than solving it numerically in tiny time-steps like a digital computer, this machine solves it continuously. Each op-amp-with-a-capacitor is an analog integrator; chain two of them and a constant input (gravity) becomes velocity, then position. The patch cords are the program — re-wire them and you’ve written a new equation. And because it runs in repetitive mode, it re-solves the whole trajectory dozens of times a second, so adjusting a coefficient pot updates the solution in real time. It’s a physical, continuous-time computer.”
Age 62The lifelong learner
“You may remember when ‘computer’ could mean either a digital machine or one of these. Before microprocessors were cheap, engineers designing aircraft, spacecraft, and control systems leaned on analog computers because they’d solve a differential equation instantly and let you feel the system respond as you turned a dial. This GP-6 is a teaching version of those room-sized machines. The same math that puts this arc on the screen sent Apollo to the Moon and traces back to the WWII firing-table problem that built ENIAC. It trades a little precision for something digital never quite recaptured — intuition you can hold in your hand.”

One line that lands for everyone

Section 3 · The Technical Heart

From Schematic → Equation → Wiring

This is the path an engineer walks to make an analog computer solve anything: (1) write the physics as an equation, (2) redraw the equation as a block diagram, (3) translate each block into patch cords. When you finish this section, the tangle of wires reads like a sentence.

Step 1 — The physics becomes an equation

Throw a ball straight up; ignore air. Only gravity acts, a constant downward acceleration g. The whole flight is one second-order differential equation:

d²y/dt² = −g   with start values y(0)=height, y′(0)=launch speed

Read it as a chain you can build: gravity → (add up over time) → speed → (add up over time) → height. Two “add-ups.” In calculus, adding up over time is integration.

Step 2 — The equation becomes a block diagram (the schematic)

Each piece of the equation becomes a labeled block. Follow the signal left to right; the initial “throw” enters the first integrator from below.

What an integrator actually is

The whole machine hinges on one part: an operational amplifier with a capacitor in its feedback loop. Its output is the running total of its input over time — calculus, done by electronics.

The key insight that de-tangles the wires: an op-amp integrator computes the negative integral. Chain two and the sign flips twice. So the “extra” amplifiers on the schematic (the inverters) aren’t doing physics — they’re bookkeeping the minus signs so velocity falls and the arc points the right way. See that, and the patch panel stops looking like spaghetti.

Step 3 — The block diagram becomes patch cords

On the GP-6 every block’s connections are brought out to color-coded jacks. “Programming” means plugging cords so each arrow above becomes a wire. (Exact jack list is in Section 6.) Walking the signal:

  1. +10 V reference → GRAVITY pot (#2). Dialing it sets the number g.
  2. Pot #2 → inverter (#7). Now a steady −g — a downward pull.
  3. −g → integrator #1. Its output ramps; that ramp is velocity.
  4. −10 V → INITIAL VELOCITY pot (#5) → inverter (#8) → integrator #1’s start input. Preloads the launch speed.
  5. Integrator #1 → integrator #2. Output is height.
  6. Integrator #2 → oscilloscope Y. Scope X is time → it draws height vs. time, the parabola.
Say it out loud: “Every term in the equation is a block, every arrow is a wire. The schematic is the equation, and the equation is the flight.”

Section 4

Why the Curve Is Alive — and where “apogee” hides

A digital computer steps through tiny slices of time. This machine never steps — its capacitors integrate continuously, at the speed of electricity. The whole flight is solved all at once, all the time.

A switch called Repetitive Operation makes the machine re-launch the projectile dozens of times a second, redrawing the arc each time. That’s why turning the GRAVITY or INITIAL VELOCITY knob reshapes the trajectory instantly and smoothly — nothing is recompiled; you simply changed a number in a live equation.

ModeWhat happens insideThe projectile story
Pot SetYou dial the coefficient knobs and read them on the meter.Choose gravity and launch speed.

The hidden gem: find the top of the flight

Switch the scope to view velocity instead of height and the parabola becomes a straight line sloping down. Gravity steadily steals speed. Where that line crosses zero, the ball has stopped rising — that instant is the apogee, the very top. After it, the ball falls.

Section 5 · For the Presenter

The Demo Script

A timed walkthrough — about 5–7 minutes. Stage directions are in green italics. Spoken lines are in quotes. Land the bold beats.

0 · The 60-second hookfor walk-ups

[Point at the parabola.] “See that curve? That’s a ball flying through the air — being figured out right now by this machine. And here’s the surprise: no chip, no software. It’s an analog computer — it uses electricity to act out the physics. Gravity is a voltage set by this knob. Watch.” [Turn GRAVITY slowly.] “The whole trajectory reshapes — instantly. Less gravity, it sails like the Moon; more, it slams down. I didn’t re-run anything. The wires just are the new equation. That’s the charm of analog computing.”

1 · The hook0:30

[Beside the scope, parabola visible.] “Look at this green arc — a ball thrown across a field, up, over, and down. This machine is drawing it because it’s computing the flight in real time. And it’s a computer with no chip, no code, no software. It’s called an analog computer.”

2 · Digital vs. analog1:00

“Your phone is digital — it solves problems by counting, billions of 1s and 0s a second. This one is analog. It doesn’t count — it uses voltage to stand in for real things. A voltage here is gravity, one is the ball’s speed, one is its height. It’s wired so the electricity obeys the same physics a real ball obeys. It doesn’t calculate the motion — it becomes the motion.” [Gesture at the patch cords.] “Those wires are the program.”

3 · The physics, in three steps1:15

[Mime a ball arcing.] “One thing acts on a thrown ball: gravity, pulling down steadily. So — one, gravity is a steady voltage, set by this GRAVITY knob. [Tap pot #2.] Two, add that pull up over time and you get speed — the block that adds up is an integrator. Three, add the speed up over time and you get height — that’s the arc. Gravity → integrate → speed → integrate → height.”

4 · Read the wiring like a sentence1:00

[Trace the schematic / panel with a finger.] “Start at the GRAVITY knob → into the first integrator → out comes speed. This other knob, INITIAL VELOCITY, is how hard we throw it. [Tap pot #5.] Into the second integrator → out comes height → to the scope. That’s your arc. Those little ‘invert’ amps don’t do physics — they just keep plus and minus straight so gravity pulls down and the throw goes up.”

5 · The payoff — make it live1:30

“Here’s the magic — it re-flies the ball dozens of times a second, so I can change the world mid-flight.” [Turn GRAVITY down slowly.] “Weaken gravity… the arc stretches — that’s the Moon.” [Turn it up.] “Crank it up — the arc collapses. Heavy planet.” [Turn INITIAL VELOCITY up.] “Throw harder — bigger arc. I never typed anything. The trajectory changes the instant I turn the knob, because the wiring is the equation.” [Optional: switch to the velocity view.] “This falling line is speed; where it crosses zero is the top of the flight — the apogee.”

6 · The close0:30

“For about thirty years — through the Apollo program, aircraft and missile design — this is how engineers solved the hardest equations: not by counting, but by building a little electrical model and watching it run. Digital won on precision and flexibility. But for feeling how a system behaves, nothing beats it. That’s the charm of analog computing.”

Presenter tips

• Turn knobs slowly — the magic is watching the curve morph.   • Don’t re-patch cords mid-tour unless confident (Section 6 restores it).   • If the trace is flat/off-screen, you’re likely in Reset/Hold or a pot is at zero — confirm Operate/Repetitive and raise the pots.

The one line to never skip: “It doesn’t calculate the motion — it becomes the motion.”

Section 6 · Run It / Fix It

Operator Setup & Patch List

Authority: the museum’s tan schematic handout is the master wiring diagram for this exact patch. If anything here disagrees with the physical panel or that sheet, trust the sheet. The list below reconstructs it block-for-block — verify before re-patching.

A · Power-up & display

  1. Power on the GP-6 and the HP 1200B; warm up ~1 minute.
  2. Scope: computed signal on vertical (Y), time on horizontal (X); start ~1 V/div, set the sweep so one full flight fills the screen; adjust intensity/focus.
  3. Confirm the red OVLD lamp is off. If lit, an amplifier is railed — lower the GRAVITY / INITIAL-VELOCITY pots and recheck patching.

From Cannonballs to Your Phone

1600s — Galileo shows a projectile’s path is a parabola; the math of flight begins.
1700s–1800s — Armies compile firing tables by hand; “computers” are people doing arithmetic.
1930s–40s — Analog differential analyzers mechanize the equations of motion for ballistics and engineering.
1945 — ENIAC is built to compute artillery firing tables — a founding moment of digital computing.
1950s–70s — Analog computers like this GP-6 design aircraft, spacecraft, and control systems; Apollo-era engineers live on them.
Today — The same equations run on the chip in your pocket. It all traces back to one question: where will the projectile land?
