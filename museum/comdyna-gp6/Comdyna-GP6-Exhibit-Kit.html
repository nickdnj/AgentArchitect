1Overview & the 20‑second version

This machine has no CPU and runs no software. It solves the projectile equation a = −g by being the equation:

  • Gravity is a steady voltage (set by a knob).
  • An integrator adds that up over time to get velocity (a straight, falling line).
  • A second integrator adds velocity up over time to get height (a parabola — the flight arc).
  • The oscilloscope plots height vs. time, so you literally watch the projectile fly.

Turn the GRAVITY knob and the arc changes instantly — because the wires are doing the calculus continuously, not step‑by‑step.

That is the charm of analog computing — math you can feel with your fingertips.

2From Schematic → Equation → Wiring

This is the core teaching piece. It walks the same path an engineer walked in 1965 to make an analog computer solve a problem:

1. Write the physics as an equation → 2. Re‑draw the equation as a block diagram (the schematic) → 3. Translate each block into a patch‑cord connection on the machine.

Part 1 — The Physics

Throw a ball straight up; ignore air resistance. One force acts on it: gravity, a constant downward acceleration g (≈9.8 m/s²). Three quantities describe the flight, linked like a chain:

QuantitySymbolIn plain words
AccelerationaHow fast velocity changes. Constant here: a = −g (down).
VelocityvSpeed & direction. Starts at launch speed, drops steadily.
Position (height)yHow high the ball is right now.

The whole problem is one second‑order differential equation:

     d²y
     ───  =  −g        with:  y(0)  = y₀   (launch height)
     dt²                      y'(0) = v₀   (launch velocity)

Solved on paper it gives the familiar formulas — but the analog computer never uses them. It works straight from d²y/dt² = −g and builds the answer by integrating, the way nature does:

v(t) = v₀ − g·t            →  a straight line sloping down
y(t) = y₀ + v₀·t − ½·g·t²  →  a parabola (the flight arc)

Part 2 — The Big Trick: integrate twice

Read the chain backward — the engineer's key insight:

START:   acceleration  =  −g          (a constant — just a steady voltage)
           │  integrate (add up over time)
           ▼
         velocity      =  v₀ − g·t     (a falling straight line)
           │  integrate (add up over time)
           ▼
         position      =  y₀ + v₀·t − ½g·t²   (a parabola — the flight)

So if we have a block that can integrate, we: (1) feed a constant “−g” in → out comes velocity; (2) feed velocity in → out comes position; (3) display position → watch it fly. That block is an op‑amp integrator, and the GP‑6 has four.

Part 3 — The Building Blocks

Each block performs one math operation on voltages. A voltage stands for a number (±10 V = ±full scale; the red readout shows “percent of 10 V”, so −0.588 = −5.88 V).

Block A — Coefficient Potentiometer (a knob = multiply by a constant)

A voltage divider: feed it +10 V, get back any fraction 0–1 — that fraction is a number in your equation, like g. Here: Pot #2 = GRAVITY, Pot #5 = INITIAL VELOCITY. Turning them changes the equation’s numbers while it runs.

Block B — Inverter (multiply by −1)

An op‑amp with equal resistors flips a voltage’s sign: out = −in. Sign‑juggling is half the job of patching. Here: Amps #7 and #8.

Block C — Integrator (the star)

An op‑amp with a capacitor in feedback performs calculus — its output is the running total of its input over time:

                ┌──────────┐ capacitor (C)
                │          │
 input ──[ R ]───┤−   op    ├──── output = −(1/RC)·∫ input dt + (initial value)
                 │    amp   │
           ┌─────┤+         │
       IC ─┘     └──────────┘
  • It accumulates. A steady input makes the output ramp — and that ramp is an integral.
  • It starts from an Initial Condition (IC). You preload the capacitor to a starting voltage. This is how launch velocity and launch height enter the problem.
  • It inverts as it integrates (note the minus sign). Every integrator flips the sign of what it computes — which is why the schematic has extra inverters.
⚠ The single most important idea for the wiring: an op‑amp integrator computes the negative integral, so a chain of two integrators flips the sign twice. The “extra” amplifiers (inverters #7/#8/#3) are not doing physics — they’re bookkeeping for the minus signs so velocity falls and the arc points the right way. See that, and the patch panel stops looking like spaghetti.

Part 5 — The Wiring: diagram → patch cords

On the GP‑6, every block’s connections are brought out to color‑coded jacks. “Programming” means plugging cords to make the diagram real. Each arrow above becomes one patch cord:

  1. Reference → Gravity pot. Patch +10 V to Pot #2. Dialing it sets g.
  2. Gravity pot → inverter (#7). Now you have a steady −g — a downward pull.
  3. −g → Integrator #1. Amp #1 accumulates −g → output ramps. That ramp is velocity.
  4. Set launch speed (IC). Patch −10 V → Pot #5 → Amp #8 → Amp #1 IC jack. Preloads integrator #1 so at t=0 its output already equals v₀.
  5. Velocity → Integrator #2. Amp #2 accumulates velocity → output is position (height).
  6. Position → Scope Y. Scope X = time, so it draws height vs. time — the parabola.
  7. Velocity → inverter #3 → scope (optional). Flip the display to show velocity as a falling line; zero velocity = apogee.
Why color‑coded cords? Red carry signals; black are grounds/references; each amplifier’s inputs, summing junction (SJ), and output are grouped together. Once patched, the “program” is visible — you can trace the equation with your finger.

Part 6 — Running it: IC, OP, and why the curve is alive

A digital computer would step through tiny time slices. The GP‑6 doesn’t step — its capacitors integrate continuously, at the speed of electricity. The whole flight is solved all at once, all the time. The MODE controls run the show:

ModeWhat happensProjectile meaning
PS (Pot Set)Reference applied so you can dial & read pots on the DVM.Set g and v₀.
IC (Reset)Integrators forced to starting values.Load the launch — ball in hand.
OP (Operate)Integrators integrate; the solution runs.Launch! The ball flies.
HD (Hold)Integration freezes.Pause the ball mid‑air.
RO (Repetitive Op)Auto‑cycles IC→OP many times/sec (rate = COMPUTE TIME).Re‑launches continuously → stable live trace.

Because of Repetitive Operation, the machine re‑flies the projectile dozens of times a second and redraws the arc. So turning the GRAVITY knob reshapes the whole parabola in real time — flatter on the “Moon,” steeper with more gravity. Turn INITIAL VELOCITY and the arc grows or shrinks. Nothing is recomputed in steps; the wires simply are the new equation the instant you turn the knob.

THIS is the charm of analog computing.

One‑paragraph summary (for memorizing)

A projectile obeys d²y/dt² = −g. Read backward, it says: integrate a constant (gravity) once to get velocity, integrate again to get height. The schematic redraws that as blocks — a knob for gravity, an integrator for velocity, a second for position, plus inverters to keep the minus signs honest. The wiring makes each block real with patch cords: reference → gravity pot → inverter → integrator #1 (velocity) → integrator #2 (position) → oscilloscope. Set launch speed by preloading integrator #1’s initial condition. Run it in repetitive mode and the scope draws the flight arc live — change gravity or launch speed by hand and watch the trajectory respond instantly, because the circuit isn’t calculating the motion, it is the motion.

3Demo Script

Runtime ~5–7 minAll agesStage directions in italics

Presenter needs: GP‑6 powered on and patched (see §5), HP 1200B on and showing the trace, machine in Repetitive Operation (RO).

⏱ 90‑Second Short Version (walk‑up)

[Point at the scope’s parabola.] “See that curve? That’s a ball flying through the air — being calculated right now by this machine. But here’s the thing: there’s no computer chip in here, and no software. No ones and zeros.

This is an analog computer. Instead of counting, it uses electricity to act out the physics. Gravity is just a steady voltage — set by this knob. The machine adds that up over time to get the ball’s speed, adds that up to get its height, and paints the height on the scope. That’s the flight.

Watch what happens when I change gravity. [Slowly turn the GRAVITY knob.] The whole trajectory reshapes — instantly. Less gravity, the ball sails — that’s the Moon. More gravity, it slams down. I didn’t re‑run any program. The wires just are the new equation the moment I turn the knob.

That’s the charm of analog computing — math you can feel with your fingertips.”

1. The Hook (30 sec)

[Stand beside the scope; wait for the parabola.] “Take a look at this green curve. It’s an arc — like a ball thrown across a field, going up, peaking, coming back down. And this machine is drawing it because it’s computing the ball’s flight in real time. Here’s what surprises people: this is a computer with no chip, no code, and no software. It’s called an analog computer.”

2. Digital vs. Analog — the core idea (60 sec)

“Your phone is a digital computer — it solves problems by counting, billions of yes/no steps a second. To draw this arc it would chop the flight into thousands of slices. This machine is analog. It doesn’t count — it uses voltages to physically stand in for real quantities. A voltage here is gravity, another is the ball’s speed, another is its height. Instead of running a program, it’s wired so the electricity obeys the same physics the real ball obeys. It doesn’t calculate the motion — it becomes the motion.” [Gesture to the patch cords.] “Those wires are the ‘program.’ Re‑plug them and you’ve changed the equation.”

3. The physics, in three steps (75 sec)

[Mime a ball arcing.] “Only one thing acts on a thrown ball: gravity, pulling down steadily. So: One — gravity is a steady voltage, set by this knob marked GRAVITY. [Tap Pot #2.] Two — add up that pull over time and you get the ball’s speed; the block that adds up over time is an integrator, and this machine has four. [Point to amps 1–4.] Three — add up the speed over time and you get the ball’s height; that takes a second integrator, and its output is the arc on the screen. So the recipe is: gravity → integrate → speed → integrate → height → draw it.”

4. Read the wiring like a sentence (60 sec)

[Point to the tan schematic.] “This diagram is the equation drawn as a wiring plan, and the panel matches it. Follow my finger: start at the GRAVITY knob → into the first integrator → out comes speed. This other knob, INITIAL VELOCITY, sets how hard we throw it. [Tap Pot #5.] Into the second integrator → out comes height → to the scope, plotted against time. That’s your arc.” [Optional] “Those extra amps labeled ‘invert’ don’t do physics — they just flip plus and minus signs so gravity pulls down and the throw goes up.”

5. The Payoff — make it live (90 sec)

“Here’s the magic. This machine is re‑flying the ball dozens of times a second, so I can change the world while the ball is in flight.” [Slowly turn GRAVITY down.] “Watch as I weaken gravity… the arc stretches — the ball sails and hangs longer. That’s a throw on the Moon.” [Turn GRAVITY up past normal.] “Now crank gravity up — the arc collapses. Heavy planet.” [Turn INITIAL VELOCITY up.] “Throw it harder — bigger arc.” [Pause.] “I never typed anything or re‑ran a program. The trajectory changes the instant I turn the knob — because the wiring is the equation.” [Optional, switch to velocity view.] “This straight line falling through zero is the ball’s speed. Where it crosses zero, the ball has stopped rising — that instant is the apogee, the very top. After that, it falls.”

6. The Close (30 sec)

“For about thirty years — through the Apollo program, aircraft and missile design — this is how engineers solved the hardest equations: not by counting, but by building a little electrical model and watching it run. Digital eventually won on precision and flexibility. But for feeling how a system behaves, nothing beats it. That’s the charm of analog computing.”

F. Numbers cheat‑sheet

  • Full scale: ±10 V = ±100%. DVM shows percent of 10 V (−0.160 → −16% → −1.60 V).
  • Integrator law: output = −(1/RC) × (running total of input) + initial condition. The minus sign is why inverters (#7, #8, #3) exist.
  • Apogee: where the velocity line crosses zero = the top of the flight.

6Visitor FAQ

Q: Where’s the screen and keyboard? Is it broken?
Nothing’s broken — there isn’t one. You program it with patch cords, and the answer is a picture on the oscilloscope. The wires are the keyboard; the scope is the screen.

Q: Is there a computer chip in here?
No. No chip, no memory, no software, no ones and zeros. The brains are op‑amps and capacitors wired to act out the physics directly. It’s an analog computer.

Q: Analog vs. digital, really?
Digital counts — billions of tiny yes/no steps. Analog measures — a smoothly varying voltage stands for a real quantity. Here a voltage is gravity, is speed, is height. Digital approximates by counting fast; analog mirrors the real thing continuously.

Q: What is it computing?
The flight of a projectile under gravity. The green arc is its height over time; switch the view to see its speed.

Q: How does it do math without code?
By integrating — continuously adding up. Gravity (a steady voltage) adds up over time into speed; speed adds up into height. Two integrations, done by op‑amp‑and‑capacitor integrators.

Q: Why does turning the knob change the curve instantly?
The wiring itself is the equation, and it re‑flies the projectile dozens of times a second. Turn a knob and you’ve changed a number in the live equation, so the arc reshapes immediately and smoothly.

Q: What do the two knobs do?
GRAVITY sets how strong gravity is (down = arc stretches like the Moon; up = drops fast). INITIAL VELOCITY sets how hard you throw it.

Q: What’s the red number?
A built‑in voltmeter you aim at any wire. It reads in percent of full scale (full scale = 10 V), so −0.588 ≈ −59%.

Q: What are the triangles labeled “invert”?
Sign‑flippers. Each integrator flips plus/minus as a side effect, so little inverter amps put the signs back — gravity pulls down, the throw goes up.

Q: Where’s the top of the flight?
Switch to the velocity view — the straight line falling through zero. The zero‑crossing is the apogee.

Q: Real work, or a teaching toy?
Both. This model (GP‑6) was a teaching machine, but big analog computers were serious tools for ~30 years — Apollo, aircraft and spacecraft design, missile guidance, control systems — because they solve differential equations instantly and let you feel a system respond.

Q: Why did digital win?
Precision, repeatability, memory, flexibility. But for intuition — watching a whole system breathe as you turn a knob — analog is still wonderful, which is why people build them today.

Q: Could I build one at home?
The core is just an op‑amp and a capacitor — a few dollars of parts. This box simply packages eight amplifiers, multipliers, and a tidy patch panel.

Q: How accurate is it?
Roughly a percent — enough to see the physics, not to do banking. Trading some precision for speed and insight was the whole point.

Leave them with: “It doesn’t calculate the motion — it becomes the motion. That’s the charm of analog computing.”

7Hardware & Sources

Comdyna GP‑6 general‑purpose analog computer (≈1970s–80s teaching machine): 4 integrator/summer amplifiers (top, labeled 1–4), 2 inverter amplifiers (7–8), 2 multipliers, 8 coefficient potentiometers, ±10 V & ±15 V references, built‑in addressable digital voltmeter (the red readout), capacitor‑shorting for zeroing integrators, overload protection. Paired with an HP 1200B oscilloscope (X = time, Y = computed quantity). The “program” is the patch cords.
