1Overview & the 20‑second version

This is a Silicon Graphics (SGI) workstation from the era — roughly a decade — when SGI was the only company on earth selling computers that could do real‑time 3‑D graphics at all. That single capability put these machines everywhere people needed to see data move: Hollywood, NASA, medical imaging, flight simulators — and a movie about dinosaurs.

  • It runs IRIX — SGI's UNIX — and boots to a slick graphical desktop in 1993, while most PCs were still staring at a C:\> prompt.
  • In Jurassic Park, the kid restores the door locks on an SGI by flying through a glowing 3‑D file browser — real SGI software, not a Hollywood prop.
  • The film's dinosaurs were built at Industrial Light & Magic on ~70 SGI machines like this one.
  • Under the hood: a MIPS RISC processor plus dedicated graphics hardware — the direct ancestor of the GPU in every phone and game console today.
SGI starred on both sides of the camera — the prop in the story and the tool that drew the story.

2Both Sides of the Camera

This is the core teaching piece. Two true things — and the magic is that they're the same company:

1. SGI on screen — the "It's a UNIX system!" control‑room machine → 2. SGI behind the camera — the workstations that animated the dinosaurs → 3. Why this machine made 3‑D graphics a product instead of a research project.

Part 1 — SGI on screen: "It's a UNIX system! I know this!"

In the film's climax, young Lex (Ariana Richards) sits at a workstation to restore the park's security, flies through a glowing 3‑D landscape of pedestals and wireframes, and says the immortal line. For thirty years people mocked it as fake Hollywood computer nonsense. It was 100% real software.

The interface is fsn — "File System Navigator," pronounced "fusion" — an actual experimental tool Silicon Graphics shipped as a demo on IRIX. Directories became pedestals; files became boxes you could fly between; the height of a pedestal showed how much data it held.

⚠ The honest caveat — keep it ready: the software was real, but the use was pure cinema. fsn was a viewer — you could not actually re‑lock a building's doors with it. So: real tool, dramatized scene. Saying this out loud makes you more credible, not less.

Part 2 — SGI behind the camera: the machine that made the monsters

The dinosaurs that chase Lex through that room were built on machines just like this one. At Industrial Light & Magic, artists used a fleet of roughly 70 Silicon Graphics workstations — desktop Indigos up to the Onyx graphics supercomputer. Asked what they used, ILM staff said "everything SGI makes." The pipeline:

StageTool (running on SGI)What it did
ModelAlias (PowerAnimator)Sculpt the dinosaur geometry (from a scan of Stan Winston's maquette)
AnimateSoftimage 3DBring the creatures to life, motion and performance
RenderPixar's PhotoRealistic RenderManTurn the 3‑D models into photoreal frames (hours per frame)
⚠ Don't overstate it: SGI dominated the interactive work — modeling and animation. The final frames were rendered with Pixar's RenderMan. Say the dinosaurs were built and animated on SGI, not "rendered on SGI."

Part 3 — The surprising part: most of the dinosaurs aren't CGI

Of roughly 15 minutes of on‑screen dinosaurs in the film, most are Stan Winston's full‑size animatronics — only about 4–6 minutes are computer‑generated (~50 shots). The watershed wasn't volume; it was photorealism — the first time audiences believed a computer‑generated creature was really there.

When stop‑motion legend Phil Tippett — hired to animate the dinosaurs the old way — saw ILM's digital test, he reportedly said "I feel extinct." Spielberg put the line in the movie as Dr. Grant's. ILM's Dennis Muren supervised the effects; the film won the Academy Award for Best Visual Effects and redirected the entire industry from physical/optical effects to digital.

Part 4 — Why this machine mattered

For about a decade, SGI owned 3‑D graphics. The reason this Indigo² could do what a 1993 PC couldn't:

  • MIPS RISC CPU + dedicated graphics silicon. Offloading geometry to a specialized graphics processor is the architecture behind every modern GPU.
  • OpenGL — the cross‑platform graphics standard born at SGI — is still in use everywhere today.
  • SGI's alumni went on to seed companies like NVIDIA and 3dfx. When cheap PC graphics cards finally caught up in the late 1990s, SGI faded; it went bankrupt in 2009. This machine is from its peak.
⚠ Docent honesty note — a sharp visitor will catch this: the film's control‑room hero machine was an SGI Crimson, not an Indigo². If someone says "that's not the model from the movie," say: "Exactly right — the movie's hero unit was a Crimson. Ours is an Indigo², the desktop machine from the same family, same year, same IRIX, same fsn software. Same studio of computers, smaller box."

One‑paragraph summary (for memorizing)

Silicon Graphics made the only computers that could do real‑time 3‑D, so they ended up on both sides of Jurassic Park: on screen, the "It's a UNIX system!" scene shows a real SGI tool called fsn running on a real SGI workstation; behind the camera, ILM built the film's dinosaurs on ~70 SGI machines (modeled in Alias, animated in Softimage, rendered in RenderMan). Most of the on‑screen dinosaurs are actually Stan Winston animatronics — only a few minutes are CGI — but those few minutes crossed into photorealism and changed moviemaking forever. The machine that did it ran a RISC chip and dedicated graphics hardware: the ancestor of today's GPU.

3Demo Script

Runtime ~5–7 minAll agesStage directions in italics

Presenter needs: the Indigo² powered on and logged in to the IRIX desktop (see §5), the looping "It's a UNIX system!" clip playing on the side screen, and a 3‑D demo ready to launch from the Demos catalog.

⏱ 90‑Second Short Version (walk‑up)

[Point at the side screen looping the scene.] "You know this scene — 'It's a UNIX system, I know this!' Everybody remembers it, and everybody assumed that goofy 3‑D thing was fake Hollywood nonsense. It wasn't. That was real software, running on a real Silicon Graphics computer — this kind of computer, right here.

Here's the wild part. The dinosaurs in that same movie? Also built on machines like this one. Silicon Graphics was the only company making computers that could do 3‑D graphics in real time. So they ended up in the movie and they made the movie.

[Double‑click a 3‑D demo; rotate it with the middle mouse button.] "And while your 1993 PC was showing a blinking text cursor, this was spinning a 3‑D object in real time. That's why Hollywood, NASA, and the dinosaur guys all wanted one.

The computer in the movie helped make the movie. That's the whole story."

1. The Hook (30 sec)

[Stand by the looping clip.] "Almost everyone has seen this — the kid sits down, says 'It's a UNIX system, I know this!', and flies through a weird 3‑D world to lock the doors. For thirty years people laughed at it as fake computer baloney. I'm here to tell you it was real — real software, on a real Silicon Graphics machine, exactly like the one in front of you."

2. The reveal — it was real (60 sec)

"That 3‑D file browser has a name: fsn, 'File System Navigator,' and people pronounce it 'fusion.' Silicon Graphics actually shipped it as a demo on their UNIX system. Every pedestal is a folder; every little box is a file. [Gesture to the machine.] The only Hollywood part was using it to lock doors — really it was just a fancy way to look at your files. But the computer was real, the software was real, and SGI was the only company whose machines could draw something like that in 1993."

3. The twist — it made the movie too (75 sec)

"Now here's what surprises people. [Beat.] The dinosaurs in Jurassic Park were built on machines just like this one. A studio called Industrial Light & Magic used about seventy Silicon Graphics computers to model and animate the dinosaurs. [Optional:] and here's a fact that floors people — most of the dinosaurs in the movie aren't computer graphics at all. Most are full‑size robots built by Stan Winston. Only a few minutes of the whole film are CGI — but those few minutes were the first time a computer creature looked completely real, and it changed movies forever."

4. Why this machine could do it (60 sec)

[Tap the case.] "Why SGI and nobody else? Because this machine had a special chip just for graphics — the great‑grandparent of the graphics card in your phone and your PlayStation. They invented a graphics language called OpenGL that's still used today, and their engineers later started NVIDIA. For about ten years, if you needed real‑time 3‑D — Hollywood, NASA, fighter‑jet simulators — you bought one of these."

5. The Payoff — make it live (90 sec)

"Let me show you what '1993 magic' looked like." [Open the Demos catalog; double‑click a 3‑D demo such as 'Insect' or the music visualizer.] "This is running right now, in real time. [Hold the MIDDLE mouse button and drag to rotate.] I can grab it and spin it around — smooth, instant. Remember what a normal computer looked like in 1993: a black screen with a blinking cursor, maybe Windows 3 if you were lucky. This was from another planet. That gap — that's exactly why the dinosaurs, and that famous scene, both happened on a Silicon Graphics machine."

6. The Close (30 sec)

"So next time you see that scene, you'll know the secret: the computer the kid uses to survive the dinosaurs is the same kind of computer that drew the dinosaurs. The machine in the movie helped make the movie. That's Silicon Graphics — and for one incredible decade, they were the future."

Presenter notes & recovery lines

  • Machine won't boot / no desktop: don't hard power‑cycle a UNIX box in front of a crowd — fall back to the looping clip, which carries the whole story on its own. Note it in the repair sheet.
  • "Is this the actual computer from the movie?" — "Not this exact unit, and the movie's was a Crimson — but ours is the same family, same year, same operating system, same software. Same studio of computers, smaller box."
  • "Wasn't that scene fake?" — "The software was 100% real (it's called fsn); only using it to lock doors was made up."
  • "SGI? Never heard of them." — "For a decade they owned 3‑D graphics. They invented OpenGL, their people started NVIDIA, and cheap PC graphics cards eventually put them out of business in 2009."
  • Keep the 3‑D demo moving — motion is what makes the jaw drop. Pick a demo that spins or walks.
The one line to never skip: "The computer in the movie helped make the movie. That's the charm of this machine."

6Visitor FAQ

Q: Wait — that "It's a UNIX system" scene was real?
Yes. The 3‑D file browser is genuine SGI software called fsn ("fusion"), shipped as a demo on IRIX. People assumed it was fake only because nothing else looked like it — SGI was years ahead. The only made‑up part was using it to lock doors; it was really just a way to view your files.

Q: Is this the actual computer from the movie?
Not this exact unit, and not the exact model — the film's control‑room machine was an SGI Crimson. Ours is an Indigo², the desktop machine from the same family, same year, same operating system, running the same software. Same studio of computers, smaller box.

Q: What is SGI / Silicon Graphics? I've never heard of them.
For about a decade they owned 3‑D graphics — Hollywood, NASA, science, and engineering all ran on these. They invented OpenGL (still used today), and their engineers went on to start companies like NVIDIA. Cheap PC graphics cards caught up in the late 1990s, and SGI went bankrupt in 2009. This machine is from their peak.

Q: Were the dinosaurs made on this?
On machines just like it. ILM used about 70 SGI workstations to model and animate the CG dinosaurs. The final frames were rendered with Pixar's RenderMan software, but the dinosaurs were built and animated on SGI.

Q: How much of the movie is actually CGI?
Surprisingly little. Of roughly 15 minutes of on‑screen dinosaurs, most are Stan Winston's full‑size animatronics — only about 4–6 minutes are computer‑generated. But those few minutes were the first time a CG creature looked completely real.

Q: Why was this computer special?
It had dedicated graphics hardware — a chip just for drawing 3‑D — at a time when PCs didn't. That's the ancestor of the GPU in your phone and game console. It let SGI do real‑time 3‑D when everyone else showed a blinking text cursor.

Q: What's it doing when the docent spins that shape?
Running a 3‑D graphics demo in real time. In 1993 that was astonishing; today your phone does it, because your phone has SGI's idea (a graphics processor) inside it.

Q: Real work, or a toy?
Serious work. These ran flight simulators, scientific visualization, medical imaging, and movie effects. SGI was a real powerhouse for about ten years.

Q: Why did they lose?
Cheap, fast PC graphics cards — many made by companies SGI's own people founded, like NVIDIA — caught up and undercut them. SGI couldn't compete on price and went bankrupt in 2009.

Leave them with: "The computer the kid uses to survive the dinosaurs is the same kind of computer that drew the dinosaurs. The machine in the movie helped make the movie."
